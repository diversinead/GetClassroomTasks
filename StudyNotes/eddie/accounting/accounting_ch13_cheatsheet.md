# Ch 13 — Performance Indicators (Cheat Sheet)

## Key Concepts

**Performance indicator** = a calculated number (usually a ratio or percentage) that summarises one aspect of business performance, so it can be **compared** — against prior years, industry averages, or benchmarks. A raw figure on its own is meaningless; an indicator turns it into a judgement.

The three lenses indicators look through:

- **Profitability** — is the business making enough profit relative to sales / capital / assets? (NPM, ROI, ROA)
- **Liquidity** — can the business pay its **short-term** debts as they fall due? (Working Capital Ratio)
- **Stability** — can the business survive its **long-term** debt commitments? (Debt Ratio)

## The Three Questions Indicators Answer

| Question | Category | Key indicators |
|---|---|---|
| Is the business **profitable**? | Profitability | NPM, ROI, ROA |
| Can it **pay short-term bills**? | Liquidity | Working Capital Ratio |
| Can it **survive long-term debt**? | Stability | Debt Ratio |

> An indicator on its own means nothing — always compare to **prior year**, **industry average**, or a **benchmark** (like a term deposit rate for ROI).

## Profitability — The Three Ratios

| Indicator | Formula | Tells you |
|---|---|---|
| **Net Profit Margin (NPM)** | Net Profit ÷ Sales Revenue × 100 | Cents of profit kept from every $1 of sales |
| **Return on Owner's Investment (ROI)** | Net Profit ÷ Average Capital × 100 | % return the OWNER earns on their money |
| **Return on Assets (ROA)** | Net Profit ÷ Average Total Assets × 100 | How efficiently ALL resources (debt + equity) generate profit |

**Average capital** = (Opening + Closing) ÷ 2. **Using year-end capital alone = common student error.**

**Benchmark for ROI:** must comfortably beat a risk-free term deposit, otherwise the owner is better off leaving the cash in the bank.

## ROI vs ROA — The Gearing Story

- **ROI > ROA** → debt is working *in the owner's favour* (positive gearing — borrowed funds earn more than they cost in interest)
- **ROI < ROA** → debt is *dragging down* owner returns (interest cost is higher than the profit it generates)

A highly geared business can show high ROI but only modest ROA — leverage is amplifying the owner's return.

## Liquidity — Can They Pay Next Month's Bills?

**Working Capital Ratio** = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities  (written as X : 1)

| WCR | Reading |
|---|---|
| Above 2 : 1 | Comfortable |
| 1 : 1 to 2 : 1 | Tight — watch closely |
| Below 1 : 1 | **Warning** — current liabilities exceed current assets |

> Test BOTH the **level** AND the **trend**. A WCR that has fallen 2.0 → 1.75 → 1.64 over three years is a warning even if still above 1 : 1.

## Stability — Can They Survive Long-Term?

**Debt Ratio** = Total Liabilities ÷ Total Assets × 100

| DR | Reading |
|---|---|
| ≤ 50% | Low risk, may be under-leveraged |
| 50–65% | Moderate — manageable |
| > 65% | High risk, vulnerable in downturns, may struggle to borrow more |

**Advantage of higher debt:** allows faster growth than owner could fund alone; amplifies ROI in good years.

**Disadvantage:** interest must be paid in bad years; magnifies losses; lenders may refuse further credit.

## Non-Financial Indicators

Financial indicators look **BACKWARD**. Non-financial often look **FORWARD** — they warn of problems before they hit the income statement.

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Customer** | Satisfaction scores, repeat-purchase rate, review ratings, complaints |
| **Staff** | Turnover %, engagement, training hours |
| **Operations** | Delivery time, error/rework rate, first-time-fix rate |
| **Environmental/Social** | Recycling %, ethical suppliers, community impact |

> Exam favourite: *"Name TWO non-financial indicators and explain why each matters."* — pick from **different categories** for breadth.

## The Classic Exam Trap

> *"Net profit rose this year but NPM fell. Explain."*

The business is making more total dollars but keeping **less of each $1 of sales**. Expenses are growing faster than revenue — efficiency is declining even though absolute profit is up. Always check **direction** (trend) AS WELL AS the absolute number.

## What's NOT on the Income Statement

Common trap question: list items that are NOT revenue/expenses.
- **Capital contributions, Drawings** → Owner's Equity (balance sheet)
- **Loan principal, Loan repayments** → balance sheet / cash flow
- **GST collected & paid** → liability, not revenue/expense
- **Asset purchases** (laptop, equipment) → balance sheet

## Why This Matters

Every indicator is a **comparison tool**. A confident answer always states the number, names a benchmark (prior year / industry / term deposit), and explains the trend — not just the result.
