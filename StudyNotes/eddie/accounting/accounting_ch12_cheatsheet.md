# Ch 12 — Pricing, Cost Behaviour & Break-even (Cheat Sheet)

## Key Concepts

**Break-even point** = the level of sales where **total revenue exactly equals total costs** — no profit, no loss. The whole chapter is about finding this tipping point and understanding what happens either side of it.

**Contribution margin per unit** = selling price per unit − variable cost per unit. It's the dollars each unit sold contributes toward **covering fixed costs first, then profit**. It is the *engine* of break-even — divide fixed costs by contribution margin and you get the break-even units.

**Margin of safety** = the gap between actual (or budgeted) sales and the break-even level — how far sales could fall before the business starts losing money.

## Setting a Selling Price — Two Approaches

| | Cost-based | Market-based |
|---|---|---|
| **Logic** | Take cost, add mark-up | Look at what customers / competitors pay |
| **Risk** | May price above what market accepts | May price below cost |
| **Example** | Cost $40 + 90% mark-up = $76 | Match competitor's $5.50 flat white |

**Mark-up trap:** mark-up % is calculated on the **cost** price, not the selling price.

> A good pricing answer mentions BOTH cost factors (cost, contribution, profit goal) AND market factors (competitors, customers, conditions, RRP). One-sided = lost marks.

## Cost Behaviour

| Cost type | Total cost as volume rises | Per unit as volume rises |
|---|---|---|
| **Fixed** (rent, salaries, insurance) | Stays the same | **Falls** |
| **Variable** (materials, cost of goods) | Rises in proportion | Stays the same |
| **Semi-variable** (electricity, phone) | Base fee + per-unit charge | Falls (because of fixed base) |

> For break-even, split semi-variable costs into their fixed and variable parts first.

## Key Formulas

- **Contribution margin per unit** = Selling price − Variable cost per unit
- **Break-even (units)** = Fixed costs ÷ Contribution per unit
- **Break-even (sales $)** = Break-even units × Selling price
- **Units for target profit** = (Fixed costs + Target profit) ÷ Contribution per unit
- **Margin of safety** = Actual sales − Break-even sales

## Worked Example — Reliable Golf Buggies

| Item | $ |
|---|---|
| Selling price per buggy | $120 |
| Variable cost per buggy | $80 |
| **Contribution per buggy** | **$40** |
| Fixed costs (rent + salaries + cleaning) | $4,400 |
| **Break-even units** = $4,400 ÷ $40 | **110 buggies** |
| **Break-even sales** = 110 × $120 | **$13,200** |

**Selling only 80 buggies?** Shortfall = 30 buggies × $40 = **$1,200 loss** — matches the income statement exactly.

**Want $2,000 profit?** ($4,400 + $2,000) ÷ $40 = **160 buggies**.

## Break-even Graph — What Each Line Means

- **TR line** — starts at origin, slope = selling price
- **TC line** — starts at fixed cost on Y-axis, slope = variable cost per unit
- **FC line** — horizontal at fixed cost amount
- **Break-even** = where **TR crosses TC** (NOT where TR crosses FC — common error)
- **Below break-even** = loss zone (TC above TR)
- **Above break-even** = profit zone (TR above TC)

> Always label both axes — X = units sold, Y = dollars. Unlabelled = lost presentation marks.

## Definition to Memorise (word-for-word)

> Break-even point = *the level of sales at which total revenue equals total costs, and the business makes neither a profit nor a loss.*

## The Classic Exam Trap

> *"The business sold more units this year but still made a loss. Explain."*

Possible reasons:
- Fixed costs rose faster than contribution
- Variable cost per unit rose (squeezed contribution)
- Selling price dropped (smaller contribution per unit)
- Still below break-even volume

A small **margin of safety** = high risk — even a small sales drop wipes out profit.

## Selling Below Variable Cost

If selling price < variable cost per unit, **every sale increases the loss**. Only ever justified as a short-term clearance, never as ongoing strategy.

## Why This Matters

Master one chain — IS → split fixed/variable → derive per-unit figures → contribution → break-even → margin of safety — and you can answer most pricing questions in this chapter.
