🛡 The Five Pillars of Internal Control ▼
PillarWhat it meansExample
Separation of duties No single person should handle a transaction from start to finish — the staff who handle cash should not also do the books. One employee operates the till; a different employee counts cash takings; a third employee banks them.
Authorisation Significant transactions need approval from someone above the person initiating them. Refunds over $100 require the manager's signature; staff cannot self-approve them.
Physical security Restrict access to cash, stock and records using locks, safes, alarms, CCTV and secure storage. Locked till keys, end-of-day cash secured in a safe, security tags on high-value stock, CCTV at entrances.
Documentation & records Every transaction generates a receipt or invoice; records are kept securely and reviewed regularly. Numbered receipt books; matching cash takings to till tape totals; backing up accounting software nightly.
Reconciliation & review Cross-check business records against external evidence regularly — discrepancies show up early. Monthly bank reconciliations, stocktakes against inventory records, daily till reconciliation against till tape.

💡 A well-written internal control answer names at least 2–3 pillars and ties each to the specific business in the question. "Separate the till staff from the bookkeeping staff" scores better than just "separation of duties".

💵 Cash — common risks and matching controls ▼

Scenario context: Happy Valley Amusement Park — Louder runs ride attendants who collect cash from customers. Owner can't see every transaction.

RiskControl procedure
Staff pocket cash without ringing up the salePre-numbered tickets issued from a roll — reconcile ticket numbers used to cash banked each day.
Staff give friends free ridesEvery customer must surrender a ticket before riding; ride attendant cannot enter without one.
Cash skimmed from the till between salesTill tape captures every transaction; reconcile cash drawer to till tape totals at end of each shift.
Staff borrow from the till and replace later (or don't)No cash withdrawals from the till permitted — IOU notes banned; written advance procedure required instead.
Cash held overnight on premisesBank takings daily; use night safe drops for late closes; never leave cash on site overnight.
Single staff member handles takings end-to-endSeparation of duties — one person collects, a second counts, a third banks. No employee touches all three steps.
Owner has no visibility of daily takingsOwner reviews till reconciliation reports daily; bank reconciliation prepared monthly to cross-check the books to the bank statement.

💡 The textbook's Stacks of Wax exam-style question (staff borrowing $20 from the till and leaving IOUs) is testing this exact category — the answer must explain why IOU practice undermines the till reconciliation and creates opportunity for unrecorded losses.

📋 Bank Reconciliation — the 3-step check ▼

Scenario: A small business's books show a Bank balance of $6,280. The bank statement at the same date shows $5,950. Why the gap, and is the difference legitimate?

Bank Reconciliation as at 30 June 2028
Bank balance per bank statement$5,950
Add: Deposits in transit (banked after cutoff)$1,200
Less: Unpresented cheques (issued but not yet cashed)($870)
Adjusted bank balance$6,280
— matches the books' closing Bank balance — reconciled.

The 3 steps:

1. Start with the bank statement balance (the bank's independent record).
2. Add deposits in transit (cash banked but not yet cleared) and subtract unpresented cheques (issued but not yet cashed).
3. Compare to your books — the adjusted bank balance must equal your closing Bank balance. If it doesn't, investigate: error, missed transaction, or possible fraud.

💡 Bank reconciliation is the single most important monthly control over cash — it provides INDEPENDENT external evidence that your records match reality. A business that does not reconcile is essentially trusting its own bookkeeping with no cross-check.

⚖ Ethics & privacy in internal control — how far is too far? ▼

Scenario: A local supermarket plans to install multiple cameras AND post photos of suspected shoplifters at the entrance as a deterrent — a "wall of shame".

MeasureGenerally acceptable?Why / concerns
Security cameras yesAcceptable Cameras are common, well-understood, and customers expect them in retail. Footage helps reconcile suspected losses and supports police action when needed.
Bag checks at exit conditionalAcceptable if voluntary, signed and respectful Cannot be forced — only police can compel a search. Notices at entry advising of bag checks must be visible; staff must be trained on how to ask politely.
EFTPOS/contactless-only payment yesAcceptable Removes cash-related risks (theft, robbery, errors) entirely. Some customers may object on privacy or accessibility grounds — consider edge cases (cash-only customers).
Posting photos of suspected shoplifters noNot acceptable Likely breaches privacy law — suspects have not been convicted. Risk of defamation. Damages reputation if a misidentification occurs. The supermarket has no legal authority to publicly shame customers.
Naming & banning known thieves conditionalOnly after conviction or with strong evidence and legal advice Trespass notices on convicted shoplifters are legitimate; public "wanted"-style posters of unconvicted suspects are not.

💡 Ethics questions reward answers that recognise the tension: the business owner has a legitimate interest in preventing theft, BUT customers have privacy rights and the right to be presumed innocent. Acceptable controls protect assets WITHOUT publicly punishing the unconvicted.
