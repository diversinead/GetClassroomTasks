🧮 Key Formulas — pricing & break-even ▼

Selling price from cost + mark-up

Selling price = Cost × (1 + mark-up%)

Apply a percentage mark-up to the cost price to set the selling price.

Jeans cost $40, 90% mark-up
= 40 × 1.90 = $76 selling price

Actual mark-up %

((Selling − Cost) ÷ Cost) × 100

Work backwards from a known selling price to the mark-up percentage actually applied.

Cookbook cost $18, sells for $29
= (11 ÷ 18) × 100 = 61.1%

Contribution margin per unit

Selling price − Variable cost per unit

The dollars from each unit sold that go toward paying fixed costs and then profit.

Golf buggy sells $120, var cost $80
= $120 − $80 = $40 per buggy

Break-even point (units)

Fixed costs ÷ Contribution margin per unit

How many units must be sold for revenue to exactly cover all costs — no profit, no loss.

FC $4,400, contribution $40
= 4,400 ÷ 40 = 110 buggies

Break-even point (sales $)

Break-even units × Selling price

The total revenue figure that exactly covers all costs.

110 buggies at $120 each
= 110 × $120 = $13,200

Units for a target profit

(Fixed costs + Target profit) ÷ Contribution per unit

Add the desired profit on top of fixed costs — treat profit like another fixed cost to clear.

FC $4,400, target $2,000, margin $40
= 6,400 ÷ 40 = 160 buggies

Selling price for a target profit

((FC + Target profit) ÷ Units) + VC per unit

Used when units are capped (capacity limit) and price is the lever instead of volume.

FC $4,400, target $2,000, 100 units, VC $80
= (6,400 ÷ 100) + 80 = $144

Margin of safety

Actual sales − Break-even sales

How far sales could fall before the business slips into loss — the size of the safety buffer.

Sales 160 units, BE 110 units
= 50 units (or $6,000 in sales $)

💡 Always work in per-unit figures (selling price, variable cost, contribution) before applying the break-even formulas — mixing totals and per-unit numbers is the most common arithmetic mistake.

📝 Mark-up worked example — clothing store (textbook Ex 1) ▼

Scenario: A clothing store sells three product lines. Calculate the selling price under each mark-up rate.

Item Cost price 50% mark-up 90% mark-up 150% mark-up
Jeans$40 $60$76$100
T-shirts$10 $15$19$25
Baseball caps$5 $7.50$9.50$12.50

💡 A consistent percentage mark-up gives different dollar mark-ups on different items — that is why a $5 cap and a $40 pair of jeans don't share the same dollar profit even at the same %.

💡 The break-even point is ALWAYS where the Total Revenue line crosses the Total Cost line — not where TR crosses FC. A common exam mistake is to label the FC×TR intersection as break-even.

📋 From Income Statement to Break-even — Reliable Golf Buggies ▼

Step 1: Read the income statement and identify each line as variable (cost of buggies) or fixed (other expenses).

Reliable Golf Buggies — Income Statement for month ended 31 March 2028
Sales of buggies (80 × $120)$9,600
Less Cost of buggies (variable: 80 × $80)$6,400
Gross profit$3,200
Less Other expenses (all fixed)
Salaries$1,600
Cleaning$400
Rent$2,400
Total fixed costs$4,400
Net loss($1,200)

Step 2: Pull out per-unit figures and apply the formulas.

Selling price per buggy = $9,600 ÷ 80 = $120
Variable cost per buggy = $6,400 ÷ 80 = $80
Contribution per buggy = $120 − $80 = $40
Fixed costs = $1,600 + $400 + $2,400 = $4,400
Break-even units = $4,400 ÷ $40 = 110 buggies
Break-even sales = 110 × $120 = $13,200

Step 3: Reconcile the loss against the shortfall in units.

Sold 80 buggies, needed 110 to break even — shortfall = 30 buggies
30 buggies × $40 contribution = $1,200 — matches the net loss exactly.

💡 Whenever the question gives you an income statement plus a unit price, you can always derive per-unit variable cost — and from there everything else. Don't try to break-even from totals alone.

🧠 Factors to consider when setting a selling price ▼
FactorWhy it matters
Cost of the goods/serviceThe price must cover at least the variable cost per unit or every sale loses money.
Fixed costs & volume expectedThe contribution per unit must be enough to cover fixed costs across realistic sales volumes.
Competitor pricesPricing well above competitors loses customers; pricing too far below sacrifices margin and signals low quality.
Recommended retail priceThe supplier's suggested price acts as a sensible benchmark, especially in retail.
Target customerPremium customers will pay more for service or branding; price-sensitive customers shop on price alone.
Market conditionsSeasons, trends, scarcity and competitor moves all shift what the market will accept.
Profit goalThe owner's required profit drives the minimum acceptable contribution margin.
EthicsInflating "was" prices to fake a discount, or charging extreme mark-ups on essentials, damages trust and may breach consumer law.

💡 A pricing answer that mentions BOTH cost-based factors (cost, contribution, profit goal) AND market-based factors (competitors, customer, conditions) will score better than one that names only one side.
