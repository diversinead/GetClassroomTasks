# Q5 — Maximum Mowing (Max Haas)

*Yr 11 Accounting — Chapter 8*

This question introduces the **accrual concept**: expenses are recorded when **incurred**, not when paid. It's the most important conceptual idea in the chapter and the exam will absolutely test it.

---

## The Setup

- Business: Maximum Mowing (trading as a sole trader)
- Opening bank balance (1 Feb 2028): **$540**
- Opening GST liability (owed to ATO): **$840**
- Period: February 2028

---

For each transaction, decide:

2. **What category?** (revenue, expense, liability, equity, asset, GST)
3. **Was anything incurred but not paid?** ← *this is the new twist*

### The two "incurred but not yet paid" items

These are the heart of the question:

**Feb 7 — Fuel:** $320 paid in cash, $60 still owing
- Cash outflow during February: **$320**
- Fuel **expense** for February: **$380** (the total fuel actually consumed)
- The extra $60 sits as a **current liability** ("Accrued Expenses" or "Sundry Creditors") on the balance sheet

**Feb 29 — Telephone:** $90 bill received, not yet paid
- Cash outflow during February: **$0**
- Telephone **expense** for February: **$90** (because the service has been used)
- The $90 sits as a **current liability** on the balance sheet

This is the **accrual basis** in action: match the expense to the period that benefited from it, regardless of when cash changed hands.

## (c) Relevance and the Feb 7 Fuel Item

The question asks Eddie to explain his treatment of the Feb 7 transaction (the fuel where $320 was paid and $60 is still owing) **with reference to the qualitative characteristic of relevance**.

### What is "relevance"?

Relevance is one of the qualitative characteristics in the accounting framework. Information is **relevant** if it is **capable of influencing the decisions of users** of the financial reports.

For information to be relevant, it must reflect the **true economic activity** of the period — not just the cash movements.

### Model answer

> Under the qualitative characteristic of **relevance**, information must be capable of influencing the decision-making of users. To assess whether Maximum Mowing is profitable, users need to know the **total cost of fuel consumed during the period**, not just the portion that has been paid in cash.
>
> During February, the business consumed **$380 worth of fuel**, even though only $320 has been paid for. The remaining $60 is owed to the supplier and is a real obligation of the business.
>
> Reporting only the $320 paid would **understate the true cost of operations** and **overstate net profit** for February. This would not be relevant information for decision-making, as it would mislead the owner about the actual performance of the business.
>
> Therefore, the full $380 is recorded as a **fuel expense** on the Income Statement, and the $60 still owing is reported as a **current liability** (Accrued Expenses) on the Balance Sheet. This applies the **accrual basis** of accounting — expenses are recognised when incurred, regardless of when cash is paid.

### What makes this answer strong

- Names the concept explicitly (**relevance**)
- Defines what relevance means
- Explains what would happen *if you didn't apply it*
- Links to the broader accrual concept
- Specifies the exact accounting treatment (expense on Income Statement, liability on Balance Sheet)

## Why This Question Matters

Q5 is the **conceptual heart** of the chapter. If Eddie understands:

1. **Why the Statement of Receipts and Payments and Income Statement give different totals** — one is cash, one is accrual
2. **What "accrued expenses" are** — incurred but not yet paid
3. **How to write about qualitative characteristics** in an exam context

…then he's mastered the most-tested concepts in Ch 8.

The connection to the wider course:

- This is the same concept as **depreciation** (expense recognised but no cash moves)
- This is the same concept as **prepaid rent** in Q7 (cash paid in one period, expense spread over several)
- This is the concept underlying the **"profit vs cash" exam question** (City Drycleaning)

All four ideas come back to the same principle: **profit and cash are not the same thing because expenses (and revenue) are matched to periods, not cash movements.**

---

## Three-Sentence Summary

1. The Statement of Receipts and Payments only shows actual cash movements; the Income Statement shows what was earned and consumed.
2. Expenses must be **matched to the period that benefited from them** — even if cash hasn't moved yet.
3. The "still owing" amounts ($60 fuel, $90 telephone) go on the Income Statement as expenses and on the Balance Sheet as current liabilities.
