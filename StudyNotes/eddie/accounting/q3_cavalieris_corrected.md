## (e) GST Debt or Refund at 30 April 2028

| | $ |
|---|---:|
| Opening GST debt (1 April) — *owed to ATO* | 560 |
| Add GST collected during April | 209 |
| Less GST paid during April | (245) |
| **GST debt at 30 April (owed to ATO)** | **524** |

**How to report on the balance sheet:**

Since GST paid ($245) exceeded GST collected ($209) during April, the business reduced its GST liability by $36 during the month. But the opening debt of $560 means there's still an amount owed to the ATO.

The **$524 is reported as a current liability** on the balance sheet, typically called **"GST Clearing"** or **"GST Payable"**.

If the GST paid during a period had exceeded the GST collected *and* there was no opening debt, the business would instead have a **GST refund receivable** — reported as a current asset.

## Summary — Why This Matters

The principles tested in this question:

1. **Separating cash flows from revenue/expenses** — capital, drawings, GST, asset purchases all sit outside the Income Statement
2. **Categorising revenue by service type** — many businesses want this detail; you can also group it all as one "Fees" line
3. **The owner's equity formula** — Opening Capital + Contributions + Profit (or − Loss) − Drawings = Closing Capital
4. **GST clearing** — opening balance + GST collected − GST paid = closing balance, reported as a liability (or asset) on the balance sheet

The **net loss of $1,990** is a more honest reflection of business performance than the cash position. Despite the bank balance growing from $430 to $5,704, the underlying operations are running at a loss — the cash growth came almost entirely from the owner's $7,500 capital contribution.

This is the **classic profit-vs-cash story** in action.
