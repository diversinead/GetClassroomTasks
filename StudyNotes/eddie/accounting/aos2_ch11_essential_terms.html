📊 Operating vs Investing vs Financing — what hits each section ▼
FeatureOperatingInvestingFinancing
What it covers Day-to-day trading activity Buying or selling non-current assets Changes in the financial structure of the business
Typical inflows inCash sales, customer receipts, GST collected, interest revenue inSale of equipment, vehicles, premises inLoan received, owner capital contribution
Typical outflows outWages, supplier payments, rent, GST paid, interest expense outPurchase of equipment, vehicles, premises outLoan repayments, drawings
Healthy sign positiveNet cash from operations should be positive — the business funds itself often negativeA growing business invests in new assets dependsEither — raising capital is fine; constant borrowing to survive is a red flag
Sustainability Core measure of business health Reveals whether the business is growing or shrinking its asset base Reveals owner commitment and debt reliance

💡 Operating activities are the engine room — a healthy business should fund its investing and financing from operating cash, not the other way around.

📋 Statement of Receipts & Payments vs Cash Flow Statement ▼
FeatureStatement of Receipts & PaymentsCash Flow Statement
What it lists All cash receipts then all cash payments — one big list The same cash flows, but grouped under Operating, Investing, Financing
Classification noneSingle block of receipts, single block of payments threeThree clear sections matching the activity type
Analytical value Shows total cash in/out — that's it Reveals where the cash came from and where it went — supports decision-making
Use Simpler small businesses, low-volume operators Required for evaluating performance, applying for finance, comparing across years
Bottom line shown Excess of receipts over payments Net increase/decrease in cash held + reconciles to closing bank balance

💡 Both reports contain the same dollar figures — the Cash Flow Statement just classifies them so the reader can answer questions like "can the business fund its own growth?"

🔍 Analysis cues — what to write when commenting on a CFS ▼
What you seeWhat to say
positiveNet cash from Operating is positive The business is generating enough cash from its core trading to fund itself — a sign of sustainability.
negativeNet cash from Operating is negative The business is not generating enough cash from trading to cover its operating costs — it must rely on financing or asset sales, which is unsustainable long-term.
negativeNet cash used in Investing The business is investing in non-current assets — usually a sign of growth, expansion or asset replacement.
positiveNet cash from Investing is positive The business is selling off non-current assets — could be downsizing, replacing old assets, or struggling to fund operations.
positiveNet cash from Financing is positive The owner has contributed capital or the business has taken on new debt — check whether it funds investing (healthy) or operating (warning sign).
negativeNet cash used in Financing The business is paying down debt or the owner is taking drawings — both reduce the financial obligations or owner stake.
warningOperating is negative but Financing keeps it afloat The business is borrowing to pay day-to-day bills. Sustainable only as a short-term fix; ongoing reliance signals possible insolvency.
healthyOperating funds Investing; Financing is modest Ideal pattern — the business is self-funding its growth without over-relying on debt.

💡 A CFS analysis question almost always asks you to look at all three sections together — not just one. Compare across years where possible: "Operating cash fell from $66k to $60k while Financing inflows rose — the business is increasingly dependent on loans to maintain its cash position."
