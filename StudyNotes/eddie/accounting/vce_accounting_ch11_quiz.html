📋 Short Answer Questions
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
The following items appear in the cash journals of Sarah's Cafe for the year ended 30 June 2028.
1. Loan principal repayment$6,000
2. Sale of old espresso machine$2,000
3. Interest expense paid$1,200
Classify each of the three items by both ACTIVITY (Operating / Investing / Financing) and DIRECTION (Inflow / Outflow). (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

1. Loan principal repayment $6,000 (1 mark): Financing outflow — the principal portion of a loan repayment changes the financial structure of the business.

2. Sale of old espresso machine $2,000 (1 mark): Investing inflow — proceeds from selling a non-current asset.

3. Interest expense paid $1,200 (1 mark): Operating outflow — interest is a day-to-day cost of doing business, classified as Operating even though the underlying loan is Financing.

⚠ A common mistake is to classify interest as Financing alongside the loan principal. Only the principal portion is Financing — interest goes in Operating. This is one of the most frequent exam traps.
Short Answer★ HOT4 marks
Sarah's Cafe records the following cash movements during the year ended 30 June 2028 (extract):
Receipts from customers$92,000
GST collected$9,200
Payments to suppliers$48,000
Wages paid$22,500
GST paid$5,420
Interest expense paid$1,800
Drawings$15,000
Sale of old espresso machine$2,000
Calculate the net cash provided by operating activities for the year. Show your workings clearly. (4 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Step 1 — Operating inflows (1 mark):

• Receipts from customers $92,000 + GST collected $9,200 = $101,200

Step 2 — Operating outflows (2 marks):

• Payments to suppliers $48,000 + Wages $22,500 + GST paid $5,420 + Interest paid $1,800 = $77,720 (1 mark for recognising interest as Operating, 1 mark for total)

Step 3 — Net cash from operating (1 mark):

• $101,200 − $77,720 = $23,480 net cash provided by operating activities.

⚠ Drawings $15,000 are Financing, not Operating. Sale of espresso machine $2,000 is Investing, not Operating. Including either in the operating total is an automatic 1-mark deduction.
Short Answer4 marks
A small business reports $50,000 net profit on its Income Statement, but its Cash Flow Statement shows only $15,000 net cash from operating activities for the same year.
Explain THREE possible reasons why profit and net cash from operating activities can differ. (4 marks — 1 mark per reason + 1 mark for clarity)
✅ Model Answer

Any three of the following, each with a specific explanation (1 mark each):

• Credit sales: revenue is recognised when the sale occurs, but cash only arrives when the customer pays. If accounts receivable grew during the year, profit will exceed cash from trading.

• Depreciation: reduces profit on the Income Statement but uses no cash in the current period — so reported profit is lower than the actual cash generated, OR vice versa depending on whether the question gives profit higher or lower.

• Credit purchases / accruals: expenses recognised on the IS but not yet paid in cash widen the gap, as do prepayments — cash paid out before being recognised as an expense reduces cash without reducing profit.

• Inventory build-up: cash spent buying stock that has not yet been sold reduces cash without reducing profit.

+ 1 mark for clear, organised explanation linking each reason back to the profit-cash gap.

💡 Tie each reason to a specific dollar mechanism ("AR grew by $35k" rather than just "credit sales") to score full marks. Abstract one-liners typically cap at 1 mark per reason regardless of how many you list.

Sarah Wong owns Sarah's Cafe. The following cash movements were recorded for the year ended 30 June 2028. Opening cash held at 1 July 2027 was $8,000.
Receipts from customers$92,000
GST collected$9,200

GST paid$5,420

Purchase of new coffee roaster$18,000
Capital contribution from owner$10,000
Loan principal repayments$6,000
Drawings$15,000
Prepare a classified Cash Flow Statement for Sarah's Cafe for the year ended 30 June 2028. Calculate the net change in cash held and the closing cash position. (8 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Sarah's Cafe — Cash Flow Statement for year ended 30 June 2028

Cash flows from operating activities (2 marks):

• Receipts from customers $92,000

• GST collected $9,200

• Payments to suppliers ($48,000)

• Wages ($22,500)

• GST paid ($5,420)

• Net cash provided by operating activities = $25,280

Cash flows from investing activities (2 marks):

• Sale of espresso machine $2,000

• Purchase of new coffee roaster ($18,000)

• Net cash used in investing activities = ($16,000)

Cash flows from financing activities (2 marks):

• Capital contribution from owner $10,000

• Loan principal repayments ($6,000)

• Drawings ($15,000)

• Net cash used in financing activities = ($11,000)

Reconciliation (2 marks):

• Net decrease in cash held = $25,280 − $16,000 − $11,000 = ($1,720) (1 mark)

• Cash held at start of year $8,000 − $1,720 = $6,280 closing cash held (1 mark)

💡 Mark each transaction with O / I / F on your working paper BEFORE writing the statement — this catches misclassifications (especially interest and loan principal) before they cost marks.
Extended Response★ HOT8 marks
Sarah's Cafe Cash Flow Statement for the year ended 30 June 2028 showed the following section totals.
Net cash provided by operating activities$25,280
Net cash used in investing activities($16,000)
Net cash used in financing activities($11,000)
Net decrease in cash held($1,720)
Cash held at start of year$8,000
Cash held at end of year$6,280
a. Comment on the sustainability of Sarah's business by referring to EACH of the three sections of the CFS. (4 marks)
b. The closing cash held has fallen from $8,000 to $6,280. Should Sarah be concerned? Explain. (2 marks)
c. Suggest two specific actions Sarah could take to improve her cash position. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Sustainability commentary (4 marks):

• Operating $25,280 positive: the cafe generates enough cash from trading to fund itself — a strong sign of underlying sustainability (1 mark).

• Investing ($16,000) used: Sarah has invested in a new commercial coffee roaster (partly offset by selling the old espresso machine). Net cash used in investing is usually a positive sign — the business is replacing/upgrading assets to support future growth (1 mark).

• Financing ($11,000) used: drawings ($15,000) plus loan repayments ($6,000) outweighed the $10,000 capital contribution. Sarah is taking cash out faster than she is putting it in (1 mark).

• Overall: trading is healthy, investing is growth-related, but financing outflows (driven by aggressive drawings) consumed the trading surplus and produced a small net decrease in cash. The business is sustainable for now, but the owner's drawings policy is the concern (1 mark).

(b) Concern about closing cash (2 marks):

• Mild concern only. Cash fell by 21.5% but the absolute drop ($1,720) is small, and operating cash remained strongly positive. The business has not exhausted its cash buffer in one year (1 mark).

• However, if drawings continue to exceed the trading surplus left over after investing, cash will keep falling. One year is not a crisis, but two or three years of this pattern would be (1 mark).

(c) Actions to improve cash (2 marks — any two):

• Reduce drawings — pay herself less or take drawings only out of the trading surplus after investing needs.

• Tighten accounts receivable collection — if any credit sales exist, shorten the terms or chase overdue debtors to bring cash in faster.

• Defer further asset purchases — delay the next major investment until cash position recovers.

• Negotiate longer supplier credit terms — hold onto cash for longer before paying suppliers.

⚠ A common error is to focus on Operating alone and ignore the other sections. The CFS analysis question almost always wants commentary on ALL THREE sections — only naming one will cap you at 1–2 marks for part (a).

Sarah's Cafe CFS section totals across two consecutive years are summarised below.
SectionYear ended 30/6/2027Year ended 30/6/2028
Net cash from operating$35,000$25,280
Net cash from investing($8,000)($16,000)

Net change in cash$31,000($1,720)
Closing cash held$8,000$6,280
a. Identify and quantify the THREE most significant changes between the two years' cash flow patterns. (3 marks)
b. Explain what these changes reveal about how the business funded itself across the two years. (2 marks)
c. Should Sarah be more or less worried at the end of 2028 than she was at the end of 2027? Justify your answer. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Three significant changes (3 marks — 1 each):

• Net cash from Operating fell by $9,720 ($35,000 → $25,280, a ~28% drop).

• Net cash used in Investing doubled from ($8,000) to ($16,000) — the cafe's asset spend increased significantly.

• Financing swung $15,000 from a $4,000 inflow to an $11,000 outflow — the owner shifted from contributing capital to taking large drawings + loan repayments.

(b) How the business funded itself (2 marks):

• In 2027, operating cash easily funded the modest investing spend, and the owner also added capital — producing a $31,000 net gain in cash. The business was self-sufficient and growing its cash buffer (1 mark).

• In 2028, operating cash fell while investing demands rose. Instead of receiving capital from the owner, the business paid out drawings + loan repayments. Operating cash was no longer sufficient to cover the combined investing and financing outflows — resulting in a small overall cash decline (1 mark).

(c) More or less worried (2 marks):

• More worried at the end of 2028 than 2027 (1 mark).

• All three sections moved in the wrong direction: operating cash fell, investing doubled, and financing flipped to a heavy outflow. If the 2028 pattern continues into 2029, the cash buffer could be exhausted — the trend is clearly negative even if the absolute cash position remains positive for now (1 mark).

💡 Trend analysis answers must include the DIRECTION and a QUANTIFIED change (dollars or percentage) for each indicator. Stating only that something has "decreased" without quantification typically caps at 0.5 marks per point.
