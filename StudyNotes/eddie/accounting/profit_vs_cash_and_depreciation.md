# Profit vs Cash — The Classic Exam Question

*Yr 11 Accounting — Worked answer + concept explanation*

---

## The Question

> *"The owner of City Drycleaning is a little confused. The balance of cash at bank has increased from $5,000 to $18,000 over the last 12 months. However, the business's accountant has just advised City Drycleaning has made a net loss of $25,000 for the period. Explain, using two examples in your answer, how the business's bank account can increase in a year when it has made a net loss."* **(4 marks)**

---

## How to Structure a Strong Answer

A 4-mark answer needs:

1. **A clear opening principle** explaining *why* profit ≠ cash (1 mark)
2. **Two specific examples** of cash flows that affect the bank but not profit (2 marks — 1 per example)
3. **A linking sentence** that ties it back to the question (1 mark)

---

## Model Answer

> The business's bank balance and net profit can move in different directions because they measure different things. **Net profit** is calculated on an accrual basis — it shows revenue earned minus expenses incurred over the period. The **bank balance**, however, reflects every cash flow in and out of the business, including transactions that are not classified as revenue or expenses.
>
> For example, **the owner may have contributed additional capital** during the year — say $30,000. This cash inflow increases the bank balance but is not revenue, so it does not appear on the Income Statement and has no effect on net profit.
>
> Similarly, the business may have **taken out a bank loan** of $20,000. The loan increases the bank balance because cash has been received, but it is recorded as a liability rather than revenue, so it does not improve the profit result.
>
> These cash inflows can increase the bank balance even when the underlying business operations are running at a loss, which explains how City Drycleaning's cash position has improved despite making a net loss of $25,000.

---

## Other Examples Eddie Could Use

Any **two** of these work:

### Cash inflows that are NOT revenue (increase cash, not profit)

| Example | Why it increases cash but not profit |
|---|---|
| **Loan received from the bank** | Cash in, but it's a liability — must be repaid |
| **Capital contribution from the owner** | Cash in, but it's equity — owner putting money in |
| **Sale of a non-current asset** (e.g. old van) | Cash in, but the value of the asset is removed from the balance sheet — not revenue |
| **Collection of debtors** from previous periods | Cash in this year, but the revenue was recognised in a *prior* year |
| **GST collected exceeds GST paid** | Cash in, but it's owed to the ATO — not revenue |

### Expenses that don't involve cash (reduce profit, not cash)

| Example | Why it reduces profit but not cash |
|---|---|
| **Depreciation expense** | Reduces profit but no cash leaves the business — the equipment was paid for in an earlier period |
| **Accrued expenses** (e.g. unpaid telephone bill) | Recorded as expense but cash hasn't gone out yet |
| **Bad debts written off** | Reduces profit but had no cash effect this year |

A really strong answer combines **one example of cash in that isn't revenue** with **one example of an expense that isn't cash out** — that demonstrates full mastery of the concept.

---

## Even Stronger Version

> The bank balance reflects all cash movements in the business, while net profit is based on the accrual concept — revenue earned and expenses incurred during the period, regardless of when cash changes hands. This means cash and profit can move in opposite directions.
>
> Firstly, City Drycleaning may have **received a loan of $30,000** during the year. The loan increases the bank balance immediately but is classified as a liability, not revenue, so it does not appear on the Income Statement.
>
> Secondly, City Drycleaning's net loss may include a large **depreciation expense** — for example, $15,000 on equipment. Depreciation reduces profit but involves no cash outflow, since the equipment was paid for in an earlier period.
>
> The combination of cash inflows that aren't revenue, and expenses that don't involve cash, explains how the bank balance increased by $13,000 even though the business made a $25,000 net loss.

---

## Key Points to Remember

- **State the underlying principle first** ("profit and cash measure different things") — examiners love seeing the concept named explicitly
- **Use specific dollar examples** even if you have to invent them — vague answers lose marks
- **Link back to the question** in the final sentence — don't leave the examiner to connect the dots
- For **4 marks**, give two clear examples. For **6+ marks**, combine "cash in that isn't revenue" with "expense that isn't cash out"

This question type comes up almost every year in some form. Nailing it = nailing the most important concept in the course.

# What is Depreciation Expense?

Since depreciation gets mentioned in the answer above and is a key concept in itself, here's a proper explanation.

## The Concept

**Depreciation** is the way accounting spreads the cost of a non-current asset over the years it's used, rather than expensing the whole thing in the year it was bought.

The idea: when a business buys a piece of equipment for $10,000 that lasts 5 years, the equipment helps generate revenue across *all five years*, not just the year of purchase. So the **matching principle** says we should match the cost to the periods that benefit from it.

## A Simple Example

A business buys a delivery van for **$50,000** on 1 January. The van is expected to last **5 years**, after which it will be worth nothing.

Without depreciation, you'd have two bad options:

- **Expense the full $50,000 in Year 1** — makes Year 1 look terrible and Years 2-5 look artificially good. Wrong picture.
- **Treat it as just an asset forever** — but the van *is* wearing out and losing value, so the balance sheet would lie too. Wrong picture.

**Solution: depreciation.**

$50,000 ÷ 5 years = **$10,000 depreciation expense per year**

Each year for five years:
- The Income Statement shows a $10,000 depreciation expense
- The balance sheet shows the van's value decreasing by $10,000

After 5 years, the van's "book value" is $0 — fully depreciated.

## Why Depreciation Is Weird (and Important)

Depreciation is the **only common expense that doesn't involve any cash leaving the business in the year it's recorded.**

The cash already left when the asset was purchased — sometimes years earlier. The depreciation expense is just an accounting allocation of that earlier cost.

This is exactly why it's the perfect example for the "profit fell but cash didn't" exam question — it reduces profit without touching the bank.

## Where Depreciation Appears

| Report | How it appears |
|---|---|
| **Income Statement** | As an expense — "Depreciation expense $10,000" |
| **Balance Sheet** | The asset's value is reduced — either directly, or via an "Accumulated Depreciation" account |
| **Cash Flow Statement** | **Doesn't appear** in the cash movements at all — because no cash moved |

## Connecting Back to What Eddie Already Knows

Remember Q7 in the Ch 8 exercises — the Jetski Hire question where Hawthorn paid $125,000 upfront for 5 years' rent? The correct answer was to recognise **$25,000 per year** as rent expense, not the full $125,000.

**Depreciation works the exact same way**, just for a physical asset instead of prepaid rent:

- Hawthorn paid $125,000 for 5 years of *rent benefit* → expense $25,000 per year
- The delivery van cost $50,000 for 5 years of *useful life* → expense $10,000 per year

Both apply the **matching principle**: match the cost to the periods that benefit from it.

## The Three-Sentence Summary

1. **Depreciation** spreads the cost of a non-current asset over its useful life.
2. It reduces profit each year but does **not** involve any cash movement.
3. This is why a business can have a low profit but healthy cash flow — depreciation is hitting the Income Statement without hitting the bank.
