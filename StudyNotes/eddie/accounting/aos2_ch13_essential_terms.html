🧮 Key Formulas — profitability, liquidity & stability indicators ▼

Net profit margin (NPM)

(Net profit ÷ Sales revenue) × 100

Cents of profit retained from every $1 of sales after all expenses are paid.

Warrnambool Trailer Hire: NP $27,500, Sales $125,000
= 27,500 ÷ 125,000 × 100 = 22%

Return on owner's investment (ROI)

(Net profit ÷ Average capital) × 100

Return earned for every $1 the owner has invested in the business — compare to a term deposit.

Bendigo 2027: NP $110k, avg capital $425k
= 110 ÷ 425 × 100 = 25.9%

Rate of return on assets (ROA)

(Net profit ÷ Average total assets) × 100

How efficiently the business converts its assets into profit, regardless of how those assets were funded.

Bernie's: NP $124k, avg assets $620k
= 124 ÷ 620 × 100 = 20%

Working capital ratio (WCR)

Current assets ÷ Current liabilities

Ability to pay short-term debts as they fall due. Result is expressed as X : 1.

Bill's Mowing: CA $24k, CL $16k
= 24 ÷ 16 = 1.5 : 1

Debt ratio (DR)

(Total liabilities ÷ Total assets) × 100

Proportion of the business's assets that have been financed by debt (rather than the owner).

City Car Hire: TL $330k, TA $550k
= 330 ÷ 550 × 100 = 60%

Percentage of sales (expense)

(Expense ÷ Sales revenue) × 100

How much of every sales dollar is consumed by a particular expense line — used in pie charts and IS percentage breakdowns.

Wages $61,250 of $125,000 sales
= 61,250 ÷ 125,000 × 100 = 49%

💡 Always use average capital and average total assets where opening and closing figures are given — using year-end alone is a common mark deduction.

📊 What does a "good" number look like? — indicator interpretation ▼
IndicatorHealthyWarningWhat to compare to
Net profit margin higherRising margin over time, beating the industry average fallingMargin shrinking year-on-year — expenses growing faster than revenue Prior years, industry average, budget

Debt ratio low50% or below — the owner has funded most of the business; manageable risk highAbove ~65% indicates rising financial risk — lenders may refuse further finance Industry average, prior years, comfort of the owner

💡 There is no single "correct" number for any indicator — what matters is the COMPARISON. Always compare a figure to a benchmark (prior year, industry average, or budget) before drawing a conclusion.

📈 Trend Analysis — Bendigo Tractor Repairs (2026–2028) ▼

Scenario: The owner is pleased that profit has risen every year. But trends in ROI and ROA tell the fuller story — are the higher profits actually delivering a better return on the growing asset base?

Item 31 Dec 2026 31 Dec 2027 31 Dec 2028
Net profit ($000)$90$110$120
Owner's capital ($000, year-end)$400$450$460
Total assets ($000, year-end)$620$700$800
ROI (NP ÷ avg capital)22.5%25.9%26.4%
ROA (NP ÷ avg assets)14.8%16.7%16.0%

What to write in a comment:

Profit IS rising every year — the owner is correct to be pleased on that measure alone.
ROI has risen steadily from 22.5% to 26.4% — each dollar of capital is earning more, so the owner is being better rewarded for their investment.
ROA rose then fell from 16.7% to 16.0% in 2028 — the business added $100k of assets in 2028 but profit only rose $10k. New assets are not yet generating proportional returns.

💡 Rising profit on its own can be misleading. Always test rising profit against the asset base — if assets grew faster than profit, ROA falls and efficiency is dropping.

⚔ ROA comparison — Eddie's vs Bernie's Electricals ▼

Scenario: Two electrical contractors look very different in raw dollar terms — but the ratio tells a different story about which business is more efficient.

IndicatorEddie's ElectricalsBernie's Electricals
Net profit$135,000$124,000
Average total assets$750,000$620,000
Rate of return on assets18.0%20.0%

What this tells us:

Eddie's makes more profit ($135k vs $124k) but also has more assets working.
Bernie's squeezes 20c of profit from every $1 of assets vs Eddie's 18c — Bernie's is the more efficient operator.
Bernie's could be more efficient because of leaner asset use, better pricing, or tighter cost control — the ratio flags WHERE to look, not WHY.

💡 A ratio doesn't explain itself — it tells you which business is more efficient, but the "why" needs investigation (asset utilisation, pricing, costs). Examiners reward answers that name the gap AND suggest possible reasons.

🧠 Non-financial indicators — what to look at beyond the numbers ▼
CategoryIndicatorWhy it matters
CustomerCustomer satisfaction surveys, net promoter score, repeat customer %, online reviewsFuture revenue depends on customers coming back — rising complaints predict falling sales.
CustomerNumber of new customers, customer retention rateGrowth in a service business comes from acquiring AND retaining customers, not just billing more per visit.
EmployeesStaff turnover, sick leave, training hours, engagement scoresHigh turnover signals poor culture and adds recruitment/training cost; high training hours often precede service-quality improvements.
OperationsTime taken to deliver service, error/rework rates, first-time-fix rateEfficient delivery directly affects margin — reworking a job consumes labour twice without earning twice.
Environmental / socialWaste recycled, energy use per service, supplier ethicsIncreasingly important to customers, suppliers and regulators; can also reduce costs (e.g. lower energy bills).
Market positionMarket share, brand awareness, competitor pricing comparisonHelps the owner understand whether financial trends are due to internal performance or wider market shifts.

💡 A typical 4-mark exam question asks for two non-financial indicators — pick one customer-related AND one operations/employee-related to demonstrate breadth, and always explain WHY each matters for that specific business.
