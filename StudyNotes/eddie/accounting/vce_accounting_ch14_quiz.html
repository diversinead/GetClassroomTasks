📋 Short Answer Questions
Short Answer★ HOT4 marks
Karen Withers is the owner of Stacks of Wax, a small business that sells candle products and oil burners. She has three casual employees working in the shop. Withers likes to have good working relationships with her staff. She even lets them borrow up to $20 from the till to pay for lunch or takeaway coffees, if they leave an IOU note in the cash register.
State and explain TWO reasons why Withers should NOT be allowing her staff to complete their own IOUs and then take cash from the cash register. (4 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Reason 1 — The till reconciliation fails (2 marks): At the end of the shift, the cash drawer plus any IOUs should equal the till tape sales total plus the float. But IOUs are self-written and unverified — a staff member can write an IOU, take the cash, then quietly destroy the note. A genuine theft is now indistinguishable from an unpaid IOU. The reconciliation control no longer detects loss.

Reason 2 — No audit trail for repayment (2 marks): If an IOU is repaid the cash reappears and the note is torn up — leaving no record of either the loan or the repayment. If it's NOT repaid, the loss is simply hidden by ignoring the unpaid IOU. Either way, the business cannot track who owes what, and small losses can accumulate without detection.

(Other acceptable reasons: it normalises taking cash from the till for personal use; it sets a precedent that staff may abuse; it makes it hard to distinguish loans from theft if questioned by an auditor or owner.)

⚠ Just stating the reason without explanation caps at 1 mark each (so 2/4 max). The question is "state AND explain" — both halves are required for full marks.
Short Answer★ HOT4 marks
Charlie Ross is a tomato grower from Yarrawonga. He sells through six workers at several local markets. To keep things simple, his workers operate on a cash-only basis with no receipts issued and no cash register at the market sites. Last summer, the workers returned 20 kg of tomatoes — well under what was sent. Ross is concerned about the gap between tomatoes sent to market and cash takings.
a. Identify TWO internal control problems with the way Ross runs the market operation. (2 marks)
b. Suggest TWO specific procedures Ross should introduce. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Problems (2 marks):

• No documentation linking cash to sales — without receipts, there is no record of what was sold for what price. A worker can sell tomatoes and keep the cash without any trace (1 mark).

• No stocktake reconciliation — with no count at start of day or end, the business cannot tell whether the 20 kg difference is from theft, damage, or unsold returned tomatoes. Losses can accumulate before they are detected (1 mark).

(b) Procedures (2 marks):

• Issue pre-numbered receipts for every sale — receipts torn from a numbered book provide a sales total that can be reconciled to cash banked. Missing numbers signal cash taken without sale (1 mark).

• Stocktake at start and end of each market day — count tomatoes out, count tomatoes returned, compare to receipts. Discrepancies investigated immediately (1 mark).

(Other acceptable procedures: provide a cash register at each market site; rotate which worker bags the cash; have two workers count and sign off takings; require receipts to be matched against banked cash by Ross himself.)

💡 Tie each procedure to the specific problem it solves. A procedure named in isolation ("use a stocktake") scores worse than one that references the gap it closes ("use a stocktake at start AND end of market day to identify whether the 20 kg shortfall is theft or unsold stock").
Short Answer3 marks
Kamala Khan runs a hair salon with four hairdressers. She has decided to stop accepting cash entirely — from now on, all customers must use contactless card payment.
Discuss ONE advantage and ONE disadvantage of moving to a contactless-only system from an internal control perspective. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Advantage (1.5 marks): The most significant internal control risks — staff pocketing cash, miscounted floats, IOUs from the till, robbery on the way to the bank — all disappear because no cash physically changes hands. Every transaction is automatically captured by the POS system, giving Kamala a complete audit trail without daily reconciliation effort.

Disadvantage (1.5 marks): The salon now depends entirely on the card terminal and internet connection. If either fails the business cannot transact — whereas a cash backup would keep trading. Some customers (older clients, customers without cards) may also be lost. New control risks emerge around card data security and the potential for chargebacks.

⚠ A common error is to give an advantage but no disadvantage (or vice versa). The question asks for both — missing one halves the marks.
📚 Extended Response Questions
Extended Response★ HOT8 marks
Nick Louder owns the Happy Valley Amusement Park, which operates six different rides at $6 per ride. One employee looks after each ride. Customers pay the cash to the ride attendant when they first get on, which eliminates the cost of tickets. Louder wants to know how much each ride earns. He has instructed the ride attendants to count the cash received at the end of the day, write the total in an exercise book in the office, and then put the money in the top drawer of his desk. Louder is satisfied with this system because it has helped him identify and replace the least popular rides.
a. List FOUR internal control problems with the way Louder is running his business. (4 marks)
b. Write a step-by-step plan for how Louder should operate the cash side of his business, from selling tickets through to banking the money. (4 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Problems (4 marks — 1 each):

• No pre-numbered tickets — there is no way to verify how many rides each attendant actually gave compared to how much cash was taken. An attendant can give a free ride to a friend, or pocket cash without any independent record.

• No separation of duties — each ride attendant collects cash, counts cash, and records cash. A single employee handles end-to-end, which is the textbook failure mode for cash internal control.

• Self-reported takings in an exercise book — the attendants control the only record of revenue. They can under-report cash and pocket the difference and Louder has nothing to cross-check against.

• Cash held overnight in an unsecured desk drawer — no safe, no daily banking, no insurance protection. A burglary, fire, or after-hours theft destroys the day's takings with no recovery.

(b) Step-by-step plan (4 marks):

• Step 1 (1 mark): Set up a central ticket booth at the park entrance, staffed by a different employee from the ride attendants. Customers buy pre-numbered tickets at the booth, not from the attendant.

• Step 2 (1 mark): Customers surrender tickets to ride attendants who do not handle cash. Attendants drop torn tickets into a locked box at the ride.

• Step 3 (1 mark): At end of day, a supervisor reconciles tickets sold (booth records) against tickets torn (locked box) against cash collected. Discrepancies investigated immediately.

• Step 4 (1 mark): Two staff members count and sign off the day's takings; cash is banked using a night safe (or at the bank next morning) — never left in an office desk overnight.

💡 The cleanest extended-response answers here name the internal control pillar invoked (separation of duties, documentation, physical security, reconciliation) AND apply it to the specific business context. Examiners reward this structure consistently.
Extended Response6 marks
The owner of a local supermarket has noticed that there have been several customers attempting to shoplift from the store recently. He is now considering installing multiple security cameras. If he detects shoplifters on the footage, he plans to print images of the thieves and display them at the entrance of the shop as a "wall of shame".
a. Are security cameras an acceptable form of internal control in a retail setting? Explain your answer. (3 marks)
b. A friend of the owner has stated that the "wall of shame" is a breach of privacy and unethical. Do you agree? Discuss. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Security cameras — acceptable? (3 marks):

• Yes, security cameras are an acceptable internal control in a retail setting (1 mark).

• They deter theft (visible cameras discourage shoplifting before it happens) and provide independent evidence if an incident occurs — supporting police reports and insurance claims (1 mark).

• This is acceptable provided customers are clearly informed of the recording via signage at entry, footage is stored securely, and access is restricted to legitimate purposes. Hidden cameras in private areas (e.g. changing rooms) would NOT be acceptable (1 mark).

(b) Wall of shame — agree with the friend? (3 marks):

• Yes, the friend is correct — displaying photos of suspected shoplifters publicly is both unethical and likely unlawful (1 mark).

• Suspected shoplifters have NOT been convicted in court. Publicly displaying their image breaches privacy norms, exposes the business to defamation if anyone is misidentified, and amounts to extra-judicial punishment by the owner (1 mark).

• The legitimate alternative is to file a police report with the CCTV footage as evidence — the justice system, not the supermarket, is the right body to determine guilt and impose consequences. The owner has every right to use cameras and call police; he has no right to publicly shame an unconvicted person (1 mark).

⚠ The strongest answers identify the TENSION: the owner has a legitimate interest in preventing theft, BUT customers retain privacy rights and the right to be presumed innocent. Acceptable controls protect assets WITHOUT publicly punishing the unconvicted — that distinction earns top marks.
Extended Response4 marks
The owner of a small suburban bakery wants to put internal control procedures in place for the first time.
a. Explain what is meant by the term "internal control". (1 mark)
b. Describe TWO specific internal control procedures the bakery owner should implement over CASH, and explain what each procedure protects against. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Definition (1 mark): Internal control is a system of procedures put in place within the structure of a business to protect its assets, prevent fraud and error, and ensure that financial records accurately capture what has happened.

(b) Two cash procedures (3 marks):

• Separation of duties (1.5 marks) — the person operating the till during the day should not also be the person who counts end-of-day takings and banks the cash. This means no single employee can both steal cash AND cover it up in the records. At the bakery, the morning staff handle the till; the owner counts and banks at end of day.

• Daily till reconciliation (1.5 marks) — the cash in the till at end of each shift must equal the till tape total plus the opening float. Any discrepancy is investigated immediately. This catches small thefts before they accumulate, and surfaces honest errors quickly so they can be corrected.

(Other acceptable procedures: pre-numbered receipts; daily banking; banning IOUs from the till; monthly bank reconciliations; locked cash drawer between transactions.)

💡 For full marks, every procedure named must be linked to the risk it controls. "Separation of duties" alone is worth 0.5 marks; "separation of duties so no single employee can steal AND hide it" is worth 1.5.
