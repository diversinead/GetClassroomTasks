# Ch 10 — Financing for a Small Business (Cheat Sheet)

## Sources of Finance — Internal vs External

| | Internal | External |
|---|---|---|
| **From** | Inside the business | Outside lenders/suppliers |
| **Cost** | No interest | Interest + fees |
| **Examples** | Retained profits, owner capital | Trade credit, overdraft, term loan, leasing |
| **Control** | Owner keeps full control | Lender may set conditions |

> Start-ups usually rely on **external** finance — no retained profits yet.

## Interest — The Three Rates

| | What it means | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Flat rate** | Interest charged on the **original principal** for the whole term | Same $ every year, **even after you've paid most of the loan off** |
| **Reducing balance** | Interest charged on the **outstanding balance** | $ falls each year as principal is repaid — the *honest* rate |
| **Effective rate** | The **true annual cost** of a flat-rate loan = avg interest ÷ avg balance × 100 | The number to compare against a reducing-balance quote |

**Rule of thumb:** Effective rate ≈ **roughly double** the flat rate.
> A 10% flat loan really costs ~20% p.a. — always ask which type is quoted.

## Key Formulas

- **Flat interest** = Principal × Rate × Time
- **Effective rate** = (Avg annual interest ÷ Avg balance) × 100
- **Debt Ratio** = Total Liabilities ÷ Total Assets × 100
- **ROI** = Net Profit ÷ Owner's Capital × 100   *(use average capital if it changed during the year)*

## Gearing — Low vs High

**Gearing** = the *mix* of debt and equity used to fund a business. It tells you how much of the business is funded by **borrowed money** versus the **owner's own capital**. Measured by the **debt ratio** (Liabilities ÷ Assets × 100).

- More debt in the mix → **higher gearing** → more risk, but bigger ROI swings
- More owner equity in the mix → **lower gearing** → safer, but smaller ROI

| | Low gearing (≤50% debt) | High gearing (>50% debt) |
|---|---|---|
| Funded mostly by | Owner equity | Borrowed funds |
| Risk | Lower — fewer fixed interest commitments | Higher — must pay interest even in bad years |
| **ROI in good times** | Modest boost | **Magnified UP** |
| **ROI in bad times** | Mild dip | **Magnified DOWN** |
| Lender view | Easy to get more credit | Higher credit risk |

**Why gearing amplifies ROI:** the SAME profit is shared between FEWER owner dollars when more of the funding is debt — so each owner $ earns a bigger return. But if profit dips below the interest bill, those few owner $ absorb the whole loss.

## Worked Example — Same Profit, Different Gearing

Sarah's Cafe needs $100,000 in assets. Profit before interest = $14,000. Interest 8%.

| | Low gearing (A) | High gearing (B) |
|---|---|---|
| Owner's capital | $80,000 | $20,000 |
| Debt | $20,000 | $80,000 |
| Profit before interest | $14,000 | $14,000 |
| Less Interest (8%) | ($1,600) | ($6,400) |
| **Net profit** | **$12,400** | **$7,600** |
| **ROI** | **15.5%** | **38%** |

> Option B's owner earns more than DOUBLE the ROI — but if profit fell to $6,000, Option B would make a **loss** after interest while Option A would still be profitable.

## The Classic Exam Trap

> *"Explain why high gearing can be both an advantage AND a disadvantage."*

A one-sided answer caps your marks. Always cover BOTH:
- **Advantage:** Amplifies ROI in good years; lets the business grow faster than owner funds alone allow.
- **Disadvantage:** Fixed interest must be paid in bad years too; magnifies losses; harder to borrow further.

## The 5 Cs of Credit (lender's checklist)

**C**haracter (track record) · **C**apacity (ability to repay) · **C**apital (owner's own stake) · **C**ollateral (security pledged) · **C**onditions (economy/industry)

If you can confidently compare flat vs reducing rates AND explain how gearing magnifies ROI in both directions, you've nailed the two highest-frequency topics in this chapter.
