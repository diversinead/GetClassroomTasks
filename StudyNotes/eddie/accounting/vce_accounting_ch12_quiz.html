📋 Short Answer Questions
Short Answer3 marks
A clothing store sells three product lines. The owner is considering a uniform 90% mark-up across all items.
Jeanscost $40
T-shirtscost $10
Baseball capscost $5
Calculate the selling price of each item under a 90% mark-up. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Jeans (1 mark): $40 × 1.90 = $76

T-shirts (1 mark): $10 × 1.90 = $19

Baseball caps (1 mark): $5 × 1.90 = $9.50

⚠ Mark-up percentage is applied to the COST price (not the selling price). Multiply by 1.90, not 0.90 — students who multiply by 0.90 and report $36 / $9 / $4.50 lose all three marks.
Short Answer★ HOT4 marks
The Year 12 Students' Representative Council (SRC) is planning to make and sell rugby tops to raise funds. The school will charge the SRC $60 per week to rent a room for production. Each rugby top will cost $45 in materials to make, and is intended to retail for $50.
a. Calculate the contribution margin per rugby top. (1 mark)
b. Calculate how many rugby tops the SRC must sell each week to break even. (2 marks)
c. How many tops would the SRC need to sell in a week to make a profit of $100? (1 mark)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Contribution margin (1 mark): $50 selling price − $45 variable cost = $5 per top.

(b) Break-even units (2 marks): Fixed costs ÷ contribution per unit (1 mark for formula) = $60 ÷ $5 = 12 rugby tops per week (1 mark for answer).

(c) Target profit units (1 mark): ($60 fixed + $100 target) ÷ $5 contribution = 32 tops.

💡 Always state the formula in words (or symbols) BEFORE plugging in numbers — examiners award a method mark for the right formula even when the arithmetic is wrong.
Short Answer4 marks
Reliable Golf Buggies incurred the following costs during March 2028:
Cost of each buggy (materials)$80 per buggy
Monthly salaries$1,600
Monthly cleaning$400
Monthly rent$2,400
a. Classify each cost as fixed or variable, with a brief reason. (2 marks)
b. State the total fixed costs and the variable cost per buggy. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Classification (2 marks):

• Cost of each buggy ($80) — variable: total cost rises and falls in direct proportion to the number of buggies sold (1 mark).

• Salaries, cleaning, rent — fixed: each is paid in full regardless of how many buggies are sold during the month (1 mark).

(b) Totals (2 marks): Total fixed costs = $1,600 + $400 + $2,400 = $4,400 (1 mark). Variable cost per buggy = $80 (1 mark).

⚠ A classification answer that simply labels each line as "fixed" or "variable" without justification will typically lose 1 of the 2 marks — examiners want to see the behavioural reason.
📚 Extended Response Questions
Extended Response★ HOT8 marks
Chris Biffin owns Reliable Golf Buggies, a small business that sells golf buggies at $120 each. The following income statement was prepared for March 2028.
Reliable Golf Buggies — Income Statement for month ended 31 March 2028
Sales of buggies$9,600
Less Cost of buggies$6,400
Gross profit$3,200
Less Salaries$1,600
Less Cleaning$400
Less Rent$2,400
Net loss($1,200)
a. How many golf buggies did Biffin sell during March? (1 mark)
b. State the variable cost per buggy. (1 mark)
c. Calculate the contribution margin per buggy. (1 mark)
d. Calculate the break-even point in units. (2 marks)
e. Explain why the business suffered a loss in March, supporting your answer with reference to the break-even point. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Units sold (1 mark): $9,600 ÷ $120 = 80 buggies.

(b) Variable cost per buggy (1 mark): $6,400 ÷ 80 = $80.

(c) Contribution margin per buggy (1 mark): $120 − $80 = $40.

(d) Break-even units (2 marks): Total fixed costs = $1,600 + $400 + $2,400 = $4,400 (1 mark). Break-even = $4,400 ÷ $40 = 110 buggies (1 mark).

(e) Explanation of the loss (3 marks):

• Biffin sold only 80 buggies, which is 30 below the break-even point of 110 (1 mark).

• Each buggy contributes $40 toward covering fixed costs — meaning the business needed 110 buggies just to cover the $4,400 of fixed expenses (1 mark).

• The shortfall of 30 buggies × $40 contribution = $1,200, which matches the net loss shown on the income statement exactly (1 mark).

💡 Reconciling the loss back to the formula ("30 buggies short × $40 = $1,200") is the gold-standard answer — it proves the student understands both the formula and the income statement.
Extended Response★ HOT8 marks
Ebony Rea runs Classic Barstools. The following income statement was prepared for May 2028, during which Rea sold 48 barstools.
Classic Barstools — Income Statement for month ended 31 May 2028
Sales of barstools$7,200
Less Cost of barstools$3,840
Gross profit$3,360
Less Wages$2,000
Less Electricity$130
Less Telephone$120
Less Advertising$200
Net profit$910
a. Calculate the selling price per barstool, the variable cost per barstool, and the contribution margin per barstool. (3 marks)
b. Calculate the break-even point in units. (2 marks)
c. If Rea wants to earn a profit of $3,500 per month, how many barstools must she sell at the current price? (1 mark)
d. If Rea can only produce and sell 70 barstools per month, what selling price would she need to charge to earn $3,500 profit? (Assume the cost per barstool stays at the current variable cost.) (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Per-unit figures (3 marks):

• Selling price = $7,200 ÷ 48 = $150 per barstool (1 mark).

• Variable cost = $3,840 ÷ 48 = $80 per barstool (1 mark).

• Contribution margin = $150 − $80 = $70 per barstool (1 mark).

(b) Break-even units (2 marks): Total fixed costs = $2,000 + $130 + $120 + $200 = $2,450 (1 mark). Break-even = $2,450 ÷ $70 = 35 barstools (1 mark).

(c) Units for $3,500 profit (1 mark): ($2,450 + $3,500) ÷ $70 = $5,950 ÷ $70 = 85 barstools.

(d) Selling price at capped volume (2 marks): Required contribution per barstool = ($2,450 + $3,500) ÷ 70 = $85 (1 mark). Required selling price = $85 + $80 variable cost = $165 per barstool (1 mark).

💡 When the question caps the unit volume, the lever is PRICE, not volume. Use the formula ((FC + target profit) ÷ units) + VC per unit — do not try to use the standard break-even units formula.
Extended Response★ HOT8 marks
Kira owns Kira's Kebabs. She currently charges $10 per kebab. Each kebab costs $4 in ingredients to make. Her weekly fixed expenses are casual wages $500 and rent $300. Over the last few months Kira has averaged sales of 500 kebabs per week. She is considering three options:
Option 1Drop selling price to $8 per kebab — expects sales to rise 20% to 600/week.
Option 2Keep $10 price, give a free soft drink with each kebab. Drinks cost 80c each. Expects sales to rise 10% to 550/week.
Option 3Run an advertising campaign costing $400/week. Expects sales to rise 10% to 550/week, no other extra costs.
a. Calculate the anticipated weekly profit under each of the three options. (5 marks)
b. Compare the results and comment briefly on each option. (1 mark)
c. Recommend which option (if any) Kira should choose, and explain why. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Baseline (for comparison): contribution per kebab = $10 − $4 = $6. Profit = (500 × $6) − $800 fixed = $2,200/week.

(a) Three options (5 marks):

• Option 1 (1 mark): contribution = $8 − $4 = $4 per kebab. Profit = (600 × $4) − $800 = $1,600.

• Option 2 (2 marks): contribution = $10 − $4 − $0.80 drink = $5.20 (1 mark). Profit = (550 × $5.20) − $800 = $2,860 − $800 = $2,060 (1 mark).

• Option 3 (2 marks): contribution = $10 − $4 = $6 (1 mark). Profit = (550 × $6) − $800 − $400 ad spend = $3,300 − $1,200 = $2,100 (1 mark).

(b) Comparison (1 mark): Option 1 destroys margin and drops profit by $600 even with the volume lift; Option 2 recovers most of the lost contribution but the drink cost erodes margin; Option 3 produces the highest profit of the three because the campaign is a known fixed cost and the unit margin is preserved.

(c) Recommendation (2 marks): None of the three options exceeds the current $2,200/week baseline (1 mark). Kira's best course is to leave pricing unchanged. If forced to pick among the three, Option 3 produces the highest projected profit ($2,100) and carries the lowest margin risk because the $400 ad spend is a fixed, known commitment — whereas Option 2's profit depends on customers actually taking the free drink (1 mark).

⚠ A common student error here is to compare only the three options to each other and miss that the baseline is best. Always benchmark against the current position before recommending a change.
