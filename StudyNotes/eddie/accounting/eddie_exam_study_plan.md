## The Strategy

Three principles driving this plan:

**1. Work backwards from the exams.**
The day before each exam is for **active recall and practice questions only** — not new content. Anything that hasn't been learned by then won't stick.

**2. Front-load Accounting.**
It's the biggest, hardest, and first. The English GA already has the AI Companion framework ready to go — that one is mostly polish. PE only needs the weekend.

**3. Spaced practice beats cramming.**
A 40-minute Accounting session every day beats one 4-hour binge. Each chapter should be touched on multiple days, not just once.

## Daily Routine (every study day)

This is the structure to apply within each day:

1. **Start with the hardest task** when his brain is freshest (usually first session of the morning).
2. **40-60 min focused blocks**, then a real break (move, eat, get outside — not scrolling).
3. **End each session with active recall** — close the book, write down 3 things he just learned without looking. This is the single highest-value habit.
4. **No new content the night before an exam.** Review only.

---

## What to Avoid

- **Re-reading textbook chapters passively.** This feels productive but barely works. Always pair reading with doing — exercises, summarising, recall.
- **Highlighting / colour-coding for hours.** Same trap. Looks like study, isn't.
- **All-nighters.** Sleep consolidates memory. A 6-hour-sleep brain at the exam is worse than a 1-hour-less-study brain.
- **Doing the easy chapter five times because it feels good.** Spend disproportionate time on the weakest chapter (Ch 11 if cash flow is shaky).
- **Studying with phone in the room.** Even face-down. Even on Do Not Disturb. Put it in another room.

## The Mindset

The goal isn't to know everything — it's to **understand the underlying principles well enough to handle whatever the exam throws at him.** For Accounting specifically:

- If he understands **why** loans aren't revenue and **why** equipment isn't an expense, he can handle any variation
- If he understands the **three sections of the Cash Flow Statement**, he can categorise any transaction
- If he understands the **matching principle**, he can answer any prepaid/accrued question

The exam will not test memorisation. It will test whether he can apply the principles. The plan above is built to get him there.
