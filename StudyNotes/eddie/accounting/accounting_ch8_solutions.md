# Yr 11 Accounting тАФ Chapter 8 Worked Solutions

*Income Statements for a Service Firm*

A walkthrough of every exercise in the chapter, with the reasoning behind each step. The big principle running through all of these: **the Income Statement only shows revenue earned and expenses incurred тАФ it does NOT show every cash flow.**

---

## The Five Key Exclusions (memorise these)

These cash flows are **NOT** revenue or expenses, so they never appear on the Income Statement:

1. **Loans received / Loan principal repaid** тАФ financing, not earnings
2. **Capital contributions** тАФ owner putting money in (equity)
3. **Drawings** тАФ owner taking money out (equity)
4. **Purchase of non-current assets** (equipment, computers, vehicles) тАФ they're assets, depreciated over time
5. **GST collected and GST paid** тАФ just passing through to the ATO

Always scan the data for these first and set them aside.

## (e) Verifiability

The qualitative characteristic of **verifiability** means that information should be supported by source documents so that different people examining the same evidence would reach the same conclusion.

The documents shown (receipts and EFT records) satisfy verifiability because:
- They are **original source documents** issued at the time of the transaction
- They show **specific details**: date, amount, GST, what the transaction was for, and who it was with
- They are **independently produced** by the business systems
- They can be **cross-checked** by an auditor against bank statements

This means the figures in the reports can be traced back to objective evidence rather than relying on memory or estimation.

The Feb 7 transaction states: "Cost of fuel for the month тАФ $320 paid in cash, $60 still owing."

- The **total fuel expense** for February is **$380** ($320 paid + $60 still owing)

- This gives a more **relevant** picture of February's true performance

This is the **accrual basis** of accounting тАФ expenses are recognised when incurred, not when paid. Similarly, the $90 telephone bill is expensed even though it isn't paid yet.

# Question 7 тАФ Hawthorn (Jetski Hire)

## (a) Advantage and disadvantage of paying the full $125,000 upfront

**Advantage:** Locks in the rental cost for 5 years and avoids future rent increases. Hawthorn may also have negotiated a discount for paying upfront.

**Disadvantage:** Significant cash outflow immediately, putting strain on cash flow. The business hasn't even started yet and is already $125,000 down before earning any revenue. Could also affect ability to fund start-up costs.

## (b) Comment on each family member's suggestion

| Family member | Suggestion | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| **Brother** | Treat full $125,000 as an expense in 2028 | **Wrong.** This violates the matching principle. The $125,000 secures rent for **5 years**, so charging the entire amount against one year overstates expenses and understates profit in 2028 and understates expenses (overstates profit) in years 2-5. |
| **Mother** | Treat one-fifth ($25,000) as an expense | **Correct in principle.** This matches the cost to the period that benefits from it (one year of rent per year of the contract). |
| **Father** | Report $30,000 per year (typical annual rent) | **Wrong.** Hawthorn didn't pay $30,000 тАФ she paid $25,000 effective per year. Using the typical amount ignores the actual agreement and overstates expenses. |

## (c) How much rent expense should be reported for the year ended 31 December 2028?

The 5-year rental runs from **1 July 2028 to 30 June 2033**. The first reporting period ends **31 December 2028** тАФ that's **6 months of rent**.

- Annual portion: $125,000 ├╖ 5 = $25,000 per year
- 6 months in 2028: $25,000 ├╖ 2 = **$12,500 rent expense for 2028**

**Reasoning тАФ accounting concepts applied:**

- **Period assumption:** Reports must cover defined periods (here, the year ended 31 December 2028).
- **Going concern:** The business is assumed to continue operating, so the $125,000 will benefit future periods, not just 2028.
- **Relevance:** The income statement should show only the rent that relates to the period being reported on.
- **Faithful representation:** Treating the full amount as a 2028 expense would not faithfully represent the economic reality (the business has 5 years of rent, not just one year).

The remaining $112,500 is reported as a **Prepaid Rent** asset on the balance sheet (current asset for the next 12 months portion; non-current asset for the rest).

# Question 8 тАФ Bobby's Boats

## (a) How much revenue (if any) for the six months ended 30 June 2028?

**Answer: $0 revenue for the six months ended 30 June 2028.**

**Reasoning:**

Under the **definition of revenue**, revenue is an **inflow of economic benefits in the form of increases in assets (or decreases in liabilities) that result in an increase in owner's equity** тАФ and it must be **earned**, not merely received.

For Bobby's Boats:
- The contract was signed 1 May 2028 тАФ but signing a contract doesn't earn revenue
- The 10% deposit ($5,000) was received within 30 days тАФ but this is a deposit, not earned revenue. The boat hasn't been built or delivered yet.
- The progress payments ($15,000 + $5,000 + $5,000 = $25,000) by 30 June are also deposits тАФ the boat is still being built.

The boat was **delivered on 22 December 2028** тАФ that's when:
- Control of the asset transfers to the customer
- The performance obligation is satisfied
- Revenue is earned

So at 30 June 2028, the $25,000 received is reported as a **Revenue Received in Advance (liability)**, not revenue. It only becomes revenue when the boat is delivered.

## (b) How much revenue should Bobby's Boats report for the six months ended 31 December 2028?

**Answer: $50,000 тАФ the full contract price.**

**Reasoning:**

By 31 December 2028:
- The boat was delivered on 22 December 2028
- Performance obligation is satisfied
- All revenue is now earned, regardless of whether the final balance has been paid

The fact that Jackson has 30 days to pay the final $25,000 doesn't matter for revenue recognition тАФ once the goods are delivered, the revenue is earned. The outstanding balance becomes a **Debtor (Accounts Receivable)** on the balance sheet.

So the income statement for the six months ended 31 December 2028 shows **$50,000 in revenue**, and the balance sheet shows:
- Cash received in advance ($25,000) is reversed
- Accounts receivable for the final $25,000

This applies the **revenue recognition principle**: revenue is recognised when earned (control transferred), not when cash is received.

## (d) Comment on cash position

Despite making a **net loss of $950**, the bank balance has only fallen from $50,000 to $37,345 тАФ a decline of $12,655. The cash position is **healthier than the profit suggests** in some ways, but worse in others:

- The loan ($18,000 received) and the assets purchased ($18,000 in furniture, equipment and computer) largely offset each other in cash terms
- Drawings ($8,800) and loan principal repayments ($2,100) used cash without affecting profit
- The business has paid out $6,105 in GST and only collected $5,300 тАФ a net cash drain that's not an expense
- **Conclusion:** The cash balance looks reasonable but is masking that the underlying operations are running at a loss. Without the $18,000 loan injection, the cash position would be much worse.

## (e) Comment on profit result

A **net loss of $950** after a full year of operation is concerning but not disastrous. Considerations:

- **First year of operation** тАФ start-up costs (advertising to build a client base, learning curve) often mean year 1 isn't profitable. Worth seeing whether revenue grows in year 2.

# Exam-Style Questions

## Question 1 тАФ A1 Car Wash computer ($3,000) treated as expense

**Explain why this is incorrect and how it should be treated.**

A computer is a **non-current asset**, not an expense. The reasoning:

- The **definition of an asset** is a resource controlled by the entity from which future economic benefits are expected to flow. A computer will be used in the business for multiple years to help generate revenue тАФ it has future economic benefit.
- The **definition of an expense** is a decrease in economic benefits in the form of an outflow or depletion of assets that results in a decrease in owner's equity. An expense is consumed within the reporting period.
- Treating the $3,000 as an expense in one year violates the **matching principle** тАФ the cost should be matched to the revenue it helps generate over its useful life (likely 3-5 years).

**Correct treatment:**
- Record the $3,000 as a **non-current asset (Office Equipment)** on the balance sheet
- Each year, recognise a **depreciation expense** to allocate the cost over the asset's useful life
- For example, with a 5-year useful life and no residual value, depreciation expense = $3,000 ├╖ 5 = **$600 per year**
- This $600 appears on the Income Statement each year for 5 years

## Question 2 тАФ City Drycleaning: cash up $13,000 but net loss of $25,000

**Explain how cash can rise while the business makes a loss.**

This is a classic case of cash flow and profit telling different stories. Possible reasons:

**Example 1: Loan received**
The business may have taken out a loan during the year тАФ say, $40,000. This boosts cash but doesn't affect profit (loans are liabilities, not revenue). So cash rises even though the business is losing money operationally.

**Example 2: Sale of a non-current asset**
The business may have sold equipment for cash тАФ say, sold a van for $30,000. The cash from the sale boosts the bank balance but doesn't appear as revenue (the asset's value is removed from the balance sheet, with any gain/loss being a separate item). Meanwhile, the day-to-day operations made a loss.

**Other possible reasons:**
- Capital contribution from owner тАФ adds cash, not profit
- Collection of debtors from previous periods тАФ cash in, no new revenue
- Significant depreciation expense тАФ reduces profit but not cash
- Bad debts written off тАФ reduces profit but had no cash effect this year

**Key principle:** Profit is measured on the **accrual basis** (revenue earned and expenses incurred), while the bank balance reflects only **cash transactions** (operating, investing AND financing). A business can therefore be cash-positive and unprofitable at the same time.

# Summary тАФ Things to Remember for the GA

1. **Income Statement = Revenue earned тИТ Expenses incurred** (accrual, not cash)
2. **Always exclude:** loans, capital, drawings, non-current assets, GST
3. **Always include:** fees, interest received, wages, rent, advertising, fuel used, etc.
4. **Prepaid amounts** = asset on balance sheet; expense only the portion used
5. **Revenue is earned when delivered**, not when cash is received (deposits = liabilities)
6. **Profit тЙа Cash** тАФ explain the difference using loans, drawings, asset purchases, depreciation
7. **Match expenses to the period** they help generate revenue in (matching principle)
