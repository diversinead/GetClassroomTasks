📋 Short Answer Questions
Short Answer★ HOT2 marks
Distinguish between cash and profit, giving ONE example of each. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Distinction (1 mark): Cash is the actual money in the business's bank account — recorded only when money physically moves in or out. Profit is revenue earned minus expenses incurred during a period — recorded when the activity happens, regardless of when cash is paid or received.

Examples (1 mark): A $1,000 sale made in June but paid by the customer in August counts as revenue (profit) in June, but only appears as cash in August. Likewise, a $500 electricity bill for June received in July is a June expense (profit) but a July cash outflow.

💡 Strong answers always link the distinction to timing — when the activity happens vs when the money moves.
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
Sarah's Café reports the following figures for the year ended 30 June 2025: Sales $85,000; Cost of goods sold $32,000; Wages $18,000; Rent $12,000; Electricity $2,400; Advertising $1,800; Insurance $1,200; Depreciation — Equipment $1,600.
(a) Calculate Gross Profit. (1 mark)
(b) Calculate Total Operating Expenses. (1 mark)
(c) Calculate Net Profit. (1 mark)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Gross Profit (1 mark): Sales $85,000 − Cost of goods sold $32,000 = $53,000.

(b) Total Operating Expenses (1 mark): $18,000 + $12,000 + $2,400 + $1,800 + $1,200 + $1,600 = $37,000.

(c) Net Profit (1 mark): Gross Profit $53,000 − Total Operating Expenses $37,000 = $16,000.

⚠ Always show formula AND calculation. A bare "$16,000" with no working risks losing the calculation mark.
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
Explain why depreciation appears as an expense on the Income Statement but does NOT appear in the Statement of Receipts and Payments. Refer to the difference between cash and profit in your answer. (3 marks)
✅ Model Answer

Why it's an expense (1 mark): Depreciation is the gradual using-up of a non-current asset (such as equipment or a delivery van) as it generates revenue over its useful life. Under accrual accounting, the cost of that asset must be matched against the revenue it helps earn each period — so a portion of the asset's cost is recorded as an expense every year.

Why no cash moves (1 mark): The cash outflow happened once, at the time of purchase, and was recorded then as a payment for a non-current asset. Each year's depreciation expense is just a paper allocation of that original cost — no further cash leaves the bank account.

Link to cash vs profit (1 mark): This is why depreciation reduces profit (Income Statement) but never appears in the Statement of Receipts and Payments — profit and cash measure different things. It is one reason a business can show low profit yet have healthy cash, or vice versa.

💡 Top answers explicitly name accrual accounting and the matching idea — matching the cost of an asset against the revenue it helps earn.
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
For each of the following, state whether it would appear on the Income Statement (yes / no) and explain in ONE sentence why. (3 marks)
(a) Owner's capital contribution of $10,000
(b) Cash sale of $850 in coffee and food
(c) Repayment of $4,000 of loan principal
✅ Model Answer

(a) NO (1 mark): An owner's capital contribution is an increase in equity, not income — the business has not earned anything; the owner has simply added funds. It belongs in the Cash Flow Statement (financing activities) and on the Balance Sheet, not the Income Statement.

(b) YES (1 mark): A cash sale is operating revenue — the business has earned income by providing goods or services. It is recorded in full as revenue on the Income Statement.

(c) NO (1 mark): Repaying loan principal reduces a liability — the business is not using up resources to earn revenue. Only the interest portion of loan repayments is an expense; the principal portion is a financing cash outflow only.

⚠ The yes/no alone is worth nothing without the reason. Always link the answer to the definition of revenue (earned) or expense (resources used to earn revenue).
Short Answer★ HOT3 marks
Sarah's Café reports Net Profit of $16,000 on Sales of $85,000 for the year ended 30 June 2025.
(a) Calculate the Net Profit Margin. (1 mark)
(b) Comment on whether this margin is strong for a café, identifying ONE factor that could be limiting it. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Net Profit Margin (1 mark): $16,000 / $85,000 x 100 = 18.8%.

(b) Comment (2 marks): An 18.8% margin is reasonable but not exceptional for a café — for every $1 of sales, the business keeps about 19 cents after all expenses. Limiting factors might include high wage costs (cafes are labour-intensive), rising rent or utility bills, or significant cost of goods sold (premium coffee, fresh produce). To improve, Sarah could review her pricing, reduce stock waste, or look at staff rostering during quieter periods.

💡 High-scoring answers always link the number to a specific business reality (high wages, rent, stock costs) rather than vague phrases like "expenses are too high".

Extended Response★ HOT6 marks
Sammy's Detailing is a small car-detailing service. For the year ended 30 June 2027, the records show: Service revenue $72,000; Wages $28,000; Rent $9,600; Insurance $2,400; Advertising $1,500; Electricity $1,800; Depreciation — Equipment $3,200.
(a) Prepare a complete Income Statement for Sammy's Detailing for the year ended 30 June 2027. (3 marks)
(b) Calculate the Net Profit Margin. (1 mark)
(c) Comment on the profitability of the business, identifying ONE strength and ONE potential concern. (2 marks)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Income Statement (3 marks):

Sammy's Detailing — Income Statement — for the year ended 30 June 2027
Revenue: Service revenue = $72,000
Less Expenses: Wages $28,000 + Rent $9,600 + Insurance $2,400 + Advertising $1,500 + Electricity $1,800 + Depreciation $3,200 = $46,500
Net Profit = $72,000 − $46,500 = $25,500

(b) Net Profit Margin (1 mark): $25,500 / $72,000 x 100 = 35.4%.

(c) Comment (2 marks): Strength: A 35.4% margin is strong for a small service business — Sammy keeps about 35 cents of every dollar earned, which leaves a healthy buffer for slow months and reinvestment. Concern: Wages of $28,000 are the largest single expense (about 39% of revenue), so the business is heavily dependent on labour productivity — if Sammy needs to hire additional staff or pay overtime, the margin could shrink quickly. The fixed costs (rent, insurance, depreciation) also mean revenue must stay above roughly $46,500 each year just to break even.

⚠ (a) MUST use the correct heading format ("for the year ended"). Service firms have no Cost of Goods Sold — do not invent one. (c) Both a strength AND a concern needed for full marks — answers that only list one side score 1.
Extended Response★ HOT5 marks
Sarah's Café reports a Net Profit of $20,000 for the year ended 30 June 2025. Despite this, by year-end Sarah is struggling to pay her own bills on time. A review of the year's activity shows: (i) $18,000 of sales remain unpaid by credit customers; (ii) Sarah paid $8,000 cash for a new espresso machine in May; (iii) the owner withdrew $7,000 in drawings during the year.
(a) Explain how a business can report a $20,000 profit yet struggle to pay bills. (2 marks)
(b) From the three items above, identify which are NOT expenses, and explain why they are excluded from the Income Statement. (2 marks)
(c) Suggest ONE action Sarah could take to improve her cash position WITHOUT affecting reported profit. (1 mark)
✅ Model Answer

(a) Profit vs cash (2 marks): Profit is calculated on revenue earned and expenses incurred, regardless of when cash moves. Sarah's $20,000 profit reflects sales she has made and expenses she has used up — but a large slice of those sales ($18,000) is sitting in unpaid customer invoices, and significant cash has gone to items that are NOT expenses (a new espresso machine, owner drawings). The result is a profitable business that cannot pay its bills because its cash is tied up in receivables and in non-expense outflows.

(b) Non-expenses (2 marks): The $8,000 espresso machine is the purchase of a non-current asset — it is not consumed in the current period, so its cost is spread over its useful life as depreciation; only depreciation appears as an expense, not the full $8,000. The $7,000 drawings reduce owner's equity — they are not a business resource used to earn revenue, so they never appear on the Income Statement.

(c) Action (1 mark): Sarah could chase up her overdue credit customers more aggressively (or tighten credit terms going forward) to convert the $18,000 in receivables into cash. This brings cash in without changing revenue or expenses, so profit is unaffected but the bank balance recovers.

💡 For (c), other valid answers include arranging a short-term overdraft, leasing equipment instead of buying outright, or pausing drawings — provided the action doesn't change reported revenue or expenses.
