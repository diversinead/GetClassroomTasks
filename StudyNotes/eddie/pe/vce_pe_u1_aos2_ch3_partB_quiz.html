Short Answer3 marks
1. Define cardiac output, stroke volume and heart rate, including the units used for each.
✅ Model Answer
✓Cardiac output = volume of blood pumped out of the heart per minute (L/min). (1 mark)
✓Stroke volume = volume of blood pumped out of the heart per beat / per systole (mL/beat). (1 mark)
✓Heart rate = number of times the heart beats per minute (bpm). (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Always include the units — many students lose marks by writing the definition without L/min, mL/beat or bpm.
Short Answer2 marks
A swimmer has a heart rate of 140 bpm and a stroke volume of 100 mL during a 400m freestyle.
2. Calculate the swimmer's cardiac output. Show your working and express the answer in L/min.
✅ Model Answer
✓Q = SV × HR = 100 × 140 = 14,000 mL/min (1 mark)
✓= 14 L/min (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Always convert mL/min → L/min in the final answer. Leaving the number in mL/min commonly costs a mark.
Short Answer3 marks
3. Describe how heart rate, stroke volume and cardiac output each change as exercise intensity rises from rest to VO2 max.
✅ Model Answer
✓Heart rate increases in a direct linear relationship with exercise intensity, right up to VO2 max. (1 mark)
✓Stroke volume increases up to its finite capacity at around 40–50% max HR, then plateaus — it does not continue to rise at higher intensities. (1 mark)
✓Cardiac output increases in a roughly linear fashion to VO2 max; once SV has plateaued, further Q increases are driven mainly by HR. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: The SV plateau is the most-missed point. State explicitly that SV stops rising while HR and Q keep climbing.
Short Answer4 marks
The Ironman Triathlon is usually scheduled to be held at a time when athletes will not have to compete in high temperatures.
4. Explain, physiologically, what occurs when exercising in high temperatures and suggest what implications this could have on an athlete's performance. (VCAA 2020 Section B Q8c style)
✅ Model Answer
✓High temperatures increase the need to thermoregulate and cool down. (1 mark)
✓To cool down, blood is redistributed to the skin's surface via vasodilation. (1 mark)
✓Less blood and oxygen is therefore sent to the working muscles due to vasoconstriction. (1 mark)
✓Less blood and oxygen at the working muscles results in a decreased rate of energy production — the athlete slows down. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Build a chain: heat → vasodilation to skin → vasoconstriction to muscles → less O2 → less energy → performance drop. Missing any link in the chain costs a mark.
Extended Response6 marks
5. Describe how blood is redistributed throughout the body as a person moves from rest to maximal exercise. Use the term ‘vascular shunt’ and explain the role of both vasodilation and vasoconstriction. Include approximate percentages of cardiac output going to muscle at rest and at maximal exercise.
✅ Model Answer
✓As exercise commences, working muscles require more fuel and oxygen (delivered via blood), and produce more waste (CO2, heat, lactate) that must be removed faster. (1 mark)
✓More blood is directed to working muscles via vasodilation — arteries/arterioles widen, increasing blood flow to that area. (1 mark)
✓Less blood is directed to non-essential organs (kidneys, liver, intestines, skin in cool conditions) via vasoconstriction — arteries/arterioles narrow, reducing blood flow. (1 mark)
✓This redistribution of blood is known as the vascular shunt; vasodilation and vasoconstriction occur simultaneously in different parts of the body. (1 mark)
✓At rest, approximately 20% of cardiac output goes to muscles and ~80% to organs. (1 mark)
✓At maximal exercise, up to 80–90% of blood leaving the heart is directed to working muscles. Once exercise ends and recovery completes, distribution returns to resting levels. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Always pair the mechanism (vasodilation/vasoconstriction) with the direction of blood flow (more/less) AND the body part it affects. Quoting the rest (20%) and maximal (80–90%) percentages picks up easy marks.
