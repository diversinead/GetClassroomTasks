## CHAPTER 4 — The Cardiovascular System

### Structure & Function (4.2)

- [ ] I can list the five functions of the cardiovascular system (transport O₂/nutrients, remove wastes, fight disease, thermoregulation, circulate blood)
- [ ] I can name and describe the four chambers of the heart (left/right atrium, left/right ventricle) and their roles
- [ ] I can trace the path of blood through the heart, from the vena cava all the way around — including every named structure
- [ ] I can distinguish between systolic and diastolic phases of the cardiac cycle
- [ ] I can explain the difference between pulmonary circulation and systemic circulation
- [ ] I can compare arteries, veins and capillaries — structure AND function for each
- [ ] I can explain the role of capillaries in gas and nutrient exchange with body cells
- [ ] I can define heart rate and calculate cardiac output using the formula: **Q̇ = SV × HR**

### Thermoregulation (4.3)

- [ ] I can define thermoregulation
- [ ] I can explain how vasodilation helps cool the body during exercise
- [ ] I can explain how vasoconstriction reduces heat loss in cold conditions
- [ ] I can discuss why aerobic performance may decrease in hot or humid conditions

### Acute Responses to Exercise (4.4)

- [ ] I can define acute responses
- [ ] I can describe what happens to heart rate from rest → submaximal → maximal exercise (and typical values: ~72 bpm rest, up to 200+ bpm max)
- [ ] I can explain that HR increases linearly with exercise intensity
- [ ] I can describe what happens to stroke volume from rest → submaximal → maximal exercise (and why it plateaus at high intensities — reduced filling time)
- [ ] I can calculate cardiac output at rest, submaximal and maximal exercise given HR and SV values
- [ ] I can explain venous return and why it increases during exercise (muscle pump, respiratory pump)
- [ ] I can complete a response table showing HR, SV, Q̇ and blood pressure changes across exercise intensities

### Key Skills (4.5)

- [ ] I can use correct anatomical terminology when describing cardiovascular structures
- [ ] I can analyse a graph of HR or blood flow data and interpret what it shows about exercise intensity
- [ ] I can explain redistribution of blood flow during exercise (away from digestive organs, toward working muscles)

## CHAPTER 5 — The Respiratory System

### Structure & Functions (5.2)

- [ ] I can list the five functions of the respiratory system
- [ ] I can name the structures of the conducting system in order: nasal cavity → pharynx → larynx → trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli
- [ ] I can describe the structure and function of each conducting system structure
- [ ] I can explain the features of the alveoli that make them ideal for gas exchange (thin walls, large surface area, rich capillary network)
- [ ] I can describe the role of the diaphragm and pleura, intercostal muscles

### Mechanics of Breathing & Gaseous Exchange (5.3)

- [ ] I can define ventilation and write the formula: **V = TV × RR**
- [ ] I can calculate ventilation given tidal volume and respiratory rate
- [ ] I can describe what happens during inspiration: diaphragm contracts (moves down), intercostals contract (ribs up/out), chest cavity expands, pressure drops, air rushes in
- [ ] I can describe what happens during expiration: diaphragm relaxes (moves up), ribs down/in, pressure increases, air pushed out; abdominal muscles assist during exercise
- [ ] I can explain gaseous exchange at the alveoli/capillary interface — O₂ moves from high concentration (alveoli) to low concentration (blood); CO₂ moves the opposite way
- [ ] I can explain gaseous exchange at the capillary/muscle interface — O₂ leaves blood and enters muscle cells; CO₂ moves from muscle into blood
- [ ] I can define and distinguish: tidal volume, vital capacity, residual volume, total lung capacity, inspiratory reserve volume, expiratory reserve volume
- [ ] I can define **VO₂ max** — the maximum volume of O₂ per minute that can be taken in, transported and used by working muscles
- [ ] I can explain why VO₂ max is best expressed relative to body weight (mL/kg/min)

### Respiratory Responses to Exercise (5.4)

- [ ] I can describe what happens to respiratory rate from rest → submaximal → maximal exercise
- [ ] I can describe what happens to tidal volume from rest → submaximal → maximal exercise (and why it plateaus)
- [ ] I can describe what happens to ventilation across intensities
- [ ] I can complete a response table showing RR, TV, V and O₂ uptake changes across exercise intensities

### Cardiovascular & Respiratory Interaction (5.5)

- [ ] I can explain how the two systems work together to deliver O₂ to muscles and remove CO₂
- [ ] I can describe the sequence: atmosphere → lungs → alveoli → capillaries → blood → heart → muscles → back again
- [ ] I can discuss the importance of haemoglobin in oxygen transport

### Key Skills (5.6)

- [ ] I can use correct anatomical terminology for all respiratory structures
- [ ] I can analyse primary data (e.g. lung volume spirometry graphs) and interpret changes at different intensities
- [ ] I can describe pulmonary diffusion and explain why it increases during exercise

## CHAPTER 6 — Enhancing the Cardiorespiratory System

### Chronic Adaptations to Aerobic Training (6.2)

- [ ] I can define chronic adaptations (long-term changes from regular training)
- [ ] I can list cardiovascular chronic adaptations: cardiac hypertrophy (enlarged left ventricle), increased stroke volume, decreased resting HR, increased cardiac output (max), increased blood volume, increased capillary density, increased haemoglobin levels
- [ ] I can list respiratory chronic adaptations: stronger diaphragm and intercostals, increased lung volume (vital capacity), increased pulmonary diffusion, increased VO₂ max
- [ ] I can evaluate these adaptations — explaining how each one enhances endurance performance
- [ ] I can compare the types of aerobic training: continuous, fartlek, long-interval, HIIT — and know what each involves

### Permitted Substances & Methods (6.3)

- [ ] I can describe altitude training: training >1500 m above sea level; lower O₂ → body produces more EPO naturally → increased RBC count → greater O₂-carrying capacity
- [ ] I can identify benefits of altitude training for endurance performance
- [ ] I know altitude training can occur at actual altitude or in simulated altitude chambers/rooms

### Prohibited Substances & Methods (6.4)

- [ ] I can explain **EPO** — a hormone naturally produced by kidneys; synthetic form increases RBC production; benefit = greater aerobic endurance and VO₂ max; harms = blood thickening, clots, stroke, heart attack, dehydration, infection risk
- [ ] I can explain **blood doping** — transfusion of own or donor RBCs to increase O₂-carrying capacity; same benefits and harms as EPO use
- [ ] I can explain **beta blockers** — block adrenaline; reduce HR, body tremors, pre-competition anxiety; benefit in precision sports (archery, shooting, golf); harms = low blood pressure, cardiac failure, tiredness, reduced endurance capacity; prohibited by WADA in precision sports
- [ ] I can explain **gene doping** — manipulation of cells or genes to enhance performance; still emerging technology
- [ ] I can identify the permitted alternative for EPO/blood doping = aerobic or altitude training; for beta blockers = psychological techniques (breathing, mental rehearsal)

### Ethical & Sociocultural Considerations (6.5)

- [ ] I can identify sociocultural factors that may pressure athletes to use prohibited substances: income, fame/prestige, coach influence, cultural norms, national ideology, peer pressure
- [ ] I can explain the ethical principle of a fair and even playing field
- [ ] I can discuss the tension between performance enhancement and athlete health and welfare
- [ ] I can make a judgement about whether a substance/method is ethically acceptable and justify that position
