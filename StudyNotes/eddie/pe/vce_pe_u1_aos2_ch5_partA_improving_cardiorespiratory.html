💪 5A.1 — Regular Aerobic Exercise: Health & Fitness Benefits ▼

Participating in regular physical activity and limiting sedentary behaviour is essential to good health and psychosocial wellbeing. To get a cardiovascular, respiratory and musculoskeletal benefit, you need to participate in sufficient levels.

TermDefinition
Physical activityAny bodily movement produced by skeletal muscles that uses energy.
Exercise & sportCommon subcategories of physical activity that are structured or planned.
Sedentary behaviourThe daily amount of time spent sitting or lying down (other than sleeping).

💡 Note: In Unit 2, you'll cover physical, social, mental, emotional and spiritual benefits of regular participation. The focus here is on the physical functioning and capacity of the cardiorespiratory systems.

📅 Australian Physical Activity Guidelines ▼
Age groupRecommended amount and type
Young people (5–17 years)
  • At least 1 hour of moderate-to-vigorous activity per day involving mainly aerobic activities.
  • Vigorous activities at least 3 days per week.
  • Several hours of light activities per day.
Adults (18–64 years)
  • Be active on most days (preferably all).
  • Weekly total: 2.5–5 hours moderate, OR 1.25–2.5 hours vigorous, OR an equivalent combination of both.
🔥 Aerobic Exercise Intensity Zones & Training Methods ▼

For aerobic exercise to provide a health benefit or improve cardiorespiratory functioning, it needs to occur at moderate to vigorous intensity (70–85% max HR). Aerobic activities and exercises primarily rely on energy generated through the aerobic energy system.

All three energy systems work together at every intensity, but one will make the biggest contribution. Aerobic activities target the 70–85% max HR ‘health benefit zone’. HIIT pushes briefly into the closer-to-max zone during work periods, with recoveries in between.

❤ 5A.2 — Cardiovascular Improvements (Chronic Adaptations) ▼

Regular aerobic exercise produces long-term changes to the cardiovascular system. These chronic adaptations increase how much blood and oxygen the body can deliver to working muscles.

AdaptationHow it improves function
Increased left ventricle thickness (cardiac hypertrophy)The left ventricle (systemic blood flow) hypertrophies → greater contraction force → increases SV and Q → more blood pumped per beat AND per minute.
Increased stroke volumeLarger left ventricle holds and ejects more blood per cycle. Contributes to higher cardiac output → more O2, fuels and nutrients to muscles, more CO2 and wastes removed.
Increased blood volumeMore red blood cells = greater O2-carrying capacity. More plasma helps remove wastes and maintains homeostasis & thermoregulation.
Improved oxygen extraction (↑ a-vO2 diff)More O2 is extracted from blood at the capillaries. Exercise can occur at higher aerobic intensities OR moderate intensity feels easier (lower RPE).
Decreased systolic blood pressureThe heart does not need to work as hard at rest. More fuel and oxygen reaches muscles.
Increased vascularisation (capillarisation)An increase in capillary density combined with improved blood redistribution to active muscles. Better O2 & nutrient delivery AND improved waste removal.

⚠ Hypertrophy: An increase in SIZE and THICKNESS of muscle fibres without an increase in their amount. The same muscle fibres become bigger and stronger.

📊 Visual: Untrained vs Trained Heart (Left Ventricle) ▼

After months of regular aerobic training, the left ventricle hypertrophies — thicker walls AND a larger chamber. The heart pumps more blood per beat (higher SV) at a lower resting heart rate. The trained athlete's heart does the same work with less effort.

🫁 5A.3 — Respiratory Improvements (Chronic Adaptations) ▼

Regular aerobic exercise also produces measurable changes in the respiratory system. These adaptations let more air move in and out of the lungs, and let more O2 cross the alveolar/capillary interface.

AdaptationHow it improves function
Increased lung capacityLungs become more elastic → can expand further → a greater amount of O2 moves in/out per breath.
Increased tidal volumeIncreased elasticity + stronger diaphragm & intercostal muscles → more air inspired per breath. Greater TV increases ventilation (VE = TV × RR) → more O2 in, more CO2 out.
Increased alveolar size & surface areaGreatly increases the surface area available for gaseous exchange → greater O2 uptake, more aerobic energy production.
Increased capillarisation (at lungs)More capillaries around alveoli → more blood can flow in/out → more O2 uptake AND more CO2 removal.
Improved strength & endurance of diaphragm and intercostalsGreater contraction force → thoracic cavity expands more → deeper breaths. Greater endurance → muscles contract longer without fatigue.

💡 Did you know? Alveoli increase in size AND number with regular training. Muscle fibres (skeletal) increase in size only — they do NOT typically increase in number.

📊 Visual: Pre vs Post Aerobic Training (Alveoli & Capillaries) ▼

Aerobic training increases alveolar size, alveolar number AND capillary density around them. Both the diffusion area and the volume of blood that can pass through it grow — so much more O2 can move from alveoli into blood per breath.

🏃 5A.4 — Improved Cardiorespiratory Health & Lifestyle Options ▼

When the cardiorespiratory system becomes more efficient, exercise becomes easier to perform. People can take on a wider range of activities — even at higher intensities — without getting exhausted. This opens up new lifestyle choices.

Individuals are therefore more likely to:

  • Exercise with increased frequency — meeting or exceeding activity guidelines.
  • Decrease sedentary or inactive behaviour.
  • Participate in activities requiring vigorous or strenuous effort (above RPE 8).
  • Have higher self-efficacy to participate in activities requiring moderate-to-high endurance — bushwalking, stand-up paddle boarding, cycling — that they might previously have avoided.
  • Participate in moderate aerobic activities — walking around a golf course, attending a spin class, dancing for a long evening.

💡 The exam idea: Improved cardiorespiratory function and capacity → exercise feels easier → person is more likely to exercise more often AND at higher intensities → their fitness improves further. It's a positive feedback loop.

📝 Summary — The Big Picture ▼

Health & fitness benefits of regular aerobic exercise

  • 'Regular' refers to frequency; 'sufficient' refers to duration and intensity.
  • 5–17 yrs: at least 1 hr moderate-vigorous activity per day.
  • 18–64 yrs: 2.5–5 hrs moderate OR 1.25–2.5 hrs vigorous per week.
  • For health benefit, exercise at 70–85% max HR.

Cardiovascular improvements (6 adaptations)

  • ↑ left ventricle thickness
  • ↑ stroke volume
  • ↑ blood volume
  • ↑ a-vO2 diff
  • ↓ blood pressure (resting systolic)
  • ↑ vascularisation / capillarisation

Respiratory improvements (5 adaptations)

  • ↑ lung capacity
  • ↑ tidal volume
  • ↑ alveolar size, number, surface area + capillarisation
  • ↑ strength and endurance of diaphragm & intercostal muscles

Lifestyle options

  • Improved capacity makes exercise easier → more frequent exercise at higher intensities.
