❤ 3B.1 — Cardiac Output, Stroke Volume & Heart Rate ▼

Three variables describe how much blood the heart moves. They are linked by a single formula. You will not be asked to do hard calculations — you need to understand how each variable behaves at rest and during exercise.

Cardiac Output Formula
Q = SV × HR Cardiac Output (L/min) = Stroke Volume (mL/beat) × Heart Rate (bpm)
VariableWhat it isUnits
Cardiac output (Q)The volume of blood pumped out of the heart per minute.L/min
Stroke volume (SV)The volume of blood pumped out of the heart per beat (per systole).mL/beat
Heart rate (HR)The number of times the heart beats per minute.bpm

💡 Example calc: 60 bpm × 70 mL = 4,200 mL/min = 4.2 L/min at rest.

📈 3B.2 — Response at Different Exercise Intensities ▼

As exercise intensity rises from rest up to maximal effort (VO2 max), the three variables respond differently. HR and Q rise linearly. SV rises only up to a point, then plateaus — this is its finite capacity.

⚠ Did you know? Stroke volume only rises until ~40–50% of max heart rate, then plateaus. Above that intensity, only heart rate keeps cardiac output climbing.

📊 Response Curves — How HR, SV & Q Change with Intensity ▼

HR and Q rise in a roughly linear pattern from rest to VO2 max. SV rises only to ~50% intensity and then plateaus at its finite capacity. Beyond that, cardiac output keeps climbing only because HR keeps climbing.

🎯 Deep Dive — Three Key Points to Remember ▼
VariablePattern of changeWhy
Heart rateRises in a direct linear relationship with exercise intensity.Sympathetic NS & adrenaline drive HR up; parasympathetic withdrawal removes the resting brake.
Stroke volumeRises along with intensity up to its finite capacity (around 40–50% max HR), then plateaus.The ventricle can only stretch and fill so much (preload limit). Once full, SV cannot rise further.

💡 Common exam trap: “During a marathon a runner increases their pace — what increases?” If they were already running, SV has already plateaued. Only HR and cardiac output increase further.

🚀 3B.3 — Redistribution of Blood During Exercise ▼

As exercise begins, working muscles need more fuel and oxygen, and produce more waste. The body diverts blood away from organs that don't urgently need it (kidneys, liver, intestines, skin at rest) and sends it to the working muscles instead.

Vasodilation (widening) of arteries/arterioles → more blood to that body part. Used at working muscles during exercise. Vasoconstriction (narrowing) → less blood to that body part. Used at non-essential organs (kidneys, liver, intestines, skin at rest). They happen at the same time — redirecting blood where it's needed most.

🔄 The Vascular Shunt — What & Why ▼

The redistribution of blood throughout the body during exercise is called the vascular shunt. It occurs because:

  • Working muscles need more oxygen and more fuel (glucose, fatty acids) — both delivered by blood.
  • Working muscles produce more waste (CO2, lactate, heat) that needs to be removed faster.
  • Organs that aren't critical during exercise (kidneys, liver, intestines, skin at rest) can temporarily get less blood.

💡 Once exercise stops and recovery is complete, blood distribution returns to its pre-exercise (resting) levels.

📈 Blood Distribution at Rest vs Exercise ▼

At rest: ~20% of cardiac output goes to muscles, ~80% to organs. Light exercise: already a big shift (~50–60% to muscles). Maximal exercise: up to ~80–90% of blood leaving the heart goes to working muscles. Note: cardiac output itself is also much larger during exercise, so muscles get a bigger share of a bigger pie.

🌡 Thermoregulation — Vasodilation to the Skin ▼

When the body overheats during exercise (or in hot conditions), it has to thermoregulate. The brain sends more blood to the skin's surface via vasodilation so that heat can be lost.

This is a key exception to the “all blood goes to muscles” rule. In hot conditions:

  • Skin: vasodilation → more blood to surface to dump heat.
  • Working muscles: may experience vasoconstriction as a result — less blood available than they'd otherwise get.
  • Performance can drop because less oxygenated blood reaches the muscles.

💡 Teacher's tip: Vasodilation and vasoconstriction occur simultaneously — some body parts receive more blood whilst others receive less. The system is always shifting where blood goes based on what the body needs most.

📝 Worked Example — VCAA 2020 (4 marks) ▼

The Ironman Triathlon is usually scheduled to be held at a time when athletes will not have to compete in high temperatures. Explain, physiologically, what occurs when exercising in high temperatures and suggest what implications this could have on an athlete's performance.

Sample answer

As the body tries to cool itself, blood flow is redirected to the skin and away from working muscle. This redistribution assists with thermoregulation but affects muscular energy production, as less oxygenated blood is available to the muscles. As a result of sweating, there is potential for blood to become more viscous, further decreasing oxygen availability. The result would be that the athlete slows down because of a slower rate of energy production.

📝 Summary — The Two Big Ideas ▼

1. Cardiac output, stroke volume and heart rate at different intensities

  • Stroke volume (volume pumped per beat) has a finite capacity and peaks around 40–50% max HR.
  • Heart rate and cardiac output increase linearly in relation to exercise intensity.
  • Formula: Q = SV × HR.

2. Redistribution of blood during exercise

  • The brain sends blood to different body parts depending on need (the vascular shunt).
  • Vasodilation = widening of arteries, increases blood supply.
  • Vasoconstriction = narrowing of arteries, reduces blood supply.
  • At rest: ~80% to organs, ~20% to muscles.
  • Light exercise: a large shift — 50–60% redirected to working muscles.
  • Maximal exercise: up to 80–90% of blood leaving the heart goes to working muscles.
