📈 1.1 Overview of the Musculoskeletal System

📊 Figure 1.1 — Musculoskeletal System Mind Map ▼

The musculoskeletal system combines the skeletal and muscular systems. Together, they interact via levers to produce movement.

🦮 1.2.1 Functions of the Skeletal System

📈 Six Functions of the Skeleton ▼
FunctionDescriptionExample
SupportProvides the structural framework that holds the body uprightVertebral column supports the head and trunk
ProtectionShields vital organs from injurySkull protects the brain; ribcage protects heart and lungs
MovementBones act as levers; muscles pull on bones to create movementFemur and tibia form the lever system at the knee
Blood cell productionRed bone marrow in long bones produces red and white blood cellsFemur, tibia, humerus
Mineral storageStores calcium and phosphorus; released into blood when neededCalcium stored in the cortical bone matrix
Energy storageYellow marrow in the diaphysis (shaft) stores fat as an energy reserveDiaphysis of the femur

💡 You must know ALL six functions. Memorise: Support, Protection, Movement, Blood cell production, Mineral storage, Energy storage.

🦲 1.2.3 Classification of Bones by Shape

🦲 Figure 1.6 — Five Types of Bone ▼
TypeDescriptionExample(s)Function
LongLength greater than width; has a diaphysis (shaft) and epiphyses (ends)Femur, humerus, tibia, fibula, radius, ulnaPrimary lever bones for movement
ShortRoughly cube-shaped; length approximately equals widthCarpals (wrist), tarsals (ankle)Absorb shock; allow gliding movement
FlatThin, broad and slightly curvedSkull, sternum, scapula, ribsMuscle attachment; organ protection
IrregularComplex shapes that do not fit other categoriesVertebrae, pelvisWeight-bearing; muscle attachment
SesamoidSmall bones embedded within tendonsPatella (kneecap)Reduce friction on tendons; redirect force

💡 The patella is the classic sesamoid bone exam example. Students often forget sesamoid as the 5th type.

🦉 1.2.4 The Vertebral Column

🦉 Figure 1.7 — The Vertebral Column (5 sections) ▼
SectionVertebraeFunction
Cervical7Supports the head. Atlas and axis (top two) allow the head to move up, down and side to side
Thoracic12Connects the ribcage to the vertebral column
Lumbar5Supports the weight of the body; provides attachment for large muscles
Sacrum5 fusedFused part of the pelvis
Coccyx4 fusedBase of the vertebral column (tailbone); site for muscle attachment

⚠ Remember: 7-12-5-5-4. Sacrum and coccyx are fused — they do not allow individual vertebral movement.

🔗 1.2.5 Types of Joints

📊 Three Categories of Joint — Overview ▼
CategoryAlso calledMovementStructureExample
FibrousSynarthrosisImmoveableBones joined by fibrous connective tissueSkull sutures, distal tibiofibular joint
CartilaginousAmphiarthrosisSlightly moveableBones connected by cartilageIntervertebral discs, pubic symphysis
SynovialDiarthrosisFreely moveableJoint cavity + synovial membrane + fluidHip, knee, shoulder, elbow

💡 All sport-relevant joints are synovial. Fibrous = skull (immoveable). Cartilaginous = spine discs (slightly moveable).

📊 Figure 1.14 — Six Types of Synovial Joint ▼

⚠ Ball-and-socket = MOST movement (hip, shoulder). Hinge = LEAST (knee, elbow, ankle). Gliding = only slight sliding between flat surfaces.

🧰 1.2.6 Structure of a Synovial Joint

🧰 Figure 1.13 — Basic Structure of a Synovial Joint ▼
StructureFunction
Articular cartilageSmooth hyaline cartilage on bone ends; reduces friction during movement
Synovial membraneLines the joint capsule; secretes synovial fluid
Synovial fluidLubricates and nourishes the joint; acts as a shock absorber
Joint capsuleFibrous outer layer enclosing the joint; holds it together
LigamentsBone-to-bone connective tissue; stabilise joint and prevent excessive ROM
TendonsDense connective tissue attaching muscle to bone; transmit muscular force to the skeleton to produce movement
BursaeFluid-filled sacs near joints that reduce friction between tendons, muscles and bony prominences
MenisciFibrocartilage pads at the knee only; absorb shock and deepen the socket

⚠ Critical: Ligament = bone-to-bone. Tendon = muscle-to-bone. This is the #1 exam mistake. Menisci exist at the knee only.

📍 1.2.7 Anatomical Position Terminology

📍 Table 1.2 — Anatomical Position Terms ▼
TermDefinitionExample
SuperiorTowards the head or upper part of the bodyThe cranium is superior to the sternum
InferiorTowards the feet or lower part of the bodyThe tarsals are inferior to the femur
AnteriorTowards the front of the bodyThe patella is on the anterior side of the body
PosteriorTowards the back of the bodyThe scapula is posterior to the sternum
MedialTowards the midline of the bodyThe sternum is medial to the rib cage
LateralTowards the outer side of the bodyThe fibula is lateral to the tibia
ProximalCloser to the trunk of the bodyThe femur is proximal to the patella
DistalFurther away from the trunk of the bodyThe phalanges are distal to the humerus
SuperficialTowards the surface of the bodyThe rib cage is superficial to the heart
DeepTowards the inner part of the bodyThe liver is deep to the skin
ProneFace down (body position)Lying on the stomach
SupineFace up (body position)Lying on the back

💡 Common pairs to memorise: Superior/Inferior, Anterior/Posterior, Medial/Lateral, Proximal/Distal, Prone/Supine. These appear in movement analysis questions.

▶️ 1.2.8 Anatomical Movements (Joint Actions)

▶️ Table 1.3 — Anatomical Movement Terms ▼
MovementDefinitionExample
FlexionDecrease in the angle of the jointBending the elbow or knee
ExtensionIncrease in the angle of the jointStraightening the elbow or knee
AbductionMovement of a body part away from the midline of the bodyLifting arm out to the side (out phase of star jump)
AdductionMovement of a body part back towards the midline of the bodyReturning arm into the body (in phase of star jump)
CircumductionMovement of the end of the bone in a circular motionDrawing a circle in the air with a straight arm; cricket bowling
RotationMovement of a body part around a central axisTurning the head from side to side
PronationRotation of the hand so the thumb moves in towards the body (palm facing down)Palm facing down
SupinationRotation of the hand so the thumb moves away from the body (palm facing up)Palm facing up
EversionMovement of the sole of the foot away from the midlineTwisting ankle out
InversionMovement of the sole of the foot towards the midlineTwisting ankle in
DorsiflexionDecrease in the angle of the joint between the foot and lower leg (toes up)Raising toes upwards; heel strike in running
PlantarflexionIncrease in the angle of the joint between the foot and lower leg (toes down)Pointing toes to the ground; push-off in sprinting
ElevationMovement of the shoulders towards the headShrugging the shoulders
DepressionMovement of the shoulders away from the headReturning shoulders to normal position after a shrug

💡 In any movement analysis question, name the joint, the joint type, the movement, and the agonist muscle. Dorsiflexion and plantarflexion are ankle-specific — do not just write flexion/extension.
