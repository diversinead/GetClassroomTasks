🫁 4A.1 — Structures of the Respiratory System ▼

The respiratory system has two zones that work together. The conducting zone moves air in and out (no gas exchange). The respiratory zone is where actual gaseous exchange happens.

Air follows the path: nose/mouth → pharynx → larynx → trachea → bronchi → bronchioles → respiratory bronchioles → alveolar ducts → alveoli. Only the last three (in the respiratory zone) are involved in gas exchange.

📊 The Two Zones — Side-by-Side ▼
FeatureConducting zoneRespiratory zone
StructuresMouth/nose, pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi, bronchiolesRespiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, alveoli
Main roleBring air into and out of the lungsSite of gaseous exchange (O2 in, CO2 out)
Gas exchange?NO — not involvedYES — this is where it happens

💡 Common exam trap: “The bronchus makes up part of the respiratory zone” is FALSE. The bronchi are still in the conducting zone. Gas exchange does NOT happen until the respiratory bronchioles and alveoli.

🎾 4A.2 — Inspiration & Expiration (Mechanics of Breathing) ▼

Two muscles move air in and out of the lungs: the diaphragm (dome-shaped muscle at base of chest) and the intercostal muscles (between each rib). Inspiration is ACTIVE, expiration at rest is PASSIVE.

Gases always flow from high pressure to low pressure. Inspiration creates a low-pressure space in the lungs (by expanding chest cavity); expiration creates a high-pressure space (by shrinking chest cavity).

📂 Mechanics Side-by-Side — Active vs Passive ▼
StepInspiration (active)Expiration at rest (passive)
DiaphragmContracts & flattens (pulls down)Relaxes & returns to dome shape (moves up)
IntercostalsExternal intercostals contract — lift ribs up & outIntercostals relax — ribs fall back to resting position

Lung pressureDecreases (below atmospheric)Increases (above atmospheric)

⚠ Did you know? The diaphragm is unique — unlike muscles that move your limbs, it works automatically without conscious thought. When you get ‘winded', the diaphragm spasms and stops functioning properly — which is why air gets sucked in suddenly afterward.

📈 Tidal Volume, Respiratory Rate & Minute Ventilation ▼

As exercise intensity rises, the body needs more O2 in and more CO2 out. It achieves this by increasing both the depth of each breath (TV) and the frequency of breaths (RR).

Minute Ventilation Formula
VE = RR × TV Minute Ventilation (L/min) = Respiratory Rate (bpm) × Tidal Volume (L)
StateTidal volume (TV)Respiratory rate (RR)Minute ventilation (VE)
Rest0.5 L12 bpm6 L/min
Jogging0.7 L20 bpm14 L/min
Running0.7 L40 bpm28 L/min

💡 Like cardiac output: Tidal volume has a finite capacity — at moderate intensities it plateaus, and further VE increases come mainly from rising respiratory rate.

🧠 4A.3 — The Brain Monitoring & Controlling Breathing ▼

Breathing is controlled automatically by the brainstem. Two structures — the medulla and the pons — combine automatic responses with feedback from chemical sensors (chemoreceptors).

The medulla houses central chemoreceptors that detect rising CO2 in brain fluid. The aorta and carotid arteries house peripheral chemoreceptors that detect falling O2 in blood. Both feed signals to the brainstem, which adjusts breathing rate and depth.

📑 Quick Reference — Who Does What ▼
StructureLocationRole
MedullaBrainstemControls frequency of contractions of the diaphragm & intercostals. Senses rising CO2 via central chemoreceptors and triggers ↑ rate/depth of breathing.
PonsBrainstem (above medulla)Responds to exercise stress; ensures smooth, uninterrupted transition between inhaling and exhaling.
Central chemoreceptorsWithin the medullaSensitive to CO2 levels in brain fluid (indirectly reflects blood CO2).
Peripheral chemoreceptorsAorta and carotid arteriesMonitor levels of O2 in blood — signal brain to ↑ breathing if O2 falls too low.

💡 In an exam: If the question is about the brain detecting CO2, the answer is central chemoreceptors in the medulla. If it's about detecting O2 in the blood, the answer is peripheral chemoreceptors in the aorta/carotid arteries.

💨 4A.4 — Gaseous Exchange at the Lungs & Muscles ▼

Gases always move from areas of high pressure/concentration to low pressure/concentration. This happens at two sites: the lungs (external respiration) and the muscles & tissues (internal respiration).

At BOTH sites, gases follow the same rule: high to low pressure. At the lungs, O2 moves into blood (alveolus has more); CO2 moves out (blood has more). At the muscles it's reversed: O2 moves out of blood (muscle is depleted); CO2 moves in (muscle just produced it).

🔗 External vs Internal Respiration — Quick Reference ▼
TermWhere it occursWhat happens
External respirationAt the LUNGS (alveolar/capillary interface)O2 enters blood; CO2 leaves blood to be exhaled.
Internal respirationAt the MUSCLES & tissues (capillary/muscle interface)O2 leaves blood to enter muscles; CO2 enters blood from muscles.
Cellular respirationINSIDE the muscle cellsThe metabolic process that breaks down glucose to produce ATP (energy). Can be aerobic or anaerobic. Produces CO2 as a by-product.

⚠ Easy to confuse: “Respiration” in PE can mean both breathing (air in and out of lungs) and gas exchange (at lungs / muscles) and cellular respiration (energy production). Read carefully to know which the exam wants.

📝 Summary — The Whole Chain ▼

Structures of the respiratory system

  • Conducting zone: mouth/nose → pharynx → larynx → trachea → bronchi → bronchioles. Brings air in/out. NO gas exchange.
  • Respiratory zone: respiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, alveoli. Site of gas exchange.

Inspiration & expiration

  • Inspiration is ACTIVE: diaphragm + intercostals contract → chest volume ↑ → lung pressure ↓ → air in.
  • Expiration at rest is PASSIVE: muscles relax → chest volume ↓ → lung pressure ↑ → air out.
  • Minute ventilation: VE = RR × TV (e.g. rest 12 × 0.5 = 6 L/min).

Brain control

  • Medulla & pons in the brainstem control breathing.
  • Central chemoreceptors in the medulla detect rising CO2.
  • Peripheral chemoreceptors in the aorta/carotid arteries detect falling O2.

Gaseous exchange

  • External respiration: at lungs — O2 in, CO2 out.
  • Internal respiration: at muscles — O2 in to muscle, CO2 out to blood.
  • Gases always move high → low pressure/concentration.
