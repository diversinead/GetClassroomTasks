⚔ 5B.1 — WADA Criteria & Ethical Considerations ▼

A substance or method gets prohibited if it meets two of these three WADA criteria:

WADA criterionQuestion to ask
PerformanceDoes it (or could it) enhance performance in sport?
Health riskDoes it have the potential to (or actually) cause a health risk to the athlete?
Spirit of sportDoes it violate the spirit of sport (as defined in the WADA Code)?

Ethical considerations to ask:

AreaQuestions to ask
Legal complianceDoes the strategy comply with WADA & Sport Integrity Australia? Is it permitted by the sporting authority?
HonestyIs there transparency? Is the strategy recorded/reported for others to monitor?
FairnessCan all athletes access this strategy? Is there a level playing field?
SafetyAre there health risks? Have athletes received enough information to give informed consent?
Role-modellingDoes the strategy provide positive role-modelling? Is it reflective of broader community values?

💡 Professional ethical behaviour: Adhering to and responding to external codes (e.g. medical and legal codes, school policies, workplace and sporting codes). WADA and Sport Integrity Australia maintain these for sport.

🏠 5B.2 — Sociocultural Considerations ▼

Sociocultural = social AND cultural issues or factors that influence groups of people — habits, beliefs and behaviours that impact values including family, peers, socio-economic status, community and cultural norms.

AreaQuestions to ask
Cultural norms & expectationsWhat does the society consider fair play? In some cultures, performance-enhancing substances are unfair; in others, accepted.
PeersAre there unspoken norms to use performance-enhancing practices? Do athletes feel compelled to use prohibited substances to keep up with teammates?
Social acceptanceIn certain cultures and sports, performance enhancing practices might be normalised or even glorified.
Socioeconomic statusDoes money impact the choices athletes make? Does a poorer background limit access to legal training and create incentives to use prohibited substances?
EducationDo athletes receive proper education about risks and consequences? Without clear education, athletes may take prohibited substances unknowingly.
🧐 5B.3 — Reasons Why Athletes Use Prohibited Strategies ▼

People may use prohibited performance-enhancing substances or strategies in sport or recreational pursuits for multiple reasons:

ReasonExplanation
Dissatisfaction with current performancePerformers have reached their genetic potential legally but need more to stay competitive.
Peer / coach pressure“Others in the team are using prohibited substances. Why shouldn't I?” OR trying to meet coach's expectations only achievable via prohibited practices.
Culture within a club, sport or peer groupAn acceptance that to be the best, prohibited practices need to be used — normalisation within the group.
Keeping up with other athletesTeammates or rivals continue to improve and pull ahead despite the permitted strategies the athlete is using.
Financial incentivesHigh-level performance brings significant sponsorships, media attention, endorsement opportunities and lucrative contracts.
Belief they will not be caughtAwareness that teammates/rivals are using prohibited substances and not being detected. May be using masking agents to conceal drugs.
“Win at all costs” mentalityThe belief of “do whatever it takes and take whatever” — knowingly breaching rules and placing own health at risk.
Fame & role model statusEgos motivate unethical behaviour to perform at the top of their sport — also linked to financial rewards.

💡 Masking agent: A substance or product that can supposedly alter the chemical composition of urine or other bodily fluids to conceal the presence of drugs.

⛰ 5B.4 — Altitude & Hypoxic Training PERMITTED ▼

Altitude training is used by endurance athletes seeking to improve their take up, transport and utilisation of oxygen for aerobic energy production. Training at 2000+ metres above sea level lowers oxygen availability, forcing the body to adapt to the hypoxic (low-oxygen) environment.

Most significant adaptations of altitude training:

  • Increased alveolar size, number and surface area → greater O2 absorption and CO2 removal.
  • Increased O2 uptake and delivery (higher VO2 max) → improved aerobic power, higher lactate inflection point, increased endurance.
  • Elevated EPO production by kidneys → more red blood cells → enhanced O2 transportation.
  • Increased capillarisation → greater O2 delivery to tissues, muscles and brain.
  • Enhanced production and repair of mitochondria → more efficient utilisation of O2 for aerobic ATP production.

⚠ EPO (Erythropoietin): A hormone secreted by the kidneys in response to hypoxia that stimulates red blood cell production. Altitude triggers it naturally — injecting synthetic EPO is prohibited.

🏔 Three Permitted Altitude / Hypoxic Methods ▼

There are three main approaches. They all create a hypoxic stimulus to trigger adaptations — they differ in HOW they create it.

'Live high, train high' produces the broadest adaptations but is expensive and disruptive. 'Live high, train low' (altitude chambers/tents) gives similar adaptations with fewer health/training risks. The elevation training mask stresses breathing muscles but does NOT replicate true altitude (no EPO/RBC response).

🩸 5B.5 — EPO & Blood Doping PROHIBITED ▼

Blood doping = artificially increasing the number of red blood cells in the bloodstream to enhance athletic performance. There are several methods, all banned.

MethodHow it works
Synthetic / recombinant EPO injectionLab-made EPO injected directly. Drives RBC production to abnormal levels. Significant aerobic and recovery benefits.
Autologous blood transfusionThe athlete's OWN blood is removed and stored weeks before competition, then reinfused shortly before the event.
Homologous blood transfusionTransfusion of blood from a compatible donor.
Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs)Stimulate bone marrow directly to produce red blood cells.
Synthetic O2-carrying substancesSynthetic substances that mimic haemoglobin.

Potential health risks:

  • Blood clotting: blood thickens and becomes more viscous → risk of blood clots, stroke, heart attack.
  • High blood pressure: thicker blood elevates BP → long-term blood vessel damage.
  • Heart and kidney damage: both organs work much harder → long-term damage.
  • Death: EPO abuse (especially combined with other PEDs) can cause fatal heart attack or stroke.
  • Blood transfusions carry additional risks: blood-borne disease from needles or donor red blood cells.

⚠ Detection: Blood and/or urine tests can detect abnormal RBC levels or synthetic EPO. A biological passport (introduced by WADA) monitors an athlete's biological markers over time to spot anomalies suggesting doping.

💊 5B.6 — Beta Blockers PROHIBITED in certain sports ▼

Beta-blockers work by blocking the effects of adrenaline, resulting in reduced heart rate and widened blood vessels. They are medications commonly used to treat high blood pressure — but their effects on tremor and arousal make them performance-enhancing in precision sports.

AspectDetail
Performance benefits ● Decreased tremor & increased fine motor control
● Reduced anxiety (optimal arousal)
Banned in sports includingArchery, pistol shooting — sports requiring precision and steadiness.
Why permitted in other sports?Beta blockers do NOT enhance endurance, strength or speed — so they don't advantage the athlete in those sports.
Potential health risks ● Hypotension (low blood pressure)
● Altered blood sugar levels
● Asthma attacks
● Cardiac failure

💡 Why precision sports? Adrenaline causes shaky hands and rapid heartbeat — both kill precision in archery or shooting. Blocking adrenaline gives a steady hand and calm pulse, an unfair advantage.

🧬 Gene Doping PROHIBITED ▼

Scientists can manipulate the DNA of cells in the body to alter genetic expression. There are two uses:

TypeDescription
Gene therapyUsed to treat and potentially cure genetic diseases and some cancers. Therapeutic, legal.
Gene dopingThe non-therapeutic use of genes, genetic elements and/or cells that have the capacity to enhance athletic performance. Banned by WADA.

Gene doping involves directly modifying genes or cells in a way that enhances physiological traits such as muscle growth, human growth hormone or endurance, or EPO production.

Potential health risks:

  • Infections, tissue damage, or autoimmune reactions
  • Cancers
  • Harmful genetic mutations

⚠ Emerging concern: Gene doping is currently very difficult to detect. It's an active area of research for anti-doping agencies.

📝 Summary — The Big Picture ▼

Ethical & sociocultural considerations

  • Ethical: legal compliance, honesty, fairness, safety, role-modelling.
  • Sociocultural: cultural norms, peers, social acceptance, SES, education.

Reasons for using prohibited strategies

  • Peer/coach pressure; performance & financial gains; fame; win-at-all-costs mindset.

Permitted — Altitude / Hypoxic Training

  • ‘Live high, train high' (real altitude).
  • ‘Live high, train low' (altitude tents/chambers).
  • Elevation training masks (respiratory muscle stress).

Prohibited — EPO & Blood Doping

  • Increases RBCs to enhance endurance — injectables OR blood transfusions.
  • Risks: clotting, high BP, heart/kidney damage, death.

Prohibited — Beta Blockers & Gene Doping

  • Beta blockers reduce tremor & increase fine motor control (banned in precision sports).
  • Gene doping is the non-therapeutic use of genes/cells to enhance performance.
