Short Answer3 marks
1. Name the two zones of the respiratory system and state which structures belong to each. Identify which zone is responsible for gaseous exchange.
✅ Model Answer
✓The conducting zone includes the mouth/nose, pharynx, larynx, trachea, bronchi and bronchioles. It moves air in and out of the lungs. (1 mark)
✓The respiratory zone includes respiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts and alveoli. (1 mark)
✓The respiratory zone is responsible for gaseous exchange. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Be precise - the bronchi and bronchioles are still in the conducting zone. Only the RESPIRATORY bronchioles (deeper) belong to the respiratory zone.
Short Answer4 marks
2. Describe what happens to the diaphragm, intercostal muscles, thoracic volume and lung pressure during inspiration.
✅ Model Answer
✓Diaphragm contracts (is active) and flattens, pulling down. (1 mark)
✓External intercostal muscles contract, lifting the ribs up and outward. (1 mark)
✓Thoracic (chest cavity) volume increases. (1 mark)
✓Lung pressure decreases below atmospheric, causing air to flow into the lungs. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Always include the cause-and-effect chain: muscle action -> volume change -> pressure change -> air movement. Just naming the muscles loses marks.

Short Answer4 marks
4. Explain how the brain detects and responds to rising CO2 levels in the blood during exercise. Name the brain structures and receptors involved.
✅ Model Answer
✓Central chemoreceptors located in the medulla detect rising CO2 levels in the brain fluid, which indirectly reflects blood CO2. (1 mark)
✓(Peripheral chemoreceptors in the aorta and carotid arteries can also detect falling O2 levels.) (1 mark)
✓The medulla then increases the rate and depth of breathing by increasing the firing rate to the diaphragm and intercostal muscles. (1 mark)
✓This brings in more O2 and expels more CO2 - returning blood gas levels toward normal. The pons also adjusts breathing pattern to keep inhale-exhale transitions smooth. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Link CHEMORECEPTOR to GAS: central -> CO2; peripheral -> O2. Mixing these up is a common mistake.
Extended Response6 marks
A cross-country runner inhales air on a cool morning. Trace what happens to a molecule of oxygen from the moment it enters the runner's nose until it reaches a working leg muscle and is used to produce energy.
5. Describe the complete pathway and key processes. Use the terms: conducting zone, respiratory zone, alveolar/capillary interface, haemoglobin, external respiration, internal respiration, and cellular respiration.
✅ Model Answer
✓Oxygen enters through the nose and passes through the CONDUCTING ZONE: pharynx -> larynx -> trachea -> bronchi -> bronchioles. No gas exchange occurs here. (1 mark)
✓The oxygen then enters the RESPIRATORY ZONE: respiratory bronchioles -> alveolar ducts -> alveoli. Gas exchange now occurs. (1 mark)
✓At the ALVEOLAR/CAPILLARY INTERFACE, oxygen diffuses from areas of high concentration (alveolus) to low concentration (capillary blood). This is EXTERNAL RESPIRATION. (1 mark)
✓The oxygen is picked up by HAEMOGLOBIN in red blood cells and transported via the pulmonary vein -> left side of heart -> aorta -> systemic arteries -> capillaries supplying the leg muscle. (1 mark)
✓At the muscle capillary, oxygen diffuses from blood (high O2) into the muscle cell (low O2) - INTERNAL RESPIRATION. (1 mark)
✓Inside the muscle cell, oxygen is used for CELLULAR RESPIRATION (aerobic breakdown of glucose) to produce ATP for energy. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: A complete chain answer scores all 6 marks. Examiners are looking for the terminology in the right order. Use the keywords explicitly - implying them costs marks.
