Short Answer2 marks
The Murray River Canoe Marathon is held over five days in January in extremely hot conditions. Paddlers must drink fluid during each day's paddling to avoid heat exhaustion.
1. State TWO other actions (not fluid intake) that would help prevent heat exhaustion. (VCAA 2003 Q4di-ii style)
✅ Model Answer
✓Any one strategy: wear lightweight/light-coloured loose-fitting clothing; wear ice vests; wear a hat (e.g. legionnaires); apply wet towels; wet the skin/clothes regularly; acclimatisation; carbo-hydration; or start earlier in the day. (1 mark)
✓A second, DIFFERENT strategy from the list above. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: The two strategies must be different categories — e.g. clothing + timing, or acclimatisation + cooling aid. Two hydration answers earn only one mark.
Short Answer3 marks
2. Name and briefly describe THREE of the four physical mechanisms by which the body loses heat.
✅ Model Answer
✓Radiation: heat passes from blood vessels to the skin and then into the air above the skin (no physical contact required). (1 mark)
✓Evaporation: heat is decreased via the conversion of a liquid to a gas (e.g. sweat evaporating off the skin). (1 mark)
✓Conduction: transfer of thermal energy between two solids in direct contact (e.g. ice pack on neck). OR Convection: heat transferred by the circulation of a fluid or gas over the body (e.g. fan, pouring water). (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Name AND describe. Just listing “radiation, evaporation, conduction” without describing each mechanism would only score partial marks. Include a brief example wherever possible.
Short Answer3 marks
3. Explain how vasodilation and vasoconstriction work together to maintain a stable core temperature in hot conditions.
✅ Model Answer
✓In hot conditions, the hypothalamus detects elevated core temperature via thermoreceptors and triggers a heat-loss response. (1 mark)
✓Blood vessels supplying the skin vasodilate (smooth muscle relaxes) — more warm blood is brought close to the surface, where heat can be lost via radiation and evaporation of sweat. (1 mark)
✓At the same time, vasoconstriction occurs in deeper/non-essential areas, ensuring blood is redirected and the system functions efficiently — this is the simultaneous nature of the vascular shunt that restores core temperature toward ~37 C. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Always link the mechanism (vasodilation/constriction) to the heat-loss method (radiation/evaporation). The hypothalamus and thermoreceptors are key terminology that examiners look for.
Short Answer4 marks
An Australian distance runner is travelling to compete in Doha (extreme heat) in three weeks. Their coach prescribes a heat acclimatisation block.
4. Identify TWO protocol elements of an effective heat acclimatisation program AND TWO physiological adaptations the athlete should develop as a result.
✅ Model Answer
✓Protocol 1 — any one of: train once per day in the first five days; avoid training more than two hours per day; gradually increase training intensity; increase sodium intake; take frequent rest breaks; use cooling stations (drinks, ice towels, fans). (1 mark)
✓Protocol 2 — a second, different protocol element from the list above. (1 mark)
✓Adaptation 1 — any one of: lower exercising heart rate; higher exercising stroke volume; lower exercising core temperature; higher sweat rate; sweating starts at a lower core temperature; lower skin temperature; lower RPE/thermal stress; faster performance in heat. (1 mark)
✓Adaptation 2 — a second, different adaptation from the list above. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Distinguish between protocols (what athletes DO) and adaptations (what the body CHANGES). The marks split evenly. Don't pair “train once a day” with “train shorter sessions” — both are time/volume controls and may earn only one mark.
Extended Response6 marks
A footballer collapses in the final quarter of a game played in 38 C with high humidity. They are conscious but disoriented, their skin is hot, and their pulse is rapid.
5. (a) Identify the most likely stage of hyperthermia and justify your choice from the stimulus. (b) Describe the recommended first aid response. (c) Explain why high humidity makes hyperthermia worse.
✅ Model Answer
✓(a) This is likely heat exhaustion (core temperature 38–40 C). Symptoms in the stimulus match: rapid pulse, hot skin, disorientation but still conscious. (1 mark)
✓(b) Remove the player from the heat source and move to a cool area (shade or indoors). Loosen or remove restrictive clothing. (1 mark)
✓(b cont.) Allow them to sip cool fluids only (small amounts). Sponge the body with cold water and fan to increase heat loss — stop fanning if shivering starts. Apply ice packs to neck, groin and armpits to cool blood returning to the heart. (1 mark)
✓(b cont.) Cool the core temperature as quickly as possible — if condition worsens or core temp climbs further, treat as heat stroke and call for medical help immediately. (1 mark)
✓(c) The body's main cooling mechanism during exercise is evaporation (sweat turning to gas), which removes heat. (1 mark)
✓(c cont.) High humidity means the surrounding air is already saturated with water vapour, limiting evaporation. Sweat remains on the skin, heat is not removed and core temperature continues to climb — making hyperthermia much more likely and severe. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Use the stimulus — quote symptoms to justify your diagnosis. For part (c), explain WHY humidity prevents evaporation rather than just stating it does.
