🌡 3C.1 — Homeostasis, Hyperthermia & Hypothermia ▼

Your body works hardest when its core temperature sits at ~37°C. Vasodilation and vasoconstriction are the body's key tools to keep it there — this is homeostasis in action.

TermDefinitionKey fact
HomeostasisMeans ‘same state'. The process of keeping the internal body environment in a steady state.Temperature, pH, blood glucose etc. all need it.
ThermoregulationThe body's effort to maintain its optimum operating temperature around 37°C.Normal core range: 36.5–37.5°C.
HyperthermiaThe body gains more heat than it loses (core temp rises above normal).Far more common during exercise than hypothermia.
HypothermiaCore temperature falls below the minimum required to maintain basic metabolic functions.Less common in sport but still tested.

💡 Did you know? Most students will experience hyperthermia and heat gain from exercise/sport. That should be the focus — but knowing hypothermia is still required.

📊 3C.2 — The Thermoregulation Loop (Hot vs Cold Response) ▼

When the core temperature deviates either way, the hypothalamus initiates a chain of responses that restores it. Hot → vasodilation & sweating → lose heat. Cold → vasoconstriction & shivering → retain heat. The brain receives input from thermoreceptors in both the hypothalamus (core temperature) and the skin (external temperature).

🔗 How Vasodilation & Vasoconstriction Work (Smooth Muscle & Precapillary Sphincters) ▼
MechanismWhat happens at the vessel wallEffect on blood flow
VasoconstrictionSmooth muscle surrounding the blood vessel contracts (squeezes) — the lumen narrows.Reduces volume of blood flowing through the vessel.
VasodilationSmooth muscle surrounding the blood vessel relaxes — the lumen widens.Allows a greater volume of blood to flow through the vessel.
Precapillary sphinctersA band of smooth muscle that surrounds each branch of the capillary as it leaves the arteriole. Brain opens/closes them.Sphincters open → blood flows into capillary bed. Sphincters closed → blood bypasses (vascular shunt).

💡 Why this matters in thermoregulation: By opening and closing precapillary sphincters, the brain can vary blood flow through capillary beds to muscles or skin. This redistributes blood to (a) increase O2 & fuel supply, (b) increase waste removal, and (c) increase heat loss from muscles and skin.

🔥 3C.3 — Hyperthermia: The Three Stages of Overheating ▼

Hyperthermia occurs when the body is producing or absorbing more heat than it can dissipate. It commonly occurs during:

  • Extended exercise — large amounts of heat produced by working muscles.
  • Low or poor hydration — reduces sweating effectiveness.
  • High external temperatures, especially coupled with high humidity (humidity limits sweat evaporation).
StageCore temperatureSymptoms
Heat cramps37–38 °CCramping, dizziness. A warning to slow down or stop.
Heat exhaustion38–40 °CExcessive sweating, cool & clammy skin, weak but rapid pulse.
Heat stroke> 40 °CSevere disorientation, hot & dry red skin, unconsciousness, shock. Medical emergency.

⚠ Heat stroke is life-threatening. If unconscious, get medical help immediately. Apply ice packs to major arteries (neck, groin, armpits) and place cold wet towels/clothing over the performer to cool blood returning to the heart and reduce core temperature fast.

🩹 First Aid & Treatment of Hyperthermia ▼
StageWhat to do
Heat cramps
  • Remove performer from the heat source; move to a cool area (shade or indoors).
  • Remove or loosen restrictive clothing.
  • Re-hydrate with fluids: water, diluted fruit juice or electrolyte drinks.
  • Eat fresh fruit (e.g. banana) to replenish potassium.
  • Gently stretch the affected muscles.
Heat exhaustion / heat stroke
  • Remove from heat source & move to a cool area.
  • Loosen or remove clothing.
  • Only allow performer to sip fluids (small amounts).
  • Sponge cold water on body + fan to increase heat loss. Stop fanning if shivering starts.
  • If conscious, give cool fluids; if unconscious, start IV fluids promptly (medical).
  • Time is critical: cool core temperature ASAP — any delay increases risk of death.

⚠ Examiner tip: Distinguish between heat cramps (mild — rehydrate, stretch) and heat stroke (emergency — cool the core aggressively). Mentioning ice packs on neck/groin/armpits is a high-quality response.

☀ 3C.4 — Heat Acclimatisation ▼

Heat acclimatisation is the process of becoming accustomed to a new climate (or new conditions) because of gradual adaptations. By living or training in hot conditions and being exposed to heat stress, athletes adapt and become better at regulating their core temperature — resulting in reduced cardiovascular strain and enhanced exercise-heat performance.

Protocol elementWhy
Train/practice once per day in the first five days in a hotter climateAvoid overload while body begins adapting.
Avoid training for more than two hours per dayKeep early sessions short to manage heat stress.
Follow days with multiple sessions with a single-session or rest dayRecovery is essential to adapt, not just push.
Increase training intensity gradually over a few daysProgressive overload tailored to heat.
Increase sodium intake via food or fluids in the first few daysSweating loses sodium; replacement keeps you hydrated & functional.
Keep cooling stations stocked with drinks, ice towels and fansExternal heat-loss aids during practice.
Take frequent rest breaksAllow body to cool between bouts.
📊 Heat Acclimatisation Adaptations ▼

After ~5–14 days of heat exposure, the body shows measurable adaptations across heart, core, sweat, skin, perception and performance — all of which let the athlete perform better in heat.

🌊 3C.5 — Four Ways to Lose Heat ▼

The body dissipates heat using four physical mechanisms. Most exam questions focus on radiation and evaporation — these are the primary methods during exercise.

MethodHow it worksSport example
RadiationHeat passes from the blood vessels to the skin and into the air above the skin (no contact needed).Heat radiates off a runner's skin into surrounding air on a cool day.
EvaporationHeat is decreased via the conversion of a liquid to a gas. Sweat evaporating off the skin is the body's key cooling method.A marathon runner's sweat evaporates — cooling the body. High humidity blocks this!
ConductionTransfer of thermal energy between two solids in direct contact.A tennis player drapes an ice towel over their shoulders during the change of ends.
ConvectionHeat transferred by the circulation of a fluid (e.g. water) or gas (e.g. air) over the body.Pouring water over yourself, or standing in front of a fan; wind cooling a cyclist.

💡 Exam trap: Pouring water over yourself = convection, not conduction. The water flows over your body. Wet towels on your shoulders = conduction (solid-to-solid contact via the wet cloth).

📊 Visual: Four Heat-Loss Mechanisms in Action ▼

In real sport, multiple mechanisms operate at once. A cyclist on a hot day uses radiation + convection (wind) + evaporation (sweat) at the same time, sometimes assisted by conduction (ice towel at rest stop).

📝 Worked Example — VCAA 2003 Q4di-ii (2 marks) ▼

The Murray River Canoe Marathon is an event held in January which lasts five days. The participants usually paddle for many hours each day. The event is usually conducted in extremely hot conditions. In past events some paddlers have suffered heat exhaustion. The paddler should drink fluid during each day's paddling to help avoid heat exhaustion. State two other actions that would help prevent heat exhaustion.

Acceptable answers (any TWO)

  • Wear lightweight/light-coloured loose fitting clothing
  • Wear ice vests
  • Wear a hat (e.g. legionnaires)
  • Apply wet towels
  • Wet the skin/clothes regularly
  • Acclimatisation
  • Carbo-hydration (carbohydrate + hydration)
  • Starting earlier in the day (to avoid peak heat)
Marking guideMarks
State one strategy that would reduce likelihood of heat exhaustion occurring1
State a second (different) strategy that would reduce likelihood of heat exhaustion occurring1

⚠ Examiner tip: The two strategies must be different — “drink water” and “drink electrolyte fluids” are both hydration and would only earn one mark. Pick two from different categories (e.g. clothing + timing, or acclimatisation + cooling aids).

📝 Summary — The Big Picture ▼

Vasodilation & vasoconstriction

  • Vasodilation: smooth muscle relaxes → widening of blood vessels → increased blood flow.
  • Vasoconstriction: smooth muscle contracts → narrowing of blood vessels → reduced blood flow.
  • Both processes occur simultaneously and assist with thermoregulation.

Hyperthermia and effects of overheating

  • Hyperthermia: body ‘heats up' and core temp rises → potential performance drops.
  • Heat cramps (+1°) → heat exhaustion (+2°) → heat stroke (+3° / medical emergency).
  • Heat acclimatisation: lower HR, lower core temp, higher sweat rate, lower skin temp, sweating starts sooner.

Four ways to lose heat

  • Radiation: blood flow to skin surface; heat into air.
  • Evaporation: sweat (slows at high core temps to conserve water).
  • Conduction: surface-to-surface contact (hotter → cooler).
  • Convection: flow of water/air over skin.
