Short Answer3 marks
1. Describe how the respiratory system and cardiovascular system work together to take up and transport oxygen to working muscles during exercise.
✅ Model Answer
✓The respiratory system takes up oxygen from the air, drawing it into the lungs where gaseous exchange occurs at the alveolar/capillary interface. (1 mark)
✓Oxygen diffuses into the blood, binds to haemoglobin and is pumped by the heart through the systemic circuit to the working muscles. (1 mark)
✓At the muscle capillaries, oxygen diffuses into the muscle for energy production; CO2 diffuses out and is transported back to the lungs to be exhaled - completing the cycle. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Use the terms cardiorespiratory, pulmonary, systemic and gaseous exchange explicitly. Show the chain from air -> alveoli -> blood -> heart -> muscles.
Short Answer2 marks
2. Define steady state and explain why it is achieved at rest.
✅ Model Answer
✓Steady state is when the body can match oxygen supply to oxygen demand. (1 mark)
✓At rest, the body's oxygen demand is LOW and is easily met by the cardiorespiratory systems - HR and RR are low and in sync. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Steady state happens at rest AND during low-to-moderate intensity exercise (not only at rest).
Short Answer3 marks
3. Describe what is meant by the a-vO2 difference and explain how it changes from rest to maximal exercise. Use values from the textbook in your answer.
✅ Model Answer
✓The a-vO2 difference (arteriovenous oxygen difference) is the difference in oxygen content between arterial blood entering the muscle and venous blood leaving it - it measures how much O2 the muscles have extracted. (1 mark)
✓At rest, the a-vO2 difference is approximately 6 mL O2 per 100 mL of blood (18 mL arterial - 12 mL venous). (1 mark)
✓At maximal exercise it widens to approximately 15 mL O2 per 100 mL blood (18 mL arterial - 3 mL venous) - because working muscles extract much more oxygen. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Quote the exact numbers from the textbook (6 mL at rest, 15 mL at max). The widening of a-vO2 diff is a key way the body delivers more O2 to muscles during exercise.
Short Answer4 marks
A runner finishes a 5 km time trial. Their coach insists they jog for 10 minutes at low intensity rather than sit down on the grass.
4. Identify what type of recovery the coach has prescribed and explain THREE physiological benefits of this approach compared to a passive recovery.
✅ Model Answer
✓The coach has prescribed an ACTIVE recovery - continuing the activity at a reduced intensity. (1 mark)
✓Benefit 1: Oxygen consumption (EPOC) remains above resting levels for longer, allowing greater oxidation of accumulated metabolic by-products like H+. (1 mark)
✓Benefit 2: The muscular/vascular pump from continued muscle contractions promotes venous return to the heart and prevents venous pooling in the legs. (1 mark)
✓Benefit 3: Continued blood flow assists with thermoregulation - heat continues to be transported to the skin to be dissipated. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Three distinct benefits earn three marks. Link H+ oxidation, venous return AND thermoregulation. Don't repeat the same idea differently.
Extended Response6 marks
5. Describe the cardiorespiratory response to a single bout of moderate-intensity exercise from rest, through exercise, to recovery. Explain how heart rate, respiratory rate, stroke volume and tidal volume change at each stage, and what happens to EPOC after the bout. Reference both the direct linear relationship and finite capacity in your answer.
✅ Model Answer
✓At rest: HR, RR, SV and TV are at low resting levels. The body is in steady state - oxygen supply matches oxygen demand. (1 mark)
✓At exercise onset: HR and RR rise rapidly in a direct linear relationship with exercise intensity, mirroring each other as the demand for O2 increases. (1 mark)
✓Stroke volume and tidal volume also rise, but only up to their finite capacity (around 40-50% maximum effort), then they plateau. (1 mark)
✓During steady-state moderate exercise, HR and RR level off at an elevated rate as the body matches O2 supply to the new (higher) demand. (1 mark)
✓At exercise off-set (recovery): HR and RR fall but remain above resting levels for some time - this elevated O2 consumption is EPOC (Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption). (1 mark)
✓EPOC supplies the extra oxygen needed to oxidise accumulated metabolic by-products (H+), replenish energy stores and return the body to homeostasis. An active recovery extends EPOC; a passive recovery shortens it. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Walk through ALL stages - rest, onset, steady state, off-set, EPOC. Use the keywords direct linear relationship and finite capacity explicitly. Mention what EPOC is used for, not just that it exists.
