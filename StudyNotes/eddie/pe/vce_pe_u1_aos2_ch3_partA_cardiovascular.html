📚 Chapter contents
❤ 3A.1 — Overview ▼

Three components: heart (pump), blood (transport medium), blood vessels (network). Together they deliver what muscles need and remove what they produce.

What it deliversWhat it removesOther roles
Oxygen to musclesCarbon dioxide to lungsThermoregulation (heat via skin blood flow)
Glucose & fatty acids (fuel)Lactic acid to liverHormone transport (e.g. adrenaline)

💡 At rest: ~5 L/min pumped. At maximal exercise: up to 40 L/min in elite athletes.

❤ 3A.2 — Heart Structure ▼

The heart is a double pump. Right side (pink) = pulmonary circuit to lungs. Left side (blue) = systemic circuit to body. The left ventricle has a thicker wall because it pumps against higher pressure. The SA node (orange dot) generates each heartbeat.

♥ Valves & Conduction — Quick Reference ▼
StructureLocation / role
Tricuspid valveRight atrium → right ventricle. Prevents backflow.
Bicuspid (mitral) valveLeft atrium → left ventricle. Prevents backflow.
Pulmonary & aortic semilunar valvesExit valves at the top of each ventricle. Stop blood flowing back from arteries.
SA nodePacemaker in right atrium wall — fires the electrical impulse for each beat (~70 bpm at rest).
AV nodeDelays signal so atria finish contracting before ventricles fire.
Bundle of His + Purkinje fibresCarry signal down the septum and spread it through ventricular walls for simultaneous contraction.

💡 The heart is myogenic — it generates its own electrical impulse without needing the brain. The autonomic nervous system can speed it up (sympathetic) or slow it down (parasympathetic), but cannot initiate the beat.

🔄 3A.3 — The Cardiac Cycle ▼

One heartbeat = one complete cardiac cycle. At higher heart rates, diastole shortens most — the filling time reduces. Elite athletes compensate with larger ventricles.

📉 Key Cardiac Terms ▼
TermWhat it meansTypical value (rest)
Heart rate (HR)Beats per minute60–100 bpm; elite athletes 40–60 bpm
Stroke volume (SV)Blood ejected by the left ventricle per beat (= EDV − ESV)~70 mL
Cardiac output (Q)Blood pumped by the left ventricle per minute = HR × SV~5 L/min
EDV (end-diastolic volume)Blood in ventricle at end of filling~120–130 mL
ESV (end-systolic volume)Blood remaining after contraction~50–60 mL

💡 SV = EDV − ESV. The more blood that fills the ventricle, the more that gets ejected.

📈 3A.4 — Cardiac Output ▼

Key point: SV plateaus at ~40–60% VO2max. Above that, only HR keeps rising to increase Q. Elite athletes have higher SVs due to larger heart chambers (cardiac hypertrophy).

⚡ What Controls Heart Rate? ▼
MechanismEffectWhen
Sympathetic NS↑ HR (releases noradrenaline)Exercise, stress
Parasympathetic NS (vagus)↓ HR — dominant at restRest, recovery
Adrenaline↑ HR & contractilityAnticipation + exercise onset
ProprioceptorsDetect muscle movement → signal to increase HR immediatelyExercise onset
ChemoreceptorsDetect rising CO2 / falling pH → ↑ HRSustained exercise
TemperatureRising blood temperature → ↑ HRProlonged exercise

💡 Anticipatory rise = HR increases BEFORE exercise starts, driven by adrenaline and the brain. This is a feed-forward response — muscles haven't moved yet.

📈 What Affects Stroke Volume? ▼
FactorHow it worksEffect on SV
Preload (venous return)More blood filling the ventricle stretches the walls → stronger contraction (Frank-Starling Law)↑
ContractilityAdrenaline increases the force of each contraction↑
AfterloadResistance the ventricle pumps against — high afterload reduces ejection↓

💡 Frank-Starling Law: The more the heart fills (↑ EDV), the harder it contracts → ↑ SV. During exercise, the skeletal muscle pump increases venous return → ↑ EDV → ↑ SV.

🔵 3A.5 — Blood Vessels ▼

Arteries have thick elastic walls to handle high pressure. Capillaries are one cell thick to allow gas exchange. Veins have a large lumen and valves to return blood at low pressure.

📅 How Does Blood Return to the Heart? (Venous Return) ▼
MechanismHow it works
Skeletal muscle pumpContracting muscles squeeze veins, pushing blood toward the heart. Valves prevent backflow. Most important during exercise.
Respiratory pumpDeep inhaling creates negative chest pressure, drawing blood into the right atrium. Stronger during exercise (deeper breaths).
Venous valvesOne-way valves stop blood pooling in limbs — critical when standing upright.
VenoconstrictionSympathetic NS constricts veins, reducing their capacity and pushing blood toward the heart.

💡 More venous return → more preload → Frank-Starling effect → higher SV. Venous return and cardiac output are always equal in steady state.

📈 3A.6 — Blood Pressure ▼
TermMeaningNormal / Exercise value
Systolic BPPressure during ventricular contraction (top number)~120 mmHg rest; up to ~200 mmHg at max exercise
Diastolic BPPressure during relaxation (bottom number)~80 mmHg rest; stays stable or slightly ↓ during aerobic exercise
HypertensionChronically high BP (>140/90 mmHg) — major cardiac risk factorManaged with aerobic exercise
Post-exercise hypotensionBP falls BELOW resting level for hours after aerobic exerciseClinically useful — one reason exercise is prescribed for hypertension

💡 During aerobic exercise: SBP rises, DBP stays stable (vasodilation reduces resistance). During heavy resistance exercise: BOTH SBP and DBP rise sharply (Valsalva manoeuvre + muscle compression of vessels).
