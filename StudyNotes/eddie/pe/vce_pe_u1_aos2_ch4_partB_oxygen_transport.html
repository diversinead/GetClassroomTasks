🟪 4B.1 — Take Up & Transport of Oxygen ▼

The respiratory and cardiovascular systems work as a single team: the respiratory system takes UP oxygen from the air, and the cardiovascular system TRANSPORTS it around the body to the working muscles.

TermWhat it means
CardioReferring to the heart itself.
CardiopulmonaryHeart and lungs.
CardiovascularHeart and blood vessels.
CardiorespiratoryHeart and respiratory system — the term that captures both systems working together.
VascularAll blood vessels (arteries, arterioles, capillaries, venules, veins) through which blood, fuels and wastes are transported.

💡 In VCAA exams the system is referred to as the cardiorespiratory system — the cardiovascular and respiratory systems working as one unit.

💑 4B.2 — Pulmonary & Systemic Circuits ▼

Blood flows through two circuits: the pulmonary circuit (heart ↔ lungs) where blood picks up O2, and the systemic circuit (heart ↔ body) where O2 is delivered to tissues. Each loop starts and ends at the heart.

Blood is constantly cycling through BOTH circuits. The right side of the heart handles the pulmonary circuit (deoxygenated blood out to lungs, oxygenated blood back). The left side handles the systemic circuit (oxygenated blood out to body, deoxygenated blood back).

📑 Pulmonary vs Systemic Circuit — Side-by-Side ▼
CircuitPathPurpose
PulmonaryRight side of heart → pulmonary artery → lungs → pulmonary vein → left side of heartBlood picks up O2 from the alveoli; releases CO2 to be exhaled.
SystemicLeft side of heart → aorta → arteries → arterioles → capillaries → venules → veins → vena cava → right side of heartOxygenated blood delivers O2 to tissues; deoxygenated blood collects CO2 and returns to heart.

💡 Exam tip: O2 concentration is HIGHEST in the capillaries surrounding the alveoli (just after gas exchange in the lungs) and the aorta. It is LOWEST in the veins coming back from working muscles.

📈 4B.3 — a-vO₂ Difference (How Much Oxygen Muscles Extract) ▼

As blood flows from artery → capillary → vein, the working tissues extract oxygen. The difference between O2 entering (arterial) and O2 leaving (venous) is called the arteriovenous oxygen difference (a-vO2 diff). At rest only a small amount is extracted; during maximal exercise muscles extract much more.

At rest, the body extracts only about 6 mL of O2 per 100 mL of blood. During maximal exercise, working muscles extract about 15 mL per 100 mL — nearly all the oxygen in arterial blood. This widening of the a-vO2 difference is a key way the body increases oxygen delivery during exercise.

😎 4B.4 — Oxygen Consumption at Rest (Steady State) ▼

At rest, the body's demand for oxygen is LOW. The cardiovascular and respiratory systems comfortably supply more than enough oxygen — this balanced state is called steady state.

  • Steady state = when the body can match oxygen supply to oxygen demand. Occurs at rest and during low–moderate intensity exercise.
  • Low respiratory rates are paired with low heart rates — the respiratory system and cardiovascular system work in sync.
  • The quicker the body can supply oxygen to working muscles, the quicker steady state can be achieved.

💡 Did you know? People who undertake aerobic training achieve steady state EARLIER and have lower resting heart rates — their oxygen delivery system is more efficient.

📊 4B.5 — Direct Linear Relationship: Intensity & HR/RR ▼

There is a direct linear relationship between exercise intensity and both heart rate (HR) and respiratory rate (RR). As intensity rises, both rise together — and as intensity drops, both fall.

When intensity rises, HR and RR rise together in a direct linear way. When intensity is steady, they level off (steady state). When intensity drops or exercise stops, they fall together. Both systems mirror exercise intensity.

⚡ Recall: Q = SV × HR / VE = TV × RR ▼

HR and RR aren't the only variables that change with intensity. Stroke volume (SV) and tidal volume (TV) also increase — but only up to their finite capacity, which is reached around 40–50% maximum effort. After that, only HR and RR keep rising.

📉 4B.6 — EPOC: Excess Post-Exercise Oxygen Consumption ▼

EPOC stands for Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption. After you stop exercising, oxygen consumption stays above resting levels for a while — that elevated portion is EPOC. The body uses it to clear metabolic by-products (like H+), restock energy stores and return to homeostasis.

After exercise, oxygen consumption falls back toward resting levels. The area ABOVE resting and UNDER the post-exercise curve is the EPOC. Active recovery (continuing low-intensity exercise) produces a larger EPOC than passive recovery (just stopping) — oxygen consumption remains above resting for longer.

🏃 Active vs Passive Recovery ▼
Recovery typeWhat it isEffect on EPOC
Passive recoveryStopping all activity and resting (sitting, lying down) once exercise ceases.SMALLER EPOC. Cardiovascular & respiratory systems return to pre-exercise levels relatively quickly.
Active recoveryThe same activity continued at a reduced intensity once exercise/training has ceased (e.g. easy jog after a hard run).LARGER EPOC. HR, RR and ventilation remain above resting for LONGER — helps oxidise accumulated metabolic by-products.

Active recovery requires both HR and SV to remain above resting levels for longer than passive, because the cardiovascular system must:

  • Transport larger amounts of oxygen to working muscles to oxidise by-products.
  • Promote vascular return to the heart and prevent venous pooling (the muscular/vascular pump keeps blood flowing).
  • Assist with thermoregulation as the body cools down.

💡 Teacher's tip: Link the extra oxygen consumed during active recovery to increased oxidation of accumulated metabolic by-products (H+). With muscle contractions still happening, the muscular pump and cardio pump work together — blood returns faster, more oxygen is delivered, and recovery accelerates.

📝 Summary — The Big Picture ▼

Take up and transport of oxygen

  • The respiratory system takes UP oxygen; the cardiovascular system TRANSPORTS it.
  • Pulmonary circuit: heart ↔ lungs (gas exchange). Systemic circuit: heart ↔ body (O2 delivery, CO2 collection).
  • a-vO2 difference: 6 mL/100 mL at rest vs 15 mL/100 mL at max exercise.

Oxygen consumption at rest

  • Low and easily met by cardiorespiratory systems.
  • Steady state: oxygen supply equals oxygen demand.

Direct linear relationship

  • HR and RR rise linearly with exercise intensity, right up to VO2 max.
  • SV and TV have finite capacities (plateau at ~40–50% max).

EPOC and recovery

  • EPOC = oxygen consumed ABOVE resting levels after exercise.
  • Active recovery → larger EPOC, faster oxidation of by-products like H+.
  • Active recovery uses the muscle pump to keep blood returning to the heart.
