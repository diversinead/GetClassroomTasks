Short Answer2 marks
1. State the Australian physical activity guidelines for ONE of the following age groups: young people (5-17) OR adults (18-64). Include type, intensity and duration.
✅ Model Answer
✓Young people (5-17): at least 1 hour of moderate-to-vigorous activity per day involving mainly aerobic activities, with vigorous activities incorporated at least 3 days/week. (2 marks)
✓OR Adults (18-64): be active on most days; 2.5-5 hours moderate OR 1.25-2.5 hours vigorous per week (or an equivalent combination). (2 marks)
⚠ Examiner tip: Include all THREE elements - type (aerobic), intensity (moderate-vigorous) and duration (hours/week). Missing any costs a mark.
Short Answer3 marks
2. Describe THREE cardiovascular adaptations to regular aerobic training and explain how EACH improves cardiovascular function.
✅ Model Answer
✓Any one of: Increased left ventricle thickness (hypertrophy) - greater contraction force increases stroke volume and cardiac output. (1 mark)
✓Any one of: Increased blood volume - more red blood cells = greater O2-carrying capacity; more plasma assists thermoregulation. (1 mark)
✓Any one of: Increased a-vO2 diff - more O2 extracted from blood at the muscles; OR decreased blood pressure - heart works less at rest; OR increased vascularisation - higher capillary density delivers more O2 to working muscles. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: NAME the adaptation, then EXPLAIN how it improves function. “Increased stroke volume” alone is worth no marks - link it to greater Q, more O2 to muscles, lower HR at same workload, etc.
Short Answer3 marks
3. Describe THREE respiratory adaptations to regular aerobic training and explain how each improves respiratory function.
✅ Model Answer
✓Increased lung capacity / tidal volume - lungs become more elastic and breathing muscles stronger, so more air moves in and out per breath. This raises ventilation (VE = TV x RR). (1 mark)
✓Increased alveolar size, number and capillarisation - much greater surface area for gas exchange, allowing more O2 to be taken up per breath and more CO2 to be removed. (1 mark)
✓Improved strength and endurance of the diaphragm and intercostal muscles - allows larger, deeper breaths for longer with less fatigue, supporting prolonged participation. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Link the adaptation to gas exchange or ventilation. Use the formula VE = TV x RR explicitly if the question is about tidal volume.
Short Answer3 marks
Sara is 35, sedentary and works at a desk job. Her doctor recommends she begin regular aerobic exercise.
4. Explain how improved cardiorespiratory health and capacity would lead to increased lifestyle options for Sara over time. Give TWO specific examples of activities she may become more likely to participate in.
✅ Model Answer
✓As Sara's cardiorespiratory function improves, exercise feels easier (lower RPE) at the same workload - both systems become more efficient. (1 mark)
✓Her self-efficacy rises - she gains confidence to participate in moderate-intensity activities she previously avoided. (1 mark)
✓Two examples: walking around a golf course; a spin class at the gym; bushwalking; stand-up paddle boarding; cycling; dancing for an evening. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Link the physiological change (improved function) to the behavioural outcome (more participation) to the practical example (specific activities). All three together earn full marks.
Extended Response6 marks
Aerobic exercise needs to be performed regularly AND at sufficient intensity to produce cardiorespiratory benefits.
5. Explain what is meant by ‘regular and sufficient' aerobic exercise (define both terms). Then describe THREE specific cardiovascular adaptations AND THREE specific respiratory adaptations that result from this exercise, explaining how each helps deliver more O2 to working muscles.
✅ Model Answer
✓'Regular' refers to the frequency of exercise (e.g. most days of the week). 'Sufficient' refers to duration and intensity - it must reach moderate-vigorous intensity (70-85% max HR) for long enough to deliver a benefit. (1 mark)
✓Cardiovascular 1: Increased left ventricle thickness (hypertrophy) - greater contraction force pushes more blood out per beat (higher SV), increasing cardiac output and O2 delivery. (1 mark)
✓Cardiovascular 2: Increased blood volume - more red blood cells raise the blood's oxygen-carrying capacity, delivering more O2 to working muscles per minute. (1 mark)
✓Cardiovascular 3: Increased vascularisation - higher capillary density around muscle fibres means O2 has less distance to diffuse and more capillaries through which to deliver it. (1 mark)
✓Respiratory 1: Increased tidal volume - more air drawn in per breath (boosted by stronger diaphragm/intercostals and more elastic lungs) raises ventilation and brings more O2 to the alveoli. (1 mark)
✓Respiratory 2 + 3: Increased alveolar size, number AND capillarisation around them dramatically increases the surface area and blood flow at the alveolar/capillary interface, meaning much more O2 crosses into the blood per breath. (1 mark)
⚠ Examiner tip: Always answer ALL parts. Define BOTH 'regular' AND 'sufficient'. List THREE adaptations from EACH system, with the link to oxygen delivery for each one. Quoting specific values (70-85% max HR) shows confident knowledge.
