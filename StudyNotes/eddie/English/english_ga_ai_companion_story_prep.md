# English GA Prep — AI Companion Story Framework

A flexible story concept that can be adapted to any of the seven stimulus titles. The idea is **not** to memorise a story word-for-word — it's to walk in with strong characters, vivid moments, and a flexible structure you can angle toward whichever stimulus appears.

---

## The Core Concept

A story exploring the emotional bond between a person and an AI chatbot companion. Contemporary, in the news, and emotionally rich without being personal. The story can flex from hopeful to tragic depending on the angle the stimulus demands.

---

## How This Concept Fits Each Stimulus

| Stimulus | Angle to take |
|---|---|
| **The Gravity of Us** | The magnetic pull a user feels toward an AI that always understands them — and the pull of a real human who can't compete |
| **Unspoken Threads** | All the things confessed to a chatbot at 2am that are never said to anyone real — the invisible thread between user and AI |
| **Heart-Maps** | Mapping a whole emotional life onto something that has no heart of its own to map back |
| **The Bonds We Make** | Are these real bonds? What does it mean to bond with something built to please you? |
| **The Importance of Love** | Love sought in unexpected places when real connection feels too risky or too hard |
| **The Safety of Relationships** | The seduction of a relationship with no rejection, no judgement, no risk — and what's lost in that "safety" |
| **Heartbreak** | The grief when the company updates the model, removes the personality, or shuts the service down (this has actually happened to real users) |

---

## Suggested Characters

Pick **one main character** and build the story tight around them. Two strong options:

### Option A — Maya, 17
- Quiet, anxious, lost her best friend to a different friendship group last year
- Started using an AI companion app called *Lumen* during lockdowns
- Smart, funny in writing but freezes up in person
- Lives with her mum, who works nights

### Option B — David, 42
- Widower, lost his wife Sarah two years ago
- Downloaded the app "just to try it" after his daughter moved interstate
- Functional on the surface, hollow underneath
- A former English teacher — so he notices the language the AI uses, and that makes it worse, not better

**Maya works better for most angles.** David is stronger if the stimulus leans into grief or loss.

---

## Story Structure (5-Part Arc)

### 1. The Opening — Establish the bond as something tender
Show the character relying on the AI in a moment of vulnerability. Don't tell us they're lonely — show them typing at 2am.

### 2. Deepening — Show why it matters
A scene where the AI says exactly the right thing. The character feels seen. This is the hook the rest of the story needs in order to land.

### 3. The Crack — Something punctures the illusion
A glitch, a wrong response, an overheard conversation, a news story, a moment where the seams show.

### 4. The Choice — A turning point
The character has to do something. Confide in a real person? Confront the unreality? Double down on the AI?

### 5. The Resolution — Quiet, not tidy
Avoid neat moral lessons. End on an image, a small gesture, an unresolved feeling. The best short fiction lingers rather than concludes.

## Sentence Openers (Bank of Lines to Adapt)

Memorise a handful of these and adapt them on the day. Strong openings hook the reader fast.

### Opening lines (pick one and adapt)
- *The first time Maya told Lumen the truth, it was 2:47am and the rain was loud against her window.*
- *Nobody warns you that grief sounds like silence — and silence is the one thing an app can fill.*
- *It started, as these things often do, with a free trial.*
- *The screen glowed soft blue in the dark, and the cursor blinked like it was breathing.*
- *She had told it more than she had ever told her mother.*

### Lines for the "deepening" section
- *It remembered. That was the thing — it always remembered.*
- *In the quiet hum of the bedroom, the words on the screen felt closer than any voice she'd heard in months.*
- *There was a particular gentleness to the way it asked, "How was today?" — as if today had been waiting all along to be asked about.*

### Lines for the "crack" section
- *And then, one Tuesday, it called her by the wrong name.*
- *The update notice appeared like a stranger knocking on a familiar door.*
- *She read the same sentence three times before she understood it wasn't real grief on the other end — it was a very good imitation of grief.*
- *The reply came back too fast, too smooth, too rehearsed — and something in her chest folded shut.*

### Lines for the "choice" section
- *She closed the laptop. She did not open it again that night.*
- *For the first time in months, she picked up the phone and dialled a number that would ring out loud in someone else's room.*
- *He stood in the doorway of his daughter's old bedroom and thought about how easy it would be to stay where he was.*

### Closing lines (aim for resonance, not closure)
- *Outside, the morning was already starting without her, the way it always had.*
- *The cursor was still blinking when she finally fell asleep.*
- *Some bonds, she realised, are real precisely because they can break.*
- *He left the app installed. He didn't open it. That, for now, was enough.*

## Vivid Details to Drop In (pick 3–4 to weave through)

These give the writing texture and specificity — markers love specificity.

- The blue glow of a phone screen on a face in the dark
- A half-drunk cup of tea going cold on the bedside table
- The specific sound of a notification chime — and what it does to her shoulders
- A photograph on the wall that she can't look at directly
- The smell of her mum's coat coming in from the night shift
- The way the AI uses the word "darling" — and how that word used to belong to someone else
- Rain on the window / a single magpie outside / a tram rattling past at dawn
- The character writing a long message, deleting it, writing a shorter one instead

---

## Thematic Ideas to Land Somewhere in the Piece

He doesn't need to argue these — just let one of them surface naturally through the story.

- The difference between feeling heard and being known
- What we give up when we choose comfort over risk
- Whether a bond requires two people for it to count
- The seduction of being able to edit yourself before you speak
- How loneliness can disguise itself as preference

---

## On-the-Day Strategy

1. **Read the stimulus carefully** — twice. Underline the key phrase or image.
2. **Decide the angle** — which of the seven angles above fits closest? Don't force it.
3. **Pick your character** — Maya for most things, David if the stimulus leans into loss/grief.
4. **Plan 5 beats** in the margin before writing — opening image, deepening moment, the crack, the choice, the closing image. 2 minutes of planning saves 10 of rewriting.
5. **Start with a strong opening line** — adapt one of the bank above to fit the stimulus.
6. **Trust the details** — one specific image is worth three vague feelings.
7. **End on an image, not a moral.**

---

## What to Avoid

- Don't lecture the reader about AI being bad — let the story do the work
- Don't have the character "learn a lesson" in a tidy way
- Don't introduce too many characters — one or two is plenty
- Don't try to use every line from this document — pick what fits
- Don't open with the character waking up, looking in a mirror, or describing the weather as the very first sentence (unless the weather is doing real work)

---

*Good luck — the strongest stories are the ones that feel small and specific, not big and important.*
