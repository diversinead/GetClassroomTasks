# LESSON 1 — Operations management and business objectives
*Read: Chapter 3.1 and 3.2*

## 1. Operations and operations management

**Operations** = the day-to-day activities that turn **inputs** (resources) into **outputs** (the goods or services sold). Every business has operations of some kind.

**Operations management (OM)** = planning, organising, leading and controlling those activities so the business uses its resources well and meets its objectives.

In short:
- **Operations** = the activities themselves.
- **Operations management** = overseeing those activities to make them work as well as possible.

## 2. Four key concepts

| Concept | Definition | Plain English |
|---|---|---|
| **Effectiveness** | How well the business achieves its objectives. | "Doing the **right things**." |
| **Efficiency** | How well the business uses its resources. | "Doing things **right**." |
| **Productivity** | Outputs produced ÷ inputs used (e.g. units per labour-hour). | More output for the same inputs = higher productivity. |
| **Business competitiveness** | The ability to outperform rivals on price, quality, speed, service or innovation. | Why customers choose you over competitors. |

**How they connect:** Better OM raises productivity → lowers cost per unit (efficiency) → enables better pricing (competitiveness) → while still meeting customer expectations (effectiveness).

## 3. Relationship between OM and business objectives

Businesses set measurable goals — profit, market share, productivity, customer satisfaction, CSR. Operations is where products are made and where most costs occur, so OM directly affects whether these objectives are achieved.

| Business objective | OM contribution | Example strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Increase profit | Cuts input costs and waste | Just-in-time inventory, lean management |
| Increase market share | Better quality and faster delivery | TQM, automated production |
| Improve productivity | More output from the same inputs | Robotics, AI scheduling, Kaizen |
| Fulfil CSR commitments | Reduces environmental and social impact | Ethical sourcing, sustainable packaging |
| Increase shareholder value | Higher efficiency lifts profit and share price | Lean management + quality assurance |

## 4. APPLY — Australia Post case study (Jacaranda pg. 219)

**Objective:** Increase productivity and reduce parcel-handling costs.

**OM actions:** Built automated sorting facilities (Chullora, Redbank) using barcode scanners, conveyors and robotics to sort 50,000+ parcels/hour. Added route-optimisation software and real-time tracking.

**Outcome:**
- **Productivity** ↑ — same workforce processes more parcels per shift.
- **Efficiency** ↑ — lower cost per parcel.
- **Effectiveness** ↑ — faster, trackable delivery for customers.
- **Competitiveness** maintained against StarTrack and Amazon Logistics.

## 5. Why this relationship matters

1. **Operations is where strategy meets reality.** A great plan fails if operations can't deliver.
2. **Operations consumes most of the business's resources**, so inefficiency directly erodes profit.
3. **Operations creates the customer experience.** Quality issues damage market share and reputation.
4. **Poor OM undermines good objectives** — and great OM (e.g. Toyota's lean system) can become a competitive advantage in itself.

**Cause-and-effect chain:**
> *OM strategy → operational change → improved efficiency/quality/productivity → contributes to business objective → improved business performance.*

## 6. PRACTICE — Sample exam responses

### Q. Describe the relationship between operations management and business objectives. (4 marks)

> Operations management is the planning, organising, leading and controlling of the activities that transform inputs into goods and services. Business objectives are measurable goals such as profit, market share or CSR commitments.
>
> Because operations is where most costs occur and where products are created, OM decisions directly affect whether objectives are met. For example, lean management lowers production costs and supports the profit objective, while quality assurance improves customer satisfaction and supports market share.
>
> Effective OM converts strategic objectives into day-to-day actions that deliver value, while inefficient OM undermines even a well-designed strategy.

# LESSON 2 — Manufacturing vs service businesses
*Read: Chapter 3.4 (first half)*

## 1. The two business types

**Manufacturing business:** Transforms raw materials into **tangible** finished goods. Production happens in a factory, customers usually aren't present, goods can be made in advance and stored. Typically **capital intensive** (relies on machinery and tech).
- *Examples:* Toyota Australia, Bega Cheese, BlueScope Steel, Boeing Australia, ChocYum.

**Service business:** Provides **intangible** activities, expertise or experiences. The service is produced and consumed at the same time, the customer is often present, and it can't be stored. Typically **labour intensive** (relies on staff skills).
- *Examples:* Commonwealth Bank, Qantas, Medibank, KEJ's Cafe, WCTY Cleaning Services.

## 2. Comparing the two

| Characteristic | Manufacturing | Service |
|---|---|---|
| **Output** | Tangible — can be stored | Intangible — an experience or activity |
| **Customer contact** | Low | High |
| **Production timing** | Made in advance, then sold | Produced and consumed simultaneously |
| **Inventory** | Significant | Minimal |
| **Resource intensity** | Capital intensive | Labour intensive |
| **Quality measurement** | Easier — measurable specs | Harder — relies on customer perception |
| **Customisation** | Standardised | Often customised |
| **Location** | Centralised factories | Decentralised, near customer |

**Similarities** (often missed):
- Both transform inputs into outputs through a managed system.
- Both use OM to balance efficiency and effectiveness.
- Both pursue the same business objectives (profit, productivity, market share, CSR).
- Both can apply OM strategies like quality management and lean principles.

## 3. Sample short-answer responses

### Q. Outline two characteristics of a manufacturing business.

> 1. **Tangible outputs:** Produces physical goods that can be stored before sale (e.g. ChocYum's chocolate bars).
> 2. **Capital intensive:** Relies heavily on machinery and automation rather than labour.

### Q. Explain the characteristics of a service business.

> A service business produces **intangible outputs** — activities, expertise or experiences (such as banking or cleaning). Because services can't be stored, they are **produced and consumed at the same time**, so **customer contact is typically high**. Service operations are usually **labour intensive**, depending on staff skill and interaction quality. Quality is harder to measure objectively, so service businesses rely on customer feedback. Delivery is usually **decentralised**, happening near the customer.

### Q. Compare a manufacturing and service business (3 characteristics).

> A manufacturing business produces **tangible** goods that can be stored, whereas a service business produces **intangible** outputs that can't be stored. Both transform inputs into outputs through a managed operations system.
>
> **Customer contact** is low in manufacturing (customers rarely enter the production area) but high in services (e.g. KEJ's Cafe customers are present while their meals are prepared).
>
> **Resource intensity** differs too — manufacturing is **capital intensive** (machinery, automation), while services are **labour intensive** (staff skills and effort).

## 4. PRACTICE — Sample exam responses

### Q. 2022 VCAA Section A Q3(a) — KEJ's Cafe service characteristic. (2 marks)

> One characteristic is **high customer contact**: customers are physically present while staff prepare and serve their meals, so the cafe must manage wait times, service quality and ambience as part of operations. ⚠️ *Verify against actual 2022 paper.*

### Q. 2019 VCAA — Compare manufacturing operations (ChocYum) with service operations. (4 marks)

> Both manufacturing and service businesses transform inputs (labour, capital, information) into outputs through a managed operations system, and both rely on OM to deliver value efficiently and effectively.
>
> However, ChocYum produces **tangible** goods (chocolate) that can be stored and shipped, while a service business produces **intangible** outputs that are typically produced and consumed at the same time.
>
> ChocYum's operations are **capital intensive** (machinery, refrigeration, packaging lines), whereas service businesses are usually **labour intensive**, depending on staff skills and customer interaction.
>
> Both share the same purpose of transforming inputs into outputs, but manufacturing produces standardised, storable goods through capital-heavy processes, while services focus on customer-facing, intangible delivery driven by human interaction.

# LESSON 3 — Key elements of the operations system
*Read: Chapter 3.5 (second half)*

## 1. The three elements

The operations system has **three elements**:

### Element 1 — Inputs (resources used)
- **Raw materials** — physical materials in the finished product (steel, milk, cocoa).
- **Labour** — human effort, knowledge and skill.
- **Capital equipment** — buildings, machinery, vehicles, computers.
- **Information & data** — customer orders, market data, specs.
- **Energy & utilities** — electricity, gas, water.
- **Time** — production lead time.

### Element 2 — Transformation processes (activities)
- *Manufacturing:* assembly, mixing, cutting, packaging, quality testing.
- *Service:* analysis, advice, transaction processing, customer interaction.
- Supported by technology (CAD/CAM, robotics, AI), quality systems (QC/QA/TQM), and process design (lean, layout, scheduling).

### Element 3 — Outputs (what is delivered)
- The intended good or service (cars, cheese, banking transactions, cleaning).
- By-products and waste (to be minimised).
- Less tangible outcomes — customer satisfaction, brand reputation.

## 2. Applied to a manufacturing and service business

| Element | Manufacturing — Bega Cheese | Service — Commonwealth Bank |
|---|---|---|
| **Inputs** | Raw milk, cultures, packaging, factory machinery, refrigeration, energy, factory workers | Customer information, branch/online platforms, banking software, qualified staff, secure data infrastructure |
| **Transformation** | Pasteurising, culturing, ageing, cutting, packaging on automated lines | Processing loan applications, credit checks, fund transfers, financial advice |
| **Outputs** | Packaged cheese, whey by-product, brand reputation | Approved loans, completed transactions, financial advice, customer trust |

## 3. PRACTICE — Sample exam response

### Q. 2021 VCAA Q4(a) — WCTY Cleaning Services. Outline the three elements and apply one to WCTY. (4 marks)

> The three elements of the operations system are **inputs, transformation processes and outputs**.
>
> **Inputs** are the resources used in production, such as raw materials, labour, capital equipment, information and energy.
>
> **Transformation processes** are the activities that convert inputs into outputs — assembly, processing, service delivery or quality checks.
>
> **Outputs** are the finished goods or services delivered to customers, plus any by-products or waste.
>
> Applied to **WCTY Cleaning Services**, the **inputs** include cleaning chemicals, equipment (mops, vacuums, vehicles), trained staff, customer schedules and energy. The **transformation process** involves staff travelling to customer sites and cleaning the premises to a service brief. The **outputs** are clean, maintained homes and commercial premises, plus customer satisfaction and brand reputation.
>

# CONTEMPORARY BUSINESS EXAMPLES

## Australia Post (pg 219)

**Objective:** Increase productivity and reduce per-parcel cost.

**OM actions:**
- Automated parcel-sorting at major hubs (Chullora, Redbank).
- Barcode scanning, conveyors and robotics — 50,000+ parcels/hour.
- Real-time tracking for customers; route-optimisation software for delivery.

**Key terms applied:**
- *Operations management* — overseeing the activities that turn parcels and orders into delivered mail.
- *Productivity* — output (parcels) per input (labour-hour). Automation directly raises this.
- *Efficiency* — fewer staff-hours per parcel sorted.
- *Effectiveness* — on-time delivery and tracking meet the service-quality objective.

**Result:** Lower cost per parcel, faster delivery, higher productivity, better competitiveness.

## Ford manufacturing — video case study

⚠️ *The portal links to a Ford manufacturing video. Verify specifics against the video before submission.*

**a) Inputs Ford uses**
- Raw materials: steel, aluminium, rubber, glass, plastics, paint.
- Components: engines, electronics, seats, dashboards (from tier-1 suppliers).
- Labour: assembly workers, engineers, quality inspectors, robotics technicians.
- Capital equipment: robotic welding arms, paint booths, conveyors, CAD/CAM.
- Energy and information (specs, orders, schedules).

**b) Measures of output**
- Vehicles produced per shift / day / year.
- Production lead time per vehicle.
- Defect rate or first-pass yield.
- Energy or material used per vehicle.
- Vehicles shipped to dealerships.

**c) Two business objectives + OM strategies**
1. **Increase market share** → introduce **TQM and quality assurance** for consistent vehicle quality. Higher quality builds reputation and attracts customers.
2. **Improve productivity** → invest in **robotics and automation**, producing more vehicles per labour-hour and lowering unit cost.

**d) CSR considerations**
An OM should consider:
- **Environmental impact** — emissions, waste, energy use.
- **Worker safety** — heavy machinery, ergonomics, hazardous materials.
- **Ethical sourcing** — supplier labour and environmental standards.
- **End-of-life impact** — recyclability, take-back programs.
- **Community impact** — noise, employment, factory closures.

Ford has publicly committed to CSR initiatives such as **EV production**, **renewable energy at plants**, **water-recycling in paint shops**, and **supplier codes of conduct**. So yes, CSR is considered, though critics argue more is needed on the full lifecycle impact of vehicle manufacturing.
