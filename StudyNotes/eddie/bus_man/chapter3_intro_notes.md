# LESSON 1 — The relationship between operations management and business objectives
*Read: Chapter 3.1 and 3.2*

## 1. Define and explain Operations and Operations Management

**Operations** are the day-to-day activities a business undertakes to transform inputs (its resources) into outputs (the goods or services it sells to customers). Operations exist in every business — manufacturing, service, large or small — because every business needs some kind of process to produce what it sells.

**Operations management (OM)** is the planning, organising, leading and controlling of those activities so that the business uses its resources as efficiently and effectively as possible. The operations manager is responsible for designing, monitoring and continuously improving the operations system so that the business can deliver value to its customers and meet its objectives.

In short:
- **Operations** = the activities themselves (what happens on the factory floor or service counter).
- **Operations management** = the discipline of planning and overseeing those activities to make them work as well as they can.

## 2. Define and explain Effectiveness, Efficiency, Business Competitiveness and Productivity

| Concept | Definition | Plain-English |
|---|---|---|
| **Effectiveness** | The degree to which a business achieves its stated objectives. | "Doing the **right things**." |
| **Efficiency** | The degree to which a business uses its resources well to produce outputs. | "Doing things **right**." |
| **Productivity** | The ratio of outputs produced to inputs used (e.g. units per labour-hour, parcels per shift). | More output for the same inputs = higher productivity. |
| **Business competitiveness** | The ability of a business to outperform competitors by offering better price, quality, speed, service or innovation. | Why customers choose you over the competition. |

**How they connect:** Operations management is the lever that lifts all four. Better processes raise productivity, which lowers cost per unit (efficiency), which lets the business sell at a more attractive price (competitiveness), while still meeting customer expectations (effectiveness).

## 3. Explain the relationship between Operations Management and Business Objectives

Every business sets **business objectives** — measurable goals like increasing profit, growing market share, lifting productivity, satisfying customers or meeting CSR commitments. Operations is where the business actually makes the things (or delivers the services) that earn it revenue, and where most of its costs are incurred. So OM decisions have a direct, measurable impact on whether those objectives are achieved.

| Business objective | How operations management contributes | Example OM strategy |
|---|---|---|
| Increase profit | Reduces input cost and waste, raising margin per unit sold. | Just-in-time inventory + lean management. |
| Increase market share | Better quality and faster delivery attract customers from competitors. | TQM + automated production lines. |
| Improve productivity | Better technology and processes produce more output from the same inputs. | Robotics, AI scheduling, Kaizen. |
| Fulfil CSR commitments | Operations is where most environmental and social impact happens. | Ethical sourcing, sustainable packaging, fair trade. |
| Increase shareholder value | Higher efficiency and quality lift profit, dividends and share price. | Lean management combined with quality assurance. |

## 4. APPLY — Australia Post case study (Jacaranda pg. 219)

**Business objective Australia Post is pursuing:** *Increase productivity and reduce parcel-handling cost per unit across its national logistics network.*

**How operations management is used to achieve it:**
Australia Post invested heavily in **automated parcel-sorting facilities** at sites such as Chullora (Sydney) and Redbank (QLD). These centres use barcode scanners, conveyor systems and robotic arms to sort over 50,000 parcels per hour with minimal human intervention. They also use route-optimisation software for delivery trucks and real-time parcel tracking technology for customers.

**Outcome:**
- **Productivity** rose because the same workforce processes far more parcels per shift.
- **Efficiency** improved because cost per parcel fell as labour and floor space were better utilised.
- **Effectiveness** improved because customers received parcels faster and could track them in real time, meeting the service-quality objective.
- **Competitiveness** was preserved against private couriers like StarTrack and Amazon Logistics.

The decisions made by Australia Post's operations managers (what to automate, when to invest, how to redesign sorting flow) translated the strategic objective directly into measurable operational change.

## 5. Analyse the importance of the relationship

The relationship between OM and business objectives is **critical** for several reasons:

1. **Operations is where strategy meets reality.** A business can plan brilliantly at the strategic level, but unless its operations actually deliver, the plan fails. OM is the bridge between objectives and outcomes.
2. **Operations consumes most of the business's resources.** Inputs (labour, materials, capital) account for the majority of cost in most businesses, so any inefficiency in operations directly erodes profit.
3. **Operations creates the customer experience.** The product (good or service) the customer receives is the result of operations. Quality problems, delays, or mistakes in operations directly damage market share and brand reputation.
4. **Poor OM undermines even good objectives.** A retailer can set an ambitious revenue target, but if its supply chain can't keep shelves stocked, the target is unreachable. Conversely, world-class operations (like Toyota's lean system) can become a source of competitive advantage in their own right.

**Cause-and-effect chain to memorise:**
> *OM strategy → operational change → improved efficiency/quality/productivity → contributes to business objective → improved business performance.*

## 6. PRACTICE — Sample exam responses

### Q. Describe the relationship between operations management and business objectives. (4 marks)

> Operations management is the planning, organising, leading and controlling of the activities that transform inputs into the goods and services a business sells. Business objectives are the specific, measurable goals a business sets for itself, such as making a profit, growing market share or fulfilling its CSR commitments.
>
> Because operations is where most of a business's costs are incurred and where its products are actually created, decisions made by the operations manager directly affect whether the business achieves these objectives. For example, eliminating waste through lean management lowers production cost and contributes to the profit objective, while investing in quality assurance improves customer satisfaction and contributes to market share.
>
> The relationship is therefore critical — effective operations management converts a business's strategic objectives into the day-to-day actions that deliver value to customers, while inefficient operations can undermine even a well-designed business strategy.

# LESSON 2 — Characteristics of operations management within manufacturing and service businesses
*Read: Chapter 3.4 (first half of "Key elements" portal page)*

## 1. Define and explain a manufacturing and a service business

**Manufacturing business:** A business that transforms raw materials and components into **tangible** finished goods for sale. Production usually happens in a factory, customers are not present during production, and goods can be made in advance and stored. Manufacturing operations are typically **capital intensive** — they rely heavily on machinery, equipment and technology.
- *Australian examples:* Toyota Australia, Bega Cheese, BlueScope Steel, Boeing Australia, ChocYum Pty Ltd.

**Service business:** A business that provides **intangible** activities, expertise or experiences directly to customers. The service is usually produced and consumed at the same time, the customer is often present and involved, and the service cannot be stored. Service operations are typically **labour intensive** — they rely heavily on staff knowledge and skill.
- *Australian examples:* Commonwealth Bank, Qantas, Medibank, KEJ's Cafe, WCTY Cleaning Services.

## 2. Explain, distinguish and compare features between a manufacturing and service business

| Characteristic | Manufacturing business | Service business |
|---|---|---|
| **Output** | Tangible — can be seen, touched, stored | Intangible — an activity, experience or expertise |
| **Customer contact** | Low — customer rarely present during production | High — customer often present and involved |
| **Production timing** | Goods produced in advance and stored until sold | Service is produced and consumed at the same time |
| **Inventory** | Significant raw material and finished-goods inventory | Minimal — no finished service to stock |
| **Capital vs labour intensity** | Often capital intensive (machinery, technology) | Often labour intensive (staff skills, time) |
| **Quality measurement** | Easier — measurable specs (size, weight, defects/1000) | Harder — relies on customer perception and feedback |
| **Customisation** | Standardised for mass production | Often customised to each customer |
| **Location** | Centralised factories — customers don't need to be near | Decentralised — service must be near the customer |
| **Australian example** | Toyota, Bega Cheese, ChocYum | CBA, Medibank, WCTY Cleaning, KEJ's Cafe |

**Similarities** (often missed in compare answers):
- Both transform inputs into outputs through a managed operations system.
- Both require operations management to balance efficiency and effectiveness.
- Both pursue the same overarching business objectives (profit, productivity, market share, CSR).
- Both can apply OM strategies such as quality management, technology, and lean principles.
- Both must consider stakeholder needs and CSR.

## 3. Sample short-answer responses

### Q. Outline two characteristics of a manufacturing business.

> 1. **Tangible outputs:** A manufacturing business produces physical goods that can be seen, touched and stored before being sold (e.g. ChocYum produces chocolate bars).
> 2. **Capital intensive operations:** Manufacturing typically relies heavily on machinery, equipment and technology rather than labour, with employees often operating or supervising automated production lines.

### Q. Explain the characteristics of a service business.

> A service business produces **intangible outputs** — activities, expertise or experiences (such as banking or cleaning) rather than physical products. Because the service cannot be stored, it is **produced and consumed at the same time**, which means **customer contact is typically high** as the customer is present during delivery. Service operations are usually **labour intensive**, depending heavily on staff skills, knowledge and interaction quality. Quality is harder to measure objectively, so service businesses rely on customer feedback and satisfaction surveys to gauge effectiveness. Service businesses also tend to be **decentralised**, with delivery happening at or near the customer's location rather than at a central factory.

### Q. Compare a manufacturing and service business (using 3 characteristics).

> A manufacturing business produces **tangible** goods that can be stored, whereas a service business produces **intangible** outputs that cannot be stored and are usually consumed at the point of delivery. **Both** types of business transform inputs into outputs through a managed operations system to deliver value to customers.
>
> In terms of **customer contact**, a manufacturing business typically has low customer contact — customers rarely enter the production area — whereas a service business has high customer contact, with customers often present during delivery (for example, KEJ's Cafe customers are present while their meals are prepared and served).
>
> In terms of **resource intensity**, manufacturing operations are usually **capital intensive**, relying on machinery, equipment and automation, whereas service operations are usually **labour intensive**, relying on staff skills and effort to deliver the service.

## 4. PRACTICE — Sample exam responses

### Q. 2022 VCAA Section A Q3(a) — KEJ's Cafe service business characteristic. (2 marks)

> One characteristic of the operations system of a service business such as KEJ's Cafe is **high customer contact**: customers are physically present in the cafe while staff prepare and serve their meals, which means the cafe must manage the customer experience as part of the operations process (for example, by managing wait times, table service quality and ambience). ⚠️ *Verify against actual 2022 examination report.*

### Q. 2019 VCAA — Compare manufacturing operations (ChocYum) with service operations. (4 marks)

> Both manufacturing and service businesses transform inputs (such as labour, capital and information) into outputs through a managed operations system, and both rely on operations management to deliver value efficiently and effectively to customers.
>
> However, a manufacturing business such as ChocYum Pty Ltd produces **tangible** goods (chocolate products) that can be stored and shipped, whereas a service business produces **intangible** outputs (such as legal advice or cleaning services) that are typically produced and consumed at the same time and cannot be stored.
>
> ChocYum's manufacturing operations are highly **capital intensive**, relying on production machinery, refrigeration and packaging lines, while service businesses are typically **labour intensive**, depending on staff skills, expertise and customer interaction to deliver the service.
>
> Overall, while both share the fundamental purpose of transforming inputs into outputs, manufacturing focuses on producing standardised, storable goods through capital-heavy processes, whereas service operations focus on customer-facing, intangible delivery that depends heavily on human interaction.

# LESSON 3 — Key elements of the operations system
*Read: Chapter 3.5 (second half of "Key elements" portal page)*

## 1. Describe each of the elements of the operations system

The operations system is the framework that turns resources into the goods or services a business sells. It has **three key elements**:

### Element 1 — Inputs
The **resources** used in the production process. They include:
- **Raw materials** — the physical materials that become part of the finished product (steel, milk, cocoa, paper).
- **Labour** — the human effort, knowledge and skill of employees.
- **Capital equipment** — buildings, machinery, vehicles, computers used in production.
- **Information & data** — customer orders, market data, technical specifications.
- **Energy & utilities** — electricity, gas, water.
- **Time** — the lead time available for production.

### Element 2 — Transformation processes
The **activities** that convert inputs into outputs. These vary depending on the business:
- *Manufacturing:* assembly, mixing, cutting, packaging, quality testing.
- *Service:* analysis, advice, consultation, transaction processing, customer interaction.
- Transformation is supported by technology (CAD/CAM, robotics, AI), quality systems (QC/QA/TQM), and process design (lean management, layout, scheduling).

### Element 3 — Outputs
The **finished products or services** the business delivers to customers, plus any by-products. Outputs include:
- The intended good or service (cars, cheese, banking transactions, cleaning).
- By-products and waste (which the business should minimise).
- Less tangible outcomes such as customer satisfaction and brand reputation.

## 2. Apply the three elements to a manufacturing AND a service business

| Element | Manufacturing example — Bega Cheese | Service example — Commonwealth Bank |
|---|---|---|
| **Inputs** | Raw milk, cultures, packaging, factory machinery, refrigeration, energy, factory workers | Customer information, branch/online platform, banking software, qualified staff time, secure data infrastructure |
| **Transformation** | Pasteurising, culturing, ageing, cutting, packaging cheese on automated production lines | Processing loan applications, running credit checks, transferring funds, providing financial advice |
| **Outputs** | Packaged cheese on supermarket shelves; whey by-product; brand reputation | Approved loans, completed transactions, financial advice delivered, satisfied customers, customer trust |

## 3. PRACTICE — Sample exam responses

### Q. 2021 VCAA Q4(a) — WCTY Cleaning Services. Outline the three key elements of the operations system. Apply one to WCTY Cleaning Services. (4 marks)

> The three key elements of the operations system are **inputs, transformation processes and outputs**.
>
> **Inputs** are the resources used in the production process, such as raw materials, labour, capital equipment, information and energy.
>
> **Transformation processes** are the activities that convert inputs into outputs — for example, assembly, processing, service delivery or quality checks.
>
> **Outputs** are the finished goods or services produced, along with any by-products or waste, that are delivered to customers.
>
> Applied to **WCTY Cleaning Services**, the **inputs** include cleaning chemicals and equipment (mops, vacuums, vehicles), trained cleaning staff, customer schedules and energy. The **transformation process** involves staff travelling to customer sites and physically cleaning the premises according to a service brief. The **outputs** are the clean, maintained homes and commercial premises delivered to WCTY's customers, plus customer satisfaction and brand reputation.
>

# CONTEMPORARY BUSINESS EXAMPLES

## Australia Post (pg 219)

**Business objective:** Increase productivity and reduce per-parcel cost across the national logistics network.

**Operations management actions:**
- Invested in **automated parcel-sorting** at major distribution hubs (Chullora, Redbank).
- Deployed **barcode scanning, conveyor systems and robotics** to handle 50,000+ parcels/hour.
- Implemented **real-time tracking technology** for customers and **route-optimisation software** for delivery vehicles.

**Key terms:**
- *Operations management* — planning, organising, leading and controlling the activities that transform parcels and customer orders into delivered mail.
- *Productivity* — output (parcels delivered) per input (labour-hour or shift). Australia Post's automation directly raises this ratio.
- *Efficiency* — Australia Post uses fewer staff-hours per parcel sorted than under manual processing.
- *Effectiveness* — customers receive parcels on time and can track them, meeting the service-quality objective.

**Result:** Lower cost per parcel, faster delivery, higher productivity, improved competitiveness against private couriers — directly contributing to the productivity, profit and customer-service objectives.

## Ford manufacturing — video case study questions

⚠️ *The portal links to a Ford manufacturing video. The model responses below are typical answers based on Ford's known manufacturing operations; verify specific examples against the video before submission.*

**a) List some inputs Ford uses in its operations.**
- Raw materials: steel, aluminium, rubber (tyres), glass, plastics, paint.
- Components: engines, electronic systems, seats, dashboards (often supplied by tier-1 suppliers).
- Labour: assembly-line workers, engineers, quality inspectors, robotics technicians.
- Capital equipment: robotic welding arms, painting booths, conveyor systems, CAD/CAM stations.
- Energy: electricity for the plant and machinery.
- Information: vehicle specifications, customer orders, production schedules.

**b) List measures of output mentioned in the video.**
- Number of vehicles produced per shift / per day / per year.
- Production lead time per vehicle.
- Defect rate or first-pass yield (vehicles passing quality inspection without rework).
- Energy or material used per vehicle (efficiency).
- Number of vehicles shipped to dealerships.

**c) Suggest two business objectives Ford might have, and how OM strategies could meet them.**
1. **Increase market share.** OM strategy: introduce **TQM and quality assurance** to ensure every vehicle meets a high consistent standard. Higher quality builds brand reputation, drives positive reviews, and attracts customers from competitors.
2. **Improve productivity.** OM strategy: invest in **robotics and automation** on the assembly line, so each shift produces more vehicles per labour-hour. This reduces unit cost and frees up labour for higher-skilled roles.

**d) What CSR considerations should an operations manager take into account, and is this evident at Ford?**
An operations manager should consider:
- **Environmental impact:** emissions, waste from production, energy consumption.
- **Worker safety:** safe operation of heavy machinery, ergonomics, hazardous-material handling.
- **Ethical sourcing:** ensuring suppliers (e.g. of steel, leather, electronics) meet labour and environmental standards.
- **End-of-life impact:** recyclability of vehicles, take-back programs.
- **Community impact:** noise, local employment, factory closures.

Ford has publicly committed to a number of CSR initiatives in operations — for example, **electric vehicle (EV) production** to reduce tailpipe emissions, **renewable energy use at plants**, **water-recycling programs in paint shops**, and **supplier codes of conduct** covering labour and environmental standards. So yes, CSR is considered, although critics argue Ford (like other major auto-makers) has work still to do on the full lifecycle environmental impact of vehicle manufacturing.
