🎯 Operations management & business objectives  ★ HOT TOPICLinking OM strategies to business objectives is a foundation skill for every Chapter 3 question ▼

Operations are the day-to-day activities a business undertakes to transform inputs (resources) into outputs (goods or services) for its customers.

Operations management is the planning, organising, leading and controlling of those activities so the business uses its resources as efficiently and effectively as possible to achieve its overall business objectives.

Because operations is where most of a business's costs are incurred and where its product is actually created, OM decisions have a direct, measurable impact on whether the business achieves objectives such as profit, market share, productivity and CSR.

Four key OM concepts — what each one measures

ConceptDefinitionHow OM influences it
EffectivenessThe degree to which a business achieves its stated objectives — "doing the right things".OM ensures the right products are produced to the right standard so customers and objectives are satisfied.
EfficiencyThe degree to which a business uses its resources well to produce outputs — "doing things right".OM eliminates waste, reduces cost per unit and shortens production time.
ProductivityThe ratio of outputs produced to inputs used (e.g. units per labour-hour). Higher productivity = more output for the same inputs.OM raises productivity through better technology, training, processes and factory layout.
Business competitivenessThe ability of a business to sell products at a price, quality and speed that competitors cannot easily match.OM builds competitiveness through cost leadership, quality management, innovation and reliability.

How OM strategies achieve common business objectives

Business objectiveHow operations management contributesExample OM strategy
Increase profitReducing input costs and waste increases the margin earned on each unit sold.JIT inventory + lean management cut holding costs and overproduction.
Increase market shareHigher-quality, faster-produced products attract more customers from competitors.TQM and automated production lines improve quality consistency and output speed.
Improve productivityBetter processes and technology produce more output per unit of input.Robotics, AI scheduling and Kaizen incremental improvements.
Fulfil CSR objectivesOperations is where most environmental and social impact occurs — changes here have the largest CSR effect.Ethical sourcing, fair-trade procurement, sustainable packaging.
Increase shareholder valueHigher efficiency and quality both raise profit, dividends and share price.Lean management combined with quality assurance.

Mini case study — Australia Post (Jacaranda pg. 219)

Business objective: Increase productivity and reduce parcel-handling cost per unit across the national logistics network.
OM strategies used: Automated parcel-sorting facilities (e.g. Sydney's Chullora and Redbank centres) using barcode scanners, conveyor systems and robotics — sorting over 50,000 parcels per hour with minimal human input. Investment in real-time tracking technology and route-optimisation software.
Result: Higher throughput, lower cost per parcel, faster delivery times and improved competitiveness against private couriers — directly contributing to the productivity, profit and customer-service objectives.

💡 Always link operations strategies BACK to a stated business objective. "The strategy increases productivity, which contributes to the objective of profit" is the structure that earns full marks.

🏭 Manufacturing vs service businesses  ★ HOT TOPICCompare/distinguish questions on M vs S businesses appear regularly (e.g. 2019, 2022) ▼

Manufacturing business: Produces tangible (physical) products by transforming raw materials and components into finished goods. Examples: Toyota, Bega Cheese, Boeing, BlueScope Steel, ChocYum Pty Ltd.

Service business: Provides intangible products (activities, expertise or experiences) that are usually produced and consumed at the same time, with the customer often present. Examples: Commonwealth Bank, Qantas, Medibank, KEJ's Cafe, WCTY Cleaning Services.

Operations characteristics — manufacturing vs service

CharacteristicManufacturing businessService business
OutputTangible — can be seen, touched and storedIntangible — an activity, experience or expertise
Customer contactLow — customer rarely present during productionHigh — customer often present and directly involved
Production timingGoods can be produced in advance and stored until soldService is produced and consumed at the same time — cannot be stored
InventorySignificant raw-material and finished-goods inventory heldMinimal inventory — no finished service to stockpile
Capital vs labour intensityOften capital intensive — heavy use of machinery and technologyOften labour intensive — reliant on staff knowledge and skill
Quality measurementEasier — against measurable specifications (size, weight, defects per 1000)Harder — relies on customer perception, feedback and satisfaction surveys
CustomisationOften standardised for mass production; customisation increases unit costOften customised to each customer's individual situation
Location of operationsCentralised factories — customers do not need to be near productionDecentralised — service must usually be delivered close to the customer
Australian exampleToyota Altona, Bega Cheese Strathmerton, ChocYum (chocolate)Commonwealth Bank, Medibank, WCTY Cleaning, KEJ's Cafe

Similarities (often missed in "compare" answers)

  • Both transform inputs into outputs through a managed operations system.
  • Both require operations management to balance efficiency and effectiveness.
  • Both pursue the same overarching business objectives (profit, productivity, market share, CSR).
  • Both can apply OM strategies such as quality management, technology, automation and lean principles.
  • Both must consider stakeholder needs and CSR in their operations decisions.

💡 A "compare" question requires BOTH similarities AND differences. A "distinguish" question requires differences only. Use connectives like "whereas" and "in contrast" to make differences crystal clear.

📊 Quality management strategies — comparison  ★ HOT TOPICQC vs QA vs TQM is a very common exam comparison question ▼
Feature Quality Control (QC) Quality Assurance (QA) Total Quality Management (TQM)
DefinitionInspecting finished products to identify and reject defectsBuilding systems and processes that prevent defects from occurringA whole-business philosophy where every employee is responsible for quality at every stage
FocusOutput (end product)Process (how work is done)Culture (everyone, all the time)
When appliedAfter productionDuring production (process monitoring)Continuously, at every step
Who is responsible?Quality inspectors / QC teamProcess owners and QA systemsEvery employee at every level
Cost of defectsHigh — wasteful products already madeMedium — fewer defects reach end stageLow — defects prevented before they occur
Australian exampleToyota randomly tests finished vehicles off the production line before dispatchBega Cheese uses ISO 9001 certified processes with temperature monitoring at every production stageToyota's production system involves every worker halting the line if they spot a defect

Quality Control — advantages & disadvantages

+ Advantages

  • Simple to implement as it only requires inspection at the end of the production process, requiring no major system overhaul.
  • Prevents defective products from reaching customers, protecting brand reputation and reducing customer complaints.
  • Provides clear, measurable data on defect rates that can inform future process improvements.

− Disadvantages

  • Wasteful because defective products have already consumed resources (labour, materials, energy) before being rejected.
  • Does not address the root cause of defects — the same problems will continue to occur in future batches.
  • Reactive rather than preventive — it finds problems after they happen rather than stopping them from occurring.

Quality Assurance — advantages & disadvantages

+ Advantages

  • Reduces defect rates by monitoring and controlling the process, meaning fewer products need to be rejected or reworked.
  • ISO certification provides a competitive advantage by demonstrating to customers and partners that quality systems meet international standards.
  • Creates documented, consistent processes that are easier to train new staff on and audit for compliance.

− Disadvantages

  • Significant upfront cost to design, document and certify quality systems, particularly for ISO accreditation.
  • Can create excessive paperwork and bureaucracy that slows down operations if not well-managed.
  • Systems may become outdated if not regularly reviewed and updated to reflect process changes.

Total Quality Management — advantages & disadvantages

+ Advantages

  • Embeds a culture of continuous improvement across the entire business, reducing defects and waste over time.
  • Increases employee engagement as all staff are empowered to identify problems and suggest improvements.
  • Leads to long-term cost reductions as quality problems are caught and eliminated at their source.

− Disadvantages

  • Requires a major cultural shift that can take years to implement and may face significant resistance from staff and managers.
  • Demanding training requirements across all levels of the business are costly and time-consuming.
  • Results are not immediate — the business may not see measurable improvement for a considerable time after adoption.

💡 The key difference: QC finds defects, QA prevents defects in the process, TQM makes quality everyone's job all the time. Exams often ask you to compare TWO of these — always contrast the focus (output vs process vs culture).

▶ Technology & automation strategies  ★ HOT TOPICAutomation and its impact on employees is frequently examined ▼
TechnologyWhat it involvesExample
RoboticsAutomated machines that perform physical tasks previously done by humans, such as assembly, welding or packagingToyota uses robotic arms on its Australian assembly lines to weld car body panels with precision and speed
Artificial intelligence (AI)Computer systems that simulate human decision-making and learning, used for demand forecasting, scheduling and quality detectionWoolworths uses AI to predict stock demand by store location, reducing both overstocking and out-of-stock incidents
Computer-aided design (CAD)Software that allows designers and engineers to create, modify and test product designs digitally before physical productionBoeing uses CAD to design and simulate aircraft components, identifying structural weaknesses before a single part is manufactured
Computer-aided manufacturing (CAM)Software that controls and automates machinery on the production floor, translating designs into precise machine instructionsA precision engineering firm uses CAM to automatically program CNC milling machines to cut metal parts to exact tolerances
Automated production linesA sequence of machines and conveyor systems that move products through each stage of production with minimal human interventionCoca-Cola's Australian bottling plant uses a fully automated line that fills, caps and labels 1,200 bottles per minute

Technology & automation — advantages & disadvantages

+ Advantages

  • Increases productivity and output speed as machines can operate continuously without breaks, fatigue or shift limitations.
  • Improves consistency and reduces human error, resulting in higher and more uniform product quality.
  • Reduces long-term labour costs as fewer employees are needed to perform routine or repetitive tasks.
  • Can improve workplace safety by removing employees from dangerous or physically demanding tasks.

− Disadvantages

  • High initial capital cost to purchase, install and integrate technology, which may be prohibitive for smaller businesses.
  • Can lead to significant job losses, creating negative social impact, community backlash and potential industrial action.
  • Requires ongoing maintenance and technical expertise — breakdowns can halt entire production lines and are costly to repair.
  • Technology can become obsolete, requiring further investment to upgrade or replace systems within a few years.

💡 When discussing automation, always address the impact on employees — exams commonly ask you to weigh productivity gains against job displacement. Link to CSR where relevant.

📊 Materials & inventory management strategies ▼
StrategyWhat it involves+ Advantage− Disadvantage
Just-in-time (JIT) Ordering and receiving materials only when they are needed in production, minimising inventory held on-site Reduces storage costs and waste from holding excess stock; frees up capital for other uses Any supply chain disruption (e.g. delayed delivery) can immediately halt production
Materials requirement planning (MRP) A computer-based system that calculates exactly what materials are needed, in what quantities, and when, based on production schedules Reduces over-ordering and under-ordering, improving cost efficiency and production scheduling Relies on accurate data input — errors in the system lead to incorrect materials orders
Master production schedule (MPS) A detailed plan specifying what products will be produced, in what quantities and by when, across a set production period Provides clear guidance for procurement, staffing and equipment scheduling Rigid schedule can be difficult to adjust when unexpected demand changes or supply issues arise
Forecasting Using historical data and market analysis to predict future demand and plan inventory and production accordingly Reduces the risk of stockouts (running out of materials) or overstocking, improving cash flow Forecasts can be inaccurate, particularly in volatile or rapidly changing markets

💡 JIT is most commonly examined — know both its efficiency benefits AND its vulnerability to supply disruptions (as seen in COVID-19 when global supply chains broke down).

▶ Lean management & waste minimisation strategies ▼
StrategyWhat it involvesExample
Lean managementAn approach to operations that systematically identifies and eliminates all forms of waste in order to improve efficiency and deliver more value to customersToyota's production system (TPS) is the global benchmark for lean manufacturing, eliminating overproduction, waiting time and unnecessary movement
Kaizen (continuous improvement)A philosophy of ongoing, incremental improvements to processes, products and systems, driven by all employees at every levelA Bega Cheese production team meets weekly to identify small inefficiencies in the packaging line and implement one improvement per fortnight
The seven wastes (TIMWOOD)Transport, Inventory, Motion, Waiting, Overproduction, Over-processing, Defects — the seven categories of waste lean management seeks to eliminateA food manufacturer reduces "waiting" waste by synchronising the speed of its mixing and packaging machines so neither ever sits idle
Pull systemProduction is triggered only by actual customer demand — nothing is made until an order is receivedA custom furniture maker only begins building a dining table once a customer has placed and paid for an order
Push systemProduction is based on demand forecasts — goods are made in advance and pushed through the supply chain to retailersCoca-Cola produces large volumes of soft drink based on seasonal demand forecasts and pushes stock to supermarkets before summer

Pull vs Push systems — comparison

FeaturePull systemPush system
Triggered byActual customer orders (demand)Demand forecasts (predicted demand)
Inventory levelsLow — produced to orderHigh — produced in advance
Waste riskLow — nothing made without an orderHigher — overproduction if forecast is wrong

+ Lean management advantages

  • Reduces costs by eliminating non-value-adding activities and minimising resource waste across the production process.
  • Improves product quality by identifying and removing defect-causing steps before they affect the final output.
  • Increases employee engagement as staff at all levels are encouraged to identify problems and contribute improvements.

− Lean management disadvantages

  • Significant time and cultural change required — lean is a long-term commitment, not a quick fix for operational problems.
  • Low inventory buffers mean the business is highly vulnerable to supply chain disruptions or sudden demand spikes.
  • Requires thorough and ongoing training for all staff, which adds to implementation costs and time.

💡 Kaizen is linked to TQM (Chapter 3) and also to Kotter's change model (Chapter 5) — knowing these connections will add depth to extended responses.

▶ Corporate social responsibility (CSR) in operations  ★ HOT TOPICCSR evaluate questions appear almost every year ▼
CSR strategyWhat it involvesExample
Environmentally sustainable practicesReducing environmental impact through waste reduction, renewable energy, recycling and emissions minimisationWoolworths committed to sourcing 100% renewable electricity for all stores and DCs by 2025, reducing its carbon footprint significantly
Ethical sourcingSelecting suppliers who meet ethical standards for labour conditions, environmental practices and human rightsQantas requires all catering suppliers to comply with its supplier code of conduct covering fair wages and safe working conditions
Fair trade procurementPurchasing materials or goods from suppliers certified under fair trade standards, ensuring fair pay and conditions for producers in developing countriesA Melbourne cafe sources only Fairtrade-certified coffee beans, guaranteeing farmers in Ethiopia a minimum price above market rates
Waste reduction programsActively minimising production waste through redesign, recycling initiatives and circular economy practicesBega Cheese recycles all whey by-product into animal feed and uses biogas from waste to power its Strathmerton factory
Supply chain transparencyPublicly reporting on the sourcing practices and conditions throughout the entire supply chainCotton On publishes an annual ethical supply chain report naming all Tier 1 factories and auditing results

CSR in operations — advantages & disadvantages

+ Advantages

  • Enhances brand reputation and customer loyalty, particularly among consumers who consider ethics when making purchasing decisions.
  • Attracts and retains employees who want to work for a business whose values align with their own, reducing recruitment costs.
  • Can reduce operational costs in the long run — energy efficiency, waste reduction and sustainable sourcing often lower input costs over time.
  • Reduces the risk of reputational damage, boycotts or legal action arising from unethical supply chain practices.

− Disadvantages

  • Higher upfront costs for ethical sourcing, certification and sustainable technology compared to conventional alternatives.
  • May reduce short-term profitability, creating tension with shareholders who prioritise immediate financial returns.
  • Difficult to verify ethical standards throughout a complex global supply chain, risking greenwashing accusations if claims cannot be substantiated.
  • Competitors who do not adopt CSR practices may undercut on price, putting pressure on profit margins.

Tension between profit and CSR

The core tension: CSR activities often increase short-term costs, which can reduce profit. However, businesses that ignore CSR risk losing customers, attracting regulatory scrutiny and suffering reputational damage that is far more costly in the long run.

Exam framing: When asked to evaluate CSR, always acknowledge BOTH the financial cost to the business AND the long-term reputational and social benefit. Conclude with a justified recommendation based on the scenario.

💡 A strong evaluate response connects CSR to stakeholder theory — CSR satisfies the expectations of customers, employees, communities and regulators, even when it reduces short-term profit.

📝 Model answer — describe OM & business objectives (4 marks) ▼

Sample question: Describe the relationship between operations management and business objectives. (4 marks)

Step 1 — Define operations management: Operations management is the planning, organising, leading and controlling of the activities that transform inputs (such as raw materials, labour and capital) into the goods and services a business sells.
Step 2 — Define business objectives: Business objectives are the specific, measurable goals a business sets for itself, such as making a profit, increasing market share, improving productivity or fulfilling its corporate social responsibility commitments.
Step 3 — Explain the link: Because operations is where most of a business's costs are incurred and where its products are actually created, decisions made by the operations manager directly affect whether the business achieves these objectives. For example, eliminating waste through lean management lowers production cost and contributes to the profit objective, while investing in quality assurance improves customer satisfaction and contributes to market share.
Step 4 — Significance: The relationship is therefore critical — effective operations management converts the business's strategic objectives into day-to-day actions that deliver value to customers, while inefficient operations can undermine even a well-designed business strategy.

💡 A 4-mark "describe the relationship" answer needs: define OM, define business objectives, explain HOW OM achieves objectives (with example), and state the significance.

📝 Model answer — compare manufacturing & service operations (4 marks) ▼

Sample question (2019 VCAA-style): Compare the characteristics of operations management within a manufacturing business (such as ChocYum Pty Ltd) with those of a service business. (4 marks)

Step 1 — Similarity: Both manufacturing and service businesses transform inputs (such as labour, capital and information) into outputs through a managed operations system, and both rely on operations management to deliver value efficiently and effectively to customers.
Step 2 — Difference 1 (output type): A manufacturing business such as ChocYum Pty Ltd produces tangible goods (chocolate products) that can be stored and shipped, whereas a service business produces intangible outputs (such as legal advice or cleaning services) that are typically produced and consumed at the same time and cannot be stored.
Step 3 — Difference 2 (capital vs labour intensity): ChocYum's manufacturing operations are highly capital intensive, relying on production machinery, refrigeration and packaging lines, while service businesses are typically labour intensive, depending on staff skills, expertise and customer interaction to deliver the service.
Step 4 — Overall: While both share the fundamental purpose of transforming inputs into outputs, manufacturing focuses on producing standardised, storable goods through capital-heavy processes, whereas service operations focus on customer-facing, intangible delivery that depends heavily on human interaction.

💡 A "compare" answer needs at least one similarity AND clear differences. Always pair the manufacturing and service examples side-by-side rather than describing them in two separate paragraphs.

📝 Model answer — evaluate an operations strategy (6 marks) ▼

Sample question: Evaluate the use of Total Quality Management (TQM) as an operations strategy for Acme Manufacturing. (6 marks)

Step 1 — Define the strategy: Total Quality Management is an operations strategy in which every employee at every level of the business takes responsibility for maintaining and improving quality at each stage of the production process, rather than relying on end-of-line inspection.
Step 2 — Advantage 1 + scenario link: A key strength of TQM for Acme Manufacturing is that it embeds a culture of continuous improvement across the workforce. When production line workers are empowered to halt the line and flag defects, quality problems are caught before defective products accumulate, reducing rework costs and material waste.
Step 3 — Advantage 2 + scenario link: TQM also increases employee engagement at Acme Manufacturing, as staff feel trusted and valued when their input on quality improvements is actively sought. This can reduce turnover and improve morale, which further supports productivity.
Step 4 — Disadvantage 1 + scenario link: However, implementing TQM at Acme Manufacturing would require a significant cultural change. If existing employees are accustomed to a top-down quality inspection approach, shifting to shared responsibility may face resistance and require extensive retraining, which is both costly and time-consuming.
Step 5 — Disadvantage 2 + scenario link: Additionally, TQM does not produce immediate results. Acme Manufacturing may need to invest in the program for 12–24 months before measurable improvements in defect rates and efficiency are visible, creating short-term cost pressure without immediate financial benefit.
Step 6 — Justified conclusion: Overall, TQM is an appropriate long-term strategy for Acme Manufacturing if the business is committed to sustained quality improvement and has the financial capacity to absorb the implementation costs. If Acme needs immediate quality improvements, quality control or quality assurance may be more practical short-term options while TQM is progressively introduced.

💡 A 6-mark evaluate response needs at least 2 advantages, 2 disadvantages AND a justified conclusion. Dropping any one of these components will cost you marks.

📝 Model answer — compare two quality strategies (4 marks) ▼

Sample question: Compare quality control and quality assurance as operations strategies. (4 marks)

Step 1 — Similarity: Both quality control and quality assurance aim to ensure that the products or services a business delivers meet the required standard, thereby protecting customer satisfaction and the business's reputation.
Step 2 — Difference 1 (timing): However, quality control is applied after production is complete, with inspectors checking finished products for defects. Quality assurance, by contrast, is applied during the production process itself, with systems designed to prevent defects from occurring in the first place.
Step 3 — Difference 2 (cost/waste): Quality control is more wasteful because defective products have already consumed resources before being rejected. Quality assurance reduces this waste by catching process failures early, making it more cost-efficient over time despite its higher setup cost.
Step 4 — Overall distinction: In summary, quality control is reactive (finding defects) while quality assurance is proactive (preventing defects), making quality assurance the more strategically sound long-term approach for a business seeking to minimise waste and improve efficiency.

💡 Compare questions must include BOTH a similarity AND differences. Starting with a similarity before the differences is the safest structure.
